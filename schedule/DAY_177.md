## Block 1 — A signal's lifecycle
Every alpha signal goes through stages, and each is a craft:
1. **Raw idea / data** → a hypothesis with an economic story (Day 149).
2. **Feature construction** → turn data into a clean, point-in-time, lookahead-safe number.
3. **Cleaning** → winsorize outliers, handle missing data, normalize cross-sectionally (z-score/rank).
4. **Neutralization** → strip out factor/sector exposure (Day 176).
5. **Evaluation** → IC, IC decay, turnover, capacity, robustness across sub-periods.
6. **Combination** → blend with other signals (today).
7. **Decay & retirement** → monitor; alpha decays (Day 149), and dead signals must be cut.

*Why it matters:* most of the edge is in *signal craftsmanship*, not exotic models. A clean, neutralized, well-combined set of simple signals beats one fancy raw signal.

---

## Block 2 — Signal decay: how fast does the edge fade?
A signal's predictive power decays with horizon — measure the **IC at lags 1, 2, 5, 10 days**. The decay profile tells you:
- **fast decay** (IC gone in a day) → high-frequency, high-turnover, costs-sensitive (Day 179),
- **slow decay** (IC persists weeks) → lower turnover, more capacity, more crowding risk.

The decay rate sets your **rebalance frequency** and how aggressively to trade the signal — and it mirrors the OU half-life logic (Day 173). A signal whose IC decays slower than its transaction costs accrue is a *tradeable* signal; one that decays faster than you can cheaply trade it is not.

---

## Block 3 — The combination problem
You have signals `s₁, …, s_k`, each weakly predictive and *partly redundant* (momentum signals overlap; value signals overlap). Naively averaging double-counts the overlap. Combination methods, simplest → most complex:
- **Equal weight** — shockingly hard to beat out-of-sample; robust, no parameters (the 1/N lesson, Day 147).
- **IC-weighted** — weight each signal by its (shrunk) historical IC; better signals get more say.
- **Risk-adjusted / mean-variance of signals** — treat signals like assets: `w ∝ Σ_signal⁻¹ · IC` (Day 147), accounting for their *covariance* so you don't overweight redundant ones — **with heavy shrinkage**, or it overfits.
- **ML combination** (Day 191+) — trees/regularized regression to capture nonlinear interactions, at high overfitting risk.

**Concept check:** two signals each with IC 0.04 but correlated 0.9 with each other. Does combining them help much? (Barely — they carry nearly the *same* information, so the combination's IC is only marginally above either alone. The value of a new signal is its **orthogonal** (decorrelated) component, not its raw IC.)

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · orthogonalization is the whole game
The key quantitative insight: **a signal's marginal value = its IC *after* removing what your existing signals already predict.** Decorrelate before combining:
- **Gram–Schmidt / regression orthogonalization:** regress the new signal on the existing ones, keep the **residual** — the genuinely new information (the same residual-isolation trick as factor neutralization, Day 176, and Merton, Day 167).
- **PCA on the signal set** (Day 166): find the independent components; drop redundant directions.
- the combined IR obeys a **portfolio-of-signals** version of `IR = IC·√(effective # of independent signals)` — so *adding a 4th signal correlated 0.95 with the other three barely moves the IR, while a 4th that's *orthogonal* can lift it substantially.*

This reframes research: the question is never "is this signal good?" but "**what does this signal add that I don't already have?**" Marginal, orthogonal contribution is the currency — exactly the *effective breadth* point of Day 176, now about signals instead of names.

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · diversification, one level up
The profound recursion: **you diversify signals exactly the way you diversify assets.** Phase 7's entire portfolio theory — correlation matters more than individual quality (Day 146), the value of an addition is its *marginal* contribution to the portfolio, shrink your covariance estimates (Day 122), beware overfitting the weights (Day 147) — **applies verbatim to combining alphas.** A signal is an asset; a strategy is a portfolio of signals; and the same math that says "many decorrelated weak assets beat one strong one" says "many decorrelated weak signals beat one strong one." This is the unifying meta-skill of the quant: **find weakly-correlated sources of edge and combine them so the noise cancels and the signal compounds** — whether the "sources" are stocks, factors, signals, or strategies. The principle is scale-invariant; only the labels change.

**Hard puzzle:** you've spent a month building a sophisticated new signal with a standalone IC of 0.05 — better than most signals in your live book (which average 0.03). You're excited to add it. What's the one test that determines whether it's actually worth deploying? (Its **orthogonal/marginal IC** — regress the new signal on your *existing combined* alpha and compute the IC of the **residual**. If the new signal is 0.9-correlated with what you already trade, its standalone 0.05 IC adds almost *nothing* — you're re-buying information you own, and worse, increasing crowding/turnover for no gain. A *weaker* signal (IC 0.02) that's **uncorrelated** with your book would improve the combined IR *more*. The lesson — which trips up even strong researchers — is that **standalone IC is the wrong metric; marginal contribution to the existing portfolio is the right one.** Same as never judging a stock by its return alone (Day 146).)

**Career connection:** "you have ten alpha signals — how do you combine them?" Strong answer: clean and neutralize each, measure IC *and IC decay*, then combine accounting for their **correlations** — orthogonalize new signals against existing ones (keep the residual), weight by shrunk risk-adjusted IC (or just equal-weight, which is hard to beat OOS), and judge any addition by its **marginal/orthogonal** contribution, not standalone IC. Framing signal combination as *portfolio construction over signals* — with shrinkage and decorrelation — is the senior answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Orthogonalize and combine signals
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

N, T = 400, 600
fwd = rng.standard_normal((T,N))*0.02                    # future returns
# three signals: s1,s2 overlap heavily; s3 is orthogonal & weak
truth = fwd + rng.standard_normal((T,N))*0.1
s1 = truth + rng.standard_normal((T,N))*0.5
s2 = 0.9*s1 + 0.1*truth + rng.standard_normal((T,N))*0.1  # ~redundant with s1
s3 = rng.standard_normal((T,N)) + 0.3*truth              # weaker but independent

def ic(sig):                                            # avg cross-sectional IC
    return np.mean([np.corrcoef(sig[t], fwd[t])[0,1] for t in range(T)])

print(f"IC  s1={ic(s1):.3f}  s2={ic(s2):.3f}  s3={ic(s3):.3f}")

# naive average vs orthogonalized combination
def z(x): return (x - x.mean(1,keepdims=True))/x.std(1,keepdims=True)
naive = z(s1)+z(s2)+z(s3)

# orthogonalize s2 and s3 against s1 (keep residuals = new info)
def resid(y, X):                                        # regress y on X per day, keep resid
    out=np.zeros_like(y)
    for t in range(T):
        A=np.column_stack([np.ones(N)]+[x[t] for x in X])
        beta=np.linalg.lstsq(A,y[t],rcond=None)[0]
        out[t]=y[t]-A@beta
    return out
combo = z(s1)+z(resid(s2,[s1]))+z(resid(s3,[s1,s2]))

print(f"naive-average  IC = {ic(naive):.3f}")
print(f"orthogonalized IC = {ic(combo):.3f}  <- redundant s2 down-weighted, s3's new info kept")
```
The orthogonalized combination beats the naive average because it stops double-counting the redundant `s2` and properly credits the *independent* information in `s3` — marginal-contribution thinking, in code.

**Linux:** `python combine.py | tee combine.log`; raise `s2`'s correlation with `s1` toward 1.0 and watch its marginal value vanish.

## Quant Thinking Track — Marginal value, everywhere
"What does this add that I don't already have?" is one of the most powerful questions in quant work — and in thinking generally. A new signal, a new asset, a new feature, a new hire: its worth is its *orthogonal* contribution, not its standalone quality. Training yourself to evaluate things *at the margin, conditional on what you already hold* is the difference between accumulating redundancy and compounding genuine edge.
