## Block 1 — There is no single price
A stock doesn't have *a* price — it has a **limit order book (LOB)**: a live, two-sided list of orders waiting to trade.
- **Bids** — buyers' standing offers to buy, stacked by price (highest = **best bid**).
- **Asks/offers** — sellers' standing offers to sell (lowest = **best ask**).
- **Bid–ask spread** = best ask − best bid — the gap between what you can instantly buy at vs sell at.
- **Mid-price** = (bid + ask)/2 — the convenient fiction your daily-bar backtest used.
- **Depth** — how much size sits at each level (the book's shape).

You don't trade at "the price" — you **buy at the ask, sell at the bid.** The spread is the toll for immediacy.

*Why it matters:* every fill, every cost, every execution algorithm operates on this book. The clean prices in your backtest are a coarse summary of this churning microstructure.

---

## Block 2 — Two order types, two roles
- **Market order** — "trade *now* at the best available price." It **takes liquidity**, crosses the spread, and gets immediate execution — but pays the spread (and walks the book if large). You are a **liquidity taker**.
- **Limit order** — "trade only at price `X` or better." It **provides liquidity**, rests in the book, may earn the spread — but risks *not filling* (the price runs away). You are a **liquidity maker**.

This is the fundamental tension of all trading: **immediacy vs price.** Market order = certainty of execution, uncertainty of cost. Limit order = certainty of price, uncertainty of execution. Every execution decision (Day 185) trades along this axis.

**Concept check:** best bid 10.00, best ask 10.02. You send a market buy. Fill price and immediate "cost"? (10.02 — you pay the ask. If you immediately sold, you'd get 10.00, so the round-trip loses the 2¢ spread — the *effective* cost of demanding immediacy.)

---

## Block 3 — Liquidity and what the spread compensates
**Liquidity** = the ability to trade quickly, at size, without moving the price. A liquid market has a **tight spread** and **deep book**; an illiquid one has a wide spread and thin depth. The bid–ask spread is the **market maker's compensation** for three things (the rest of this module unpacks each):
1. **Order-processing costs** — the operational cost of providing the quote.
2. **Inventory risk** (Day 182) — the maker holds positions and bears price risk.
3. **Adverse selection** (Day 183) — the maker risks trading against someone who *knows more*.

So the spread isn't arbitrary friction — it's the *price of liquidity*, set by competition among makers against these costs. Understanding what it compensates is the key to both *paying* it less (execution) and *earning* it (market making).

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the book is a queue, price is a process of arrivals
Microstructure is fundamentally about **order flow as a stochastic process**:
- orders **arrive** (market orders, new limits) and **cancel** — modeled as Poisson-like arrival processes with intensities `λ`,
- the **mid-price moves** when the book is depleted on one side (a market buy lifts the offer; if it clears the level, the best ask steps up),
- price changes are therefore **discrete (tick-sized) and event-driven**, not the smooth Brownian motion of Day 140 — though Brownian motion *emerges* as the coarse-grained limit (a sum of many small order-flow events → CLT → diffusion, Day 157's tree-to-BS logic again),
- **queue position** matters: at a given price level, orders fill **first-in-first-out**, so *where you stand in the queue* determines your fill probability (Day 187).

The deep idea: **the smooth price processes of earlier modules are the macroscopic shadow of a microscopic queueing system.** Zoom in and "geometric Brownian motion" resolves into arrivals, cancellations, and queue dynamics — like seeing the molecules behind temperature.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · microstructure is the bridge from theory to reality
Every elegant model so far — Black–Scholes hedging (Day 155), stat-arb spreads (Day 171), portfolio rebalancing (Day 147) — assumed you could *trade at the price*. Microstructure is where that assumption meets the order book and you discover the *true cost of acting on a model*. This matters at two levels:
- **For any strategy:** your alpha must clear the spread and impact (Day 179); microstructure determines whether a "profitable" signal survives execution. A signal predicting moves smaller than the spread is *untradeable* no matter how high its IC.
- **As its own alpha:** the order book's dynamics — order flow imbalance, queue depletion, spread patterns — are *themselves* predictive (Day 187), the home turf of HFT and market making (Day 186). The plumbing isn't just a cost center; it's a strategy.

**The mindset shift:** stop treating "the price" as given and start seeing the *mechanism* that produces it. The trader who understands the book pays less, fills better, and sometimes gets paid (the spread) for providing the liquidity others demand.

**Hard puzzle:** your stat-arb signal (Day 171) predicts a spread will revert by 3 basis points over the next hour. The stock's bid–ask spread is 4 basis points. Is there a trade? (Almost certainly **no** — the *predicted move (3bp) is smaller than the cost of entering and exiting (you cross ~4bp of spread on the way in, and again on the way out)*. Even a perfectly correct signal loses money because the **microstructure cost exceeds the alpha.** This is *the* reason high-IC signals can be untradeable: they live below the spread. To trade it you'd need to **provide** liquidity (post limit orders, earn the spread instead of paying it) rather than take it — turning an impossible taker strategy into a possible maker one. The signal's value is inseparable from *how* you'd execute it. This single insight kills more "profitable" backtests than any other.)

**Career connection:** "what is the bid–ask spread, and why does it exist?" Strong answer: it's the gap between the best bid and best ask in the limit order book — you buy at the ask, sell at the bid — and it exists to compensate liquidity providers for **order-processing costs, inventory risk, and adverse selection.** Crucially, it's the *minimum round-trip cost of demanding immediacy*, so any signal must predict a move larger than the spread to be tradeable as a taker. Framing the spread as the *price of liquidity* with its three components is the microstructure answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · A minimal limit order book
```python
import numpy as np
from collections import defaultdict

class OrderBook:
    def __init__(self): self.bids=defaultdict(float); self.asks=defaultdict(float)
    def add(self, side, price, size):
        (self.bids if side=='bid' else self.asks)[price]+=size
    def best_bid(self): return max(self.bids) if self.bids else None
    def best_ask(self): return min(self.asks) if self.asks else None
    def spread(self):   return self.best_ask()-self.best_bid()
    def mid(self):      return 0.5*(self.best_ask()+self.best_bid())
    def market_buy(self, size):                       # walk the book, pay impact
        cost, remaining = 0.0, size
        for px in sorted(self.asks):
            take=min(remaining, self.asks[px]); cost+=take*px; remaining-=take
            self.asks[px]-=take
            if remaining<=0: break
        return cost/size                              # average fill price

ob=OrderBook()
for p,s in [(10.02,500),(10.03,800),(10.04,1000)]: ob.add('ask',p,s)
for p,s in [(10.00,500),(9.99,800),(9.98,1000)]:   ob.add('bid',p,s)
print(f"best bid {ob.best_bid()}  best ask {ob.best_ask()}  spread {ob.spread():.2f}  mid {ob.mid():.3f}")
print(f"market buy 300  avg fill = {ob.market_buy(300):.4f}  (pays the ask)")
print(f"market buy 1000 avg fill = {ob.market_buy(1000):.4f} (walks the book → impact)")
```
A small market order fills at the ask; a large one **walks up the book**, paying progressively worse prices — market impact (Day 179) emerging directly from the book's depth.

**Linux:** `python lob.py | tee lob.log`; thin out the depth and watch the large order's average fill worsen — depth *is* liquidity.
