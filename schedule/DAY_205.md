## Block 1 — Vector Autoregression (VAR)
**VAR** generalizes AR (Day 201) to *multiple* series that depend on *each other's* lagged values. For two series:
```
x_t = c₁ + a₁₁ x_{t−1} + a₁₂ y_{t−1} + … + ε₁_t
y_t = c₂ + a₂₁ x_{t−1} + a₂₂ y_{t−1} + … + ε₂_t
```
Each variable is regressed on the lagged values of *itself and all the others*. VAR captures:
- **lead–lag relationships** — does `y`'s past help predict `x`? (the `a₁₂` term),
- **feedback loops** — mutual influence between series,
- **system dynamics** — how a shock propagates through the whole web.

It's the workhorse for modelling **interacting financial/economic series** — e.g. equity indices, bond yields, FX, and macro variables jointly. (When the series are non-stationary but cointegrated, you use its cousin the **VECM**, Day 206.)

*Why it matters:* markets don't move in isolation; cross-asset lead–lag and spillovers are real sources of signal and risk that single-series models miss entirely.

---

## Block 2 — Granger causality: does X help predict Y?
**Granger causality** asks a precise, *predictive* question: *does including the past of `X` improve the forecast of `Y` beyond `Y`'s own past?* If yes, "`X` Granger-causes `Y`." It's tested by comparing two regressions:
- `Y` on its own lags only,
- `Y` on its own lags **plus** `X`'s lags — if `X`'s lags are jointly significant (F-test), `X` Granger-causes `Y`.

Critical caveat embedded in the name: **Granger causality is *predictive*, NOT true causality.** It means "`X`'s past contains information useful for predicting `Y`" — which can arise from genuine causation, a *common driver*, or a lead–lag with no causal link. It's about **information flow / predictability**, not mechanism (the correlation-vs-causation warning of Day 123, formalized for time series).

**Concept check:** you find that oil prices Granger-cause airline stock returns. Does this prove oil *causes* airline moves? (No — it proves oil's past *helps predict* airline returns (information flow). It's *consistent* with causation (fuel is a major airline cost), but Granger causality alone can't distinguish true causation from a common macro driver or lead–lag timing. "Granger-causes" = "helps forecast," not "is the cause.")

## Block 3 — Impulse response & variance decomposition
Once you have a VAR, two tools reveal the system's dynamics:
- **Impulse Response Function (IRF):** trace how a *shock* to one variable propagates through the system over time — "if bond yields jump 1%, what happens to equities over the next 10 days?" Shows the *dynamic, multi-period* response, not just contemporaneous correlation.
- **Forecast Error Variance Decomposition (FEVD):** how much of each variable's forecast uncertainty is attributable to shocks from *each* variable — quantifying **spillovers** (how much of equity volatility comes from bond shocks vs its own).

These are the standard tools for **systemic risk and spillover analysis** — e.g. measuring how shocks propagate across markets/institutions (the Diebold–Yilmaz connectedness framework builds directly on FEVD). They turn a fitted VAR into a *map of how the financial system transmits shocks.*

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the traps (and why they matter)
VAR/Granger are powerful but riddled with traps that the disciplined quant must respect:
- **Spurious causality from non-stationarity** (Day 142): running VAR/Granger on *non-stationary* (unit-root) series produces **spurious significance** — the Granger–Newbold trap, now in a multivariate setting. **Fix:** difference to stationarity first (use returns), or if cointegrated, use a **VECM** (Day 206). *Never* run Granger causality on raw prices.
- **Omitted-variable / common-driver bias:** if a hidden `Z` drives both `X` and `Y` with different lags, `X` will spuriously "Granger-cause" `Y`. Granger causality is only as good as the variables you included — a missing common driver fabricates causality.
- **Parameter explosion / overfitting:** a VAR with `n` variables and `p` lags has `n²p` coefficients — for many series this explodes (Day 191's overfitting risk), so you need **regularization** (Bayesian VAR / shrinkage, LASSO-VAR) or few variables/lags.
- **Lag selection:** choose `p` by AIC/BIC (Day 128 bias–variance) — too few misses dynamics, too many overfits.
- **Reverse/instantaneous causality:** Granger can't resolve contemporaneous (same-period) causation or feedback directionality cleanly; IRFs require an *ordering assumption* (Cholesky) that affects results.

The meta-point: VAR/Granger answer a **predictive, conditional-on-your-variables** question, and their validity hinges entirely on **stationarity, completeness of the variable set, and overfitting control.** Treated carelessly, they manufacture impressive-looking but spurious relationships — exactly the self-deception the whole curriculum warns against.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · "causality" is a loaded word — be precise
VAR and Granger causality teach a discipline that extends far beyond time series: **be ruthlessly precise about what kind of "causality" or "relationship" you've actually established.** The word "cause" carries enormous weight, and Granger causality is a *specific, limited, predictive* notion that is constantly over-interpreted:
- **what it IS:** "`X`'s past helps predict `Y` beyond `Y`'s own past" — a statement about *information flow and predictability*,
- **what it is NOT:** proof of a causal *mechanism*, of intervention effects ("if I change `X`, `Y` will change"), or of the *direction* of true causation.

This precision matters enormously in practice:
- a Granger-causal lead–lag can be a **tradeable signal** (X predicts Y → trade Y on X's moves) *even without* true causation — for *prediction*, information flow is what you need (Day 176),
- but for **risk and decision-making** ("will hedging X protect me from Y?"), you need *true* causal structure, and Granger causality alone won't give it — you can be fooled by common drivers,
- and the **stationarity/omitted-variable traps** mean a sloppy analysis produces *confident, spurious* causal claims — the multivariate version of every overfitting lesson.

This connects to the curriculum's spine of *intellectual honesty*: just as you distrust a backtest (Day 199), deflate a Sharpe (Day 199), and separate correlation from causation (Day 123), here you must distinguish **predictive information flow (Granger) from genuine causation** and from **spurious relationships born of non-stationarity or omitted variables**. The mature quant uses VAR/Granger for what they're *for* — mapping lead–lag predictability and shock spillovers across markets — while *never* dressing up "X helps predict Y" as "X causes Y." Precision about the *kind* of claim you're making is not pedantry; it's what prevents you from trading (or hedging) on a relationship that isn't what you think it is. **Know exactly what you've shown — predictability, not mechanism — and act accordingly.**

**Hard puzzle:** you run a VAR and find that small-cap stock returns "Granger-cause" large-cap returns (small-caps lead). A colleague proposes: "Great — small-caps *drive* the market, so let's trade large-caps based on small-cap moves." Evaluate both the *trading* claim and the *causal* claim. (Two different claims, two different verdicts: **(1) The causal claim ("small-caps drive the market") is unjustified.** Granger causality shows small-cap returns *help predict* large-cap returns — **information flow, not mechanism** (Block 2). The likely *real* explanation is **not** that small-caps cause large-cap moves, but a **common driver with differential timing**: market-wide information gets incorporated into *less liquid* small-caps and large-caps at different speeds (or small-caps react faster to some macro signal), so the *lead–lag is a microstructure/liquidity artifact*, not causation. There may also be **omitted variables** (a macro factor driving both). So "small-caps drive the market" over-interprets a predictive relationship as a causal one — exactly the error to avoid. **(2) The trading claim is *potentially* valid — but with heavy caveats.** For *prediction*, you don't need true causation — if small-cap moves genuinely *lead* large-cap moves out-of-sample, that lead–lag is a **tradeable signal** regardless of mechanism (Day 176: information flow is enough for alpha). BUT: (a) verify it's **not spurious** — was the VAR run on *stationary* returns, not non-stationary prices (Day 142)? (b) is it **robust out-of-sample** and across regimes (Days 199/204), or a data-mined artifact? (c) crucially, is it **tradeable net of costs** (Day 179)? A lead–lag of *minutes* may be real but arbitraged away by faster players (Day 186) — you'd be racing HFT; a lead–lag of *days* is more tradeable but rarer and may reflect slow information diffusion that others also exploit (crowding, Day 178). (d) Such liquidity-driven lead–lags are **classic decaying signals** (Day 149). **Verdict:** reject the causal story (it's predictability from common drivers/liquidity, not causation), but *test* the trading signal rigorously — validate stationarity, out-of-sample robustness, and net-of-cost tradeability before believing it's real. The discipline: *separate the predictive claim (maybe tradeable) from the causal claim (unjustified), and validate the former hard.* Conflating them — "it predicts, therefore it causes, therefore the trade is safe" — is how you end up trading a spurious or already-arbitraged relationship.)

**Career connection:** "what's Granger causality, and can you use it to find trading signals?" Strong answer: Granger causality tests whether one series' **past helps predict** another beyond the second's own past — it's about **predictive information flow, NOT true causation** (a common driver or lead–lag can produce it). For trading, that's often *enough* — a robust out-of-sample lead–lag is a tradeable signal regardless of mechanism — but I'd be careful: run it on **stationary** series (raw prices give spurious results — the Granger–Newbold trap), watch for **omitted common drivers**, validate **out-of-sample and net of costs** (lead–lags decay and may be arbitraged by faster players), and **never claim causation** from it. I'd use the broader **VAR** framework for the joint dynamics and **impulse-response/variance-decomposition** for spillover/systemic-risk analysis. Distinguishing predictive Granger causality from true causation — and naming the stationarity and omitted-variable traps — signals real econometric maturity.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · VAR, Granger causality, and impulse response
```python
import numpy as np, pandas as pd
from statsmodels.tsa.api import VAR
from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import grangercausalitytests
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# simulate: X leads Y (Y depends on X's lag), both stationary (returns)
T=1500; X=rng.standard_normal(T)*0.01
Y=np.zeros(T)
for t in range(1,T): Y[t]=0.3*X[t-1] + 0.1*Y[t-1] + rng.standard_normal()*0.01  # X leads Y
df = pd.DataFrame({"X":X,"Y":Y})

# fit a VAR and select lag order by AIC
model = VAR(df); res = model.fit(maxlags=5, ic="aic")
print(f"selected lag order (AIC): {res.k_ar}")

# Granger causality: does X help predict Y? (and the reverse?)
print("\nDoes X Granger-cause Y? (expect YES — X leads):")
gc_xy = grangercausalitytests(df[["Y","X"]], maxlag=2, verbose=False)
print(f"  p-value (lag 1): {gc_xy[1][0]['ssr_ftest'][1]:.4f}")
print("Does Y Granger-cause X? (expect NO):")
gc_yx = grangercausalitytests(df[["X","Y"]], maxlag=2, verbose=False)
print(f"  p-value (lag 1): {gc_yx[1][0]['ssr_ftest'][1]:.4f}")

# impulse response: how a shock to X propagates to Y
irf = res.irf(8)
print(f"\nresponse of Y to a unit shock in X (8 steps): {irf.irfs[:,1,0].round(4)}")
```
The VAR + Granger test correctly finds **X Granger-causes Y but not vice versa** (X leads), and the impulse response traces how an X-shock propagates into Y — lead–lag structure quantified (on *stationary* data, avoiding the spurious-causality trap).

## Quant Thinking Track — Name your claim precisely
Across all analysis, sloppiness about *what kind of claim* you're making is a primary source of error. "X is related to Y" could mean correlation, Granger-predictability, true causation, or spurious co-trending — each with vastly different implications for action. The discipline of stating *exactly* what you've established — "X's past helps predict Y out-of-sample, which may or may not be causal" — keeps you from over-trusting a relationship and trading or hedging on something it isn't. Precision in claims is precision in thinking.
