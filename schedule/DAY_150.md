## Block 1 — The Efficient Market Hypothesis (Fama)
**EMH:** prices already reflect available information, so you can't *systematically* earn risk-adjusted excess returns. Three strengths:
- **Weak form:** prices reflect all *past prices* → **technical analysis** on price history alone can't beat the market (returns are ~unpredictable, Day 141).
- **Semi-strong form:** prices reflect all *public* information (news, fundamentals, filings) → **fundamental analysis** of public data can't systematically win; prices adjust to news fast.
- **Strong form:** prices reflect *all* information, even private/insider → *nobody* can beat the market. (Empirically false — insiders demonstrably profit, which is why insider trading is illegal.)

*Why it matters:* EMH is the **null hypothesis** of all quant research. Every strategy you build is implicitly a claim "the market is inefficient in *this specific way*," and the burden of proof is on you (Day 149 falsification).

---

## Block 2 — Why markets are *nearly* (not perfectly) efficient
Markets are efficient *because* smart, well-capitalized people *try* to beat them — competition drives prices toward fair value and arbitrages away easy edges. But the convergence is **imperfect and incomplete**:
- information takes *time* to diffuse,
- arbitrage has *costs and risks* (Block 4),
- humans are *predictably irrational* (behavioral edges, Day 149).

So the honest stance is **"markets are mostly efficient, with small, hard-won, decaying pockets of inefficiency."** That sentence *is* the quant industry's reason to exist. Perfectly efficient → no point trying; wildly inefficient → it'd already be arbitraged. The truth lives in the narrow, lucrative middle.

---

## Block 3 — The Grossman–Stiglitz paradox (the deep one)
*If prices already reflected all information, no one would be paid to gather information — so no one would gather it — so prices couldn't reflect it.* **Perfectly efficient markets are impossible.** There must be *enough* inefficiency to compensate the researchers who do the costly work of finding and correcting mispricings. **Alpha is the wage paid to the people who keep the market efficient.** This resolves the paradox: you, the quant, are *paid by the inefficiency you remove*. Markets are in a restless equilibrium where the edge is always *just* big enough to pay for the effort to chase it — and no bigger.

**Concept check:** "if markets are efficient, why do quant funds make money?" (Because they *can't* be perfectly efficient — Grossman–Stiglitz — and the residual inefficiency is precisely the compensation for the research/risk that keeps prices roughly right.)

---

## Block 4 — Anomalies and *limits to arbitrage*
Persistent, documented violations of EMH:
- **Momentum** (winners keep winning 3–12m) — arguably the most robust anomaly across markets and centuries,
- **Value, Size, Low-volatility, Quality** (Day 148 factors),
- **Post-earnings-announcement drift, calendar effects, overreaction/reversal.**

Why don't these get instantly arbitraged away? **Limits to arbitrage** (Shleifer–Vishny):
- **Fundamental risk** — the mispricing can widen before it corrects (you can be right and still get carried out),
- **Noise-trader risk** — "markets can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent" (Keynes); LTCM (1998) was *right* and still blew up,
- **Costs & constraints** — short-selling limits/borrow costs, leverage limits, transaction costs, capital constraints,
- **Career/agency risk** — managers who deviate and lose get fired before being vindicated.

So inefficiencies persist *because correcting them is risky and costly* — which is exactly why they're profitable to those willing and able to bear that risk.

**Hard puzzle:** if momentum has "worked" for decades and is widely published, why hasn't it been arbitraged to zero? (Limits to arbitrage: momentum suffers occasional violent crashes — negative skew — so harvesting it requires bearing real, hard-to-hedge tail risk; many can't or won't hold through the drawdowns. The premium is *compensation for that risk*, not a free lunch — consistent with a *nearly*-efficient market, not an inefficient one.)

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the worldview that makes you employable
The synthesis of this whole module (Days 144–150) is a coherent philosophy:
1. Markets are **mostly efficient** → be humble; most "edges" are noise (Day 124).
2. But **not perfectly** (Grossman–Stiglitz) → real, small edges exist for those who work.
3. Edges are **risk premia or behavioral/structural inefficiencies**, **decay** when crowded (Day 149), and are **limited by arbitrage costs**.
4. So you compete on **better data, better models, lower costs, more breadth** (`IR=IC·√Breadth`), and **relentless out-of-sample discipline**.

This is neither naïve ("markets are random, indexing is the only answer") nor arrogant ("I can predict prices"). It's the **calibrated middle** that quant firms hire for: *"I believe markets are hard to beat, here is a specific, economically-motivated, statistically-validated inefficiency, and here is why it should persist long enough — and at the capacity — for me to harvest it."*

**Career connection:** "do you believe markets are efficient?" The answer that gets you hired: *"Nearly — and Grossman–Stiglitz says they have to be only nearly, because perfect efficiency is self-defeating. My job is to find and harvest the residual inefficiency that pays for the research, while respecting limits to arbitrage and alpha decay."* That single paragraph demonstrates you understand the entire intellectual structure of the field.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Test weak-form efficiency on a real-ish series
```python
import numpy as np
from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import acf
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# (1) an efficient (random-walk) market: returns should be unpredictable
eff_ret = rng.standard_normal(5000)*0.01
# (2) an INEFFICIENT market with weak momentum (autocorrelated returns)
mom = np.zeros(5000)
for t in range(1, 5000):
    mom[t] = 0.08*mom[t-1] + rng.standard_normal()*0.01     # AR(1) drift

for name, r in [("efficient", eff_ret), ("momentum", mom)]:
    a = acf(r, nlags=5, fft=True)[1:]
    print(f"{name:10s} return ACF lags1-5: {np.round(a,3)}")

# naive momentum strategy: go long after an up day, short after a down day
def strat_sharpe(r):
    pos = np.sign(r[:-1])                 # yesterday's sign (lookahead-safe)
    pnl = pos * r[1:]
    return pnl.mean()/pnl.std()*np.sqrt(252)
print("strategy Sharpe  efficient:", round(strat_sharpe(eff_ret),2),
      " momentum:", round(strat_sharpe(mom),2))
```
On the efficient series the ACF ≈ 0 and the strategy earns ≈ 0 Sharpe (you *can't* beat a random walk, Day 141). On the inefficient series the positive ACF leaks into a real Sharpe — a *toy* demonstration that exploitable alpha exists exactly to the extent the market deviates from a random walk.

**Linux:** `python emh.py | tee emh.log`. Sobering exercise: run the same test on *real* daily equity returns — the ACF will be tiny, a visceral lesson in how *nearly* efficient real markets are.
