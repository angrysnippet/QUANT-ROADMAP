## Block 1 — Two faces of duration
- **Macaulay duration** = the *weighted-average time to receive the cash flows*, weights = each cash flow's present value as a fraction of price. Units: **years**. A zero-coupon bond's Macaulay duration = its maturity exactly (all cash at the end); coupon bonds are shorter (some cash arrives early).
- **Modified duration** = Macaulay / `(1 + y/freq)` — the *percentage price sensitivity* to yield:
```
% price change ≈ − ModDuration × Δy
```
A modified duration of 7 means **+1% in yields → ≈ −7% in price.** This is the working definition: duration as the **price elasticity to rates.**

*Why it matters:* duration converts an abstract "rate risk" into a hedgeable, comparable number across any bond or portfolio.

---

## Block 2 — DV01: risk in dollars
Traders think in dollars, not percentages. **DV01** (dollar value of an 01) = the price change for a **1 basis point** (0.01%) move in yield:
```
DV01 = ModDuration × Price × 0.0001
```
Also called **PV01 / BPV**. It's the rates analogue of dollar-delta: "I make/lose \$X per basis point." Hedging a bond position means matching DV01s — sell enough of another instrument so the *net* DV01 ≈ 0. The whole rates desk runs on DV01 ladders by maturity bucket.

**Concept check:** a \$10M bond with modified duration 8. Its DV01? (`8 × 10,000,000 × 0.0001 = \$8,000` per bp. A 25 bp selloff → ≈ −\$200k. That's the number a risk manager actually watches.)

## Block 3 — Duration is delta (the option analogy)
Line up the parallels with Day 154 and it clicks:
| Options | Bonds |
|--------|-------|
| Underlying = `S` | Underlying = yield `y` |
| Delta `∂V/∂S` | (−)Duration `∂P/∂y` |
| Gamma `∂²V/∂S²` | Convexity `∂²P/∂y²` (Day 163) |
| Dollar-delta | DV01 |

Duration is a **first-order (linear) approximation** to the price–yield curve — accurate for small moves, increasingly wrong for large ones (that's what convexity fixes). Same Taylor-expansion logic as the Greeks; rates just call the variables different names.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the Taylor expansion of bond price
Expand price around the current yield:
```
ΔP ≈ (dP/dy)·Δy + ½(d²P/dy²)·Δy²
   = −ModDur·P·Δy  +  ½·Convexity·P·Δy²
```
- **First term** = duration (linear, the topic today).
- **Second term** = convexity (curvature, Day 163) — the correction that matters for big moves.

Two key properties of duration:
- **Additive (value-weighted):** a portfolio's duration = the value-weighted average of its bonds' durations → you can dial portfolio rate risk like a knob.
- **Falls with coupon, rises with maturity, falls as yields rise** — all because they change *when* the PV-weighted cash flows land.

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · duration is the universal rate-risk currency
Every interest-rate-sensitive thing — a bond, a mortgage, a swap, a pension liability, a bank's loan book — has a duration, so duration becomes the **common unit** for measuring and *netting* rate risk across wildly different instruments. **Asset-liability management** (banks, insurers, pension funds) is essentially *duration matching*: make the duration of your assets equal the duration of your liabilities so a rate move hits both sides equally and your net worth is **immunized** (Day 166). The 2023 Silicon Valley Bank collapse was, at its core, a **duration mismatch**: long-duration bonds funded by instant-access deposits — rates rose, the assets cratered, the deposits ran. Duration isn't academic; it's solvency.

**Hard puzzle:** you hold a 10-year bond (duration 8) and want zero rate risk, but you can only trade a 2-year bond (duration 1.9). How much of the 2-year do you short to hedge \$10M of the 10-year? (Match **DV01s**: short notional = `(D_long × P_long)/(D_short) = (8×10M)/1.9 ≈ \$42M` of the 2-year. You short *much more notional* of the short-duration bond to equalize dollar sensitivity. Caveat: this hedges *parallel* shifts only — if the curve *steepens* (2s and 10s move differently), you're still exposed. Real desks hedge by *key-rate* durations across the curve, not one number — the limitation that motivates Day 164.)

**Career connection:** "what's duration, and how would you hedge a bond's rate risk?" Strong answer: modified duration = % price move per 1% yield change (the bond's delta); hedge by **matching DV01** with another rates instrument, while noting that single-duration hedging only neutralizes *parallel* curve shifts — non-parallel moves need key-rate durations. Mentioning the parallel-shift limitation is what separates a textbook answer from a desk answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Duration, DV01, and the hedge
```python
import numpy as np

def cashflows(face, cpn, n, freq=2):
    c = face*cpn/freq
    t = np.arange(1, n*freq+1)/freq
    cf = np.full(len(t), c); cf[-1] += face
    return t, cf

def metrics(face, cpn, y, n, freq=2):
    t, cf = cashflows(face, cpn, n, freq)
    pv = cf/(1+y/freq)**(t*freq)
    P  = pv.sum()
    macaulay = (t*pv).sum()/P
    mod_dur  = macaulay/(1+y/freq)
    dv01     = mod_dur*P*1e-4
    return P, macaulay, mod_dur, dv01

P,mac,mod,dv01 = metrics(100, 0.04, 0.05, 10)
print(f"price={P:.3f}  Macaulay={mac:.2f}y  ModDur={mod:.2f}  DV01={dv01:.4f} per $100")

# duration predicts the price move (check vs full reprice for a 50bp selloff)
P2,_,_,_ = metrics(100,0.04,0.055,10)
print(f"actual ΔP for +50bp: {P2-P:+.3f}")
print(f"duration estimate:   {-mod*P*0.005:+.3f}  (close, but misses convexity)")
```
The linear duration estimate tracks the actual move — but undershoots slightly for the 50 bp jump, the gap convexity (Day 163) explains.

**Linux:** `python duration.py | tee dur.log`; sweep coupon and maturity to confirm duration *rises with maturity, falls with coupon.*

## Quant Thinking Track — First-order thinking and its limits
Duration is the rates incarnation of the universal move: **linearize around the current point.** It's powerful (one number, additive, hedgeable) and dangerous (only locally true). Knowing *when* the linear approximation breaks — big moves, non-parallel curve shifts — is the difference between a hedge that holds and one that fails in the crisis it was built for.
