## Block 1 — Prior → Likelihood → Posterior
```
posterior ∝ likelihood × prior
P(H | E) ∝ P(E | H) · P(H)
```
- **Prior** `P(H)`: belief before evidence.
- **Likelihood** `P(E|H)`: how well a hypothesis explains the evidence.
- **Posterior** `P(H|E)`: updated belief.

*Why it matters:* a backtest is *evidence*, not truth. Your belief in a strategy should be the *posterior* after combining the result with a sensible prior — not the raw backtest number.

---

## Block 2 — Sequential updating
Yesterday's posterior is today's prior. Evidence accumulates:
```
P(H | E₁, E₂) ∝ P(E₂|H) · P(E₁|H) · P(H)
```
Each independent observation multiplies in. This is exactly how an online model (or a disciplined researcher) refines a view trade by trade.

**Concept check:** you flip a possibly-biased coin and see HHHH. Does that *prove* bias? (No — update toward bias, but with a reasonable prior that most coins are fair, four heads is weak evidence: `P(HHHH|fair)=1/16` is not tiny.)

---

## Block 3 — The base-rate / overconfidence trap
The disease-test result (Day 118: 99% accurate test → only 50% posterior) is the canonical lesson: **strong evidence + rare hypothesis = modest posterior.** In markets, a "significant" signal found by testing thousands of ideas has a *low prior of being real* — so its posterior is weak even with a good p-value (this is the multiple-testing problem, Day 125).

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics (shrinkage / Bayesian estimate)
A Bayesian estimate **shrinks** noisy sample estimates toward the prior. Rough intuition for a mean:
```
estimate ≈ (n·x̄ + k·prior_mean) / (n + k)
```
With little data (`n` small) you lean on the prior; with lots of data the sample dominates.

**Exercise:** a new trader has 5 winning trades out of 5. Naive win-rate = 100%. With a prior centred at 50% (`k=10`), Bayesian estimate ≈ `(5·1 + 10·0.5)/(5+10) ≈ 67%` — far more sensible. **Challenge:** why does shrinkage reduce overfitting to small samples?

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking (Bayesian is the quant default)

- **Priors discipline backtests:** if you tested 1,000 random signals, your prior that any *specific* "winner" is real should be low — demand much stronger evidence.
- **Bayesian shrinkage** stabilises noisy estimates (e.g. shrinking individual-stock means toward the sector/market mean — the **James–Stein** insight: shrunk estimates beat raw means).
- **Updating, not flip-flopping:** good quants move beliefs *proportionally* to evidence strength, neither ignoring new data nor over-reacting to one print.

**Hard puzzle:** two strategies show identical 1-year returns. One came from a clear economic hypothesis (high prior); the other from brute-force search over 10,000 combos (low prior). Same evidence — which do you trust more, and why? (The hypothesis-driven one: higher prior → higher posterior. Identical likelihoods, different priors.)

**Career connection:** "how would you tell if this backtest is real or overfit?" → a Bayesian answer (prior from how the idea was found × evidence strength) signals senior-level judgment.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Posterior of a coin's bias
```python
import numpy as np
# Beta prior conjugate to Bernoulli: posterior = Beta(a+heads, b+tails)
heads, tails = 8, 2
a, b = 2, 2                      # weak prior centred at 0.5
post_mean = (a+heads)/(a+b+heads+tails)
print(round(post_mean, 3))      # shrinks 0.8 toward 0.5 -> ~0.667
```

## Quant Thinking Track — Beliefs Are Distributions, Not Points
Amateurs hold point beliefs ("this works / doesn't"). Bayesians hold **distributions** and update them. That habit — quantified uncertainty that moves with evidence — is the core of quant research and risk.
