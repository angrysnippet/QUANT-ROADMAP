# Day 148 — 💹📊 Finance & Portfolio World · CAPM & Beta — The Price of Systematic Risk

> If everyone holds the market portfolio (Day 147), then an asset's fair return depends only on **how much market risk it adds** — its **beta**. CAPM is the first equilibrium model of *expected return*, and beta is the most-used number in all of finance.

**Focus:** Systematic vs Idiosyncratic Risk · Beta · The Security Market Line · CAPM Equation · Where CAPM Breaks (→ Factors)

## Block 1 — Only systematic risk is priced
From Day 146: total risk = **systematic** (market-wide, undiversifiable) + **idiosyncratic** (asset-specific, diversifiable). A diversified investor has *already* shed the idiosyncratic part for free — so the market won't pay a premium for it. **You are compensated only for the risk you cannot diversify away.** CAPM turns that one sentence into a pricing equation.

---

## Block 2 — Beta: sensitivity to the market
```
β = Cov(r_asset, r_market) / Var(r_market)
```
Beta is the slope of the asset's returns regressed on the market's — *how much it moves when the market moves*:
- `β = 1` → moves with the market (e.g. a broad index),
- `β > 1` → amplifies (high-beta: tech, cyclicals),
- `β < 1` → dampens (defensives: utilities, staples),
- `β < 0` → moves *against* the market (gold-ish, some hedges) — rare and valuable.

Beta is *the* measure of systematic exposure, and it's literally the regression slope from the linear-regression module (Day 130).

---

## Block 3 — The CAPM equation and the Security Market Line
```
E[rᵢ] = r_f + βᵢ · (E[r_m] − r_f)
```
Expected return = risk-free rate + beta × the **market risk premium**. Plotting `E[r]` against `β` gives the **Security Market Line (SML)** — a straight line; in CAPM's world, *every* asset sits exactly on it. Note what's **absent**: the asset's own σ. Total volatility is irrelevant to its expected return — **only β (systematic risk) is priced.** A wild, high-σ stock with β=0 earns the *risk-free rate* in CAPM, because its risk diversifies away.

**Concept check:** `r_f = 3%`, market premium `= 6%`. Fair expected return for β=1.5? (`3 + 1.5·6 = 12%`.) For β=0 but σ=80%? (`3%` — idiosyncratic vol earns nothing.)

---

## Block 4 — Alpha, and where CAPM breaks (the bridge to factors)
Rearrange with realized data and you get the regression:
```
rᵢ − r_f = αᵢ + βᵢ(r_m − r_f) + εᵢ
```
- **α (alpha):** return *beyond* what beta explains — the holy grail, tomorrow's topic (Day 149). CAPM says α should be **zero** for everyone; reality disagrees.
- **β·(r_m−r_f):** the part you could've gotten cheaply by just buying the index ("beta is cheap, alpha is dear").

**CAPM is empirically rejected** — but its *failures* built modern quant equity:
- **Size** (small caps beat their beta), **Value** (cheap beats expensive) → **Fama–French 3-factor**,
- **Momentum** (Carhart 4th factor), then **Profitability/Quality, Investment** → **Fama–French 5-factor**,
- **Low-volatility anomaly:** low-β stocks have historically beaten CAPM's prediction — a direct contradiction.

The lesson: CAPM's *one* systematic factor (the market) is too coarse. Returns are driven by **several** priced factors. CAPM is the **first** and conceptual ancestor of the entire factor-investing industry (APT, Fama–French, Barra risk models).

**Hard puzzle:** a stock has σ = 60% (very volatile) but β = 0.1. CAPM says its expected return ≈ risk-free. How is that not insane? (Its volatility is almost entirely *idiosyncratic* — it diversifies away in a portfolio, so the market refuses to pay a premium for it. Expected return tracks *systematic* exposure (β), not total σ. This is the single most counterintuitive — and most tested — CAPM insight.)

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · separating alpha from beta is the whole game
Every return stream decomposes into **beta** (cheap, replicable market exposure) and **alpha** (scarce, skill-based excess). The entire active-management and quant industry exists to:
1. **measure** beta exposures (risk models),
2. **hedge out** unwanted betas (market-neutral, dollar-neutral),
3. **isolate and harvest** genuine alpha (or cheap *alternative* factor premia).

When you "hedge market risk," you're zeroing β so what remains is α + idiosyncratic noise. CAPM gave the field the vocabulary — α, β, systematic, idiosyncratic — that every desk still speaks.

**Career connection:** "what's beta?" Weak: "how volatile a stock is." (Wrong — that's σ.) Strong: "`Cov(rᵢ,r_m)/Var(r_m)` — sensitivity to the market and the *only* risk CAPM prices; total volatility doesn't earn a premium because idiosyncratic risk diversifies away. CAPM is rejected empirically, which is exactly why we use multi-factor (Fama–French) models." That answer separates someone who *gets* asset pricing from someone who memorized a formula.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Estimate beta and alpha by regression
```python
import numpy as np
import statsmodels.api as sm
rng = np.random.default_rng(2)

mkt   = rng.standard_normal(2520)*0.01                     # market excess returns
true_beta, true_alpha = 1.3, 0.0002
stock = true_alpha + true_beta*mkt + rng.standard_normal(2520)*0.012  # + idio noise

X = sm.add_constant(mkt)
res = sm.OLS(stock, X).fit()
alpha, beta = res.params
print(f"beta  = {beta:.2f}  (true 1.30)")
print(f"alpha = {alpha*252:.3%} annual,  t-stat = {res.tvalues[0]:.2f}")
print(f"R² (systematic share of variance) = {res.rsquared:.2f}")

# CAPM fair return
rf, mkt_prem = 0.03, 0.06
print(f"CAPM expected return at beta={beta:.2f}: {rf + beta*mkt_prem:.2%}")
```
The α t-stat hovers near zero (no real edge here) — which is the honest default: **most apparent alpha is statistically indistinguishable from luck** (Day 124). `R²` shows how much of the stock's variance is systematic; `1−R²` is the diversifiable idiosyncratic part.

**Linux:** `python capm.py | tee capm.log`; rolling-window the regression to *see* beta drift over time — betas are not constant, another CAPM idealization that breaks.

## Quant Thinking Track — Equilibrium thinking
CAPM introduces a new move: **equilibrium reasoning** — "if everyone optimizes (Day 147) and markets clear, what *must* prices/returns be?" Even when the conclusion is empirically wrong, the *method* — derive prices from collective rational behavior, then test it — is how quants generate and falsify hypotheses. A wrong-but-precise model that you can *reject with data* is worth more than a vague one you can't.
