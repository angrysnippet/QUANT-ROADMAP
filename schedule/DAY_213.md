## Block 1 — The bet-sizing problem
Suppose you have a genuine edge — a positive-expectation bet (a trade, a strategy). How much of your capital should you risk on it? Two failure modes:
- **bet too little** → you barely grow; the edge is wasted,
- **bet too much** → a string of losses (which *will* happen, even with an edge — Day 121) wipes you out. **Ruin is absorbing** (Day 144): once at zero, you're done, and no future edge can save you.

The key realization: **bet sizing is as important as the edge itself.** A great strategy sized wrong loses money or blows up; a modest strategy sized well compounds beautifully. And critically — what you maximize is **long-run *compound* (geometric) growth** (Day 144), not single-period expected value, because you reinvest and ruin is permanent.

*Why it matters:* most blowups are *not* from bad edges but from good edges **over-bet** (Day 178). Sizing is where strategies live or die.

---

## Block 2 — The Kelly criterion
**Kelly (1956)** answers: the bet size that **maximizes long-run compound growth.** For a simple bet with win probability `p`, win/loss odds `b`:
```
f* = p − (1−p)/b      (fraction of capital to bet)
```
For a continuous return with excess return `μ` and variance `σ²` (the finance form):
```
f* = μ / σ²           (the growth-optimal leverage)
```
This is the **growth-optimal** fraction — bet `f*` and your capital grows *faster in the long run* than with any other fixed fraction. Key properties:
- **Kelly grows wealth faster than any other strategy** asymptotically (almost surely),
- it's intimately tied to the **geometric mean** (Day 144) — Kelly *maximizes the expected log of wealth* = expected geometric growth rate,
- `f* = μ/σ²` says: bet *more* when the edge `μ` is bigger and *less* when the risk `σ²` is bigger — intuitive and quantified.

**Concept check:** a bet wins 60% of the time, paying even money (`b=1`). Kelly fraction? (`f* = 0.6 − 0.4/1 = 0.20` → bet 20% of capital. Note even with a strong 60/40 edge, Kelly says bet only 20% — and most people's instinct to bet much more is exactly what causes ruin.)

---

## Block 3 — Why Kelly is "aggressive" and the case for fractional Kelly
Full Kelly is **growth-optimal but wild**: it produces *large, gut-wrenching drawdowns* (a full-Kelly bettor routinely sees their capital halve), and it's *extremely sensitive to your estimate of the edge* — and you **never know your true edge precisely** (Day 149). **Over-estimating your edge → over-betting → the growth rate turns *negative* and you court ruin** (the cliff is steep just past `f*`). So practitioners use **fractional Kelly** — bet a *fraction* of `f*` (commonly **half-Kelly**):
- **half-Kelly captures ~75% of the growth with ~50% of the volatility** — a vastly better risk-adjusted ride,
- it provides a **margin of safety** against estimation error in your edge (you don't know `μ` exactly — Day 191's parameter uncertainty),
- it dramatically reduces drawdowns and the probability of catastrophic loss.

The rule of thumb: **full Kelly is a *ceiling*, not a target** — bet *less* than Kelly, almost always. Most experienced practitioners run quarter- to half-Kelly.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the growth-vs-leverage curve and the ruin cliff
The deep mathematics: long-run **compound growth rate as a function of bet fraction `f`** is an *inverted parabola* (in the continuous/log-normal case):
```
g(f) = f·μ − ½·f²·σ²     (expected log-growth rate)
```
- maximized at **`f* = μ/σ²`** (Kelly) — the peak,
- **`g(0) = 0`** (bet nothing, no growth),
- **`g(2f*) = 0`** — bet *twice* Kelly and your long-run growth is **back to zero** despite a positive edge!,
- **`g(f) < 0` for `f > 2f*`** — over-bet past double-Kelly and you have **negative growth → ruin**, even with a genuine edge.

This curve is the single most important picture in bet sizing, and it teaches several profound lessons:
- **the volatility drag `½f²σ²`** (Day 144) is what pulls growth down at high `f` — leverage amplifies variance quadratically, and variance *destroys* compound growth,
- **the curve is asymmetric and the downside is steep** — being *under* Kelly costs you a little growth (the parabola is flat near the top); being *over* Kelly costs you a lot and can go negative. So **err on the side of betting less** — the penalty for under-betting is mild, for over-betting severe,
- **a positive edge guarantees nothing** — `μ > 0` but `f` too large → `g < 0` → you lose in the long run. *The edge is necessary but not sufficient; correct sizing is what converts edge into growth.*

This is the rigorous version of Day 178's lesson ("most blowups are over-levered good strategies") and unifies with volatility drag (Day 144), the geometric mean, and survival as the binding constraint (Day 144). The Kelly curve *is* the mathematics of "how leverage kills."

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · survival first, then growth
The Kelly criterion crowns one of the most important and hardest-won lessons in all of finance: **how much you bet matters as much as whether you have an edge — and the asymmetry of ruin means you should almost always bet less than seems optimal.** This reorganizes the whole approach to risk:
- **growth is multiplicative and ruin is absorbing** (Day 144) — you must *survive every period* to compound, so a single over-sized bet that wipes you out negates all past and future edge. **Survival is the precondition for growth**, not a competing goal,
- **the optimal bet maximizes *expected log-wealth* (geometric growth)**, not expected wealth — because you reinvest, and the geometric mean is what compounds (Day 144). Maximizing arithmetic EV leads to over-betting and ruin,
- **you never know your edge precisely** (Day 149/191), so the *robust* choice is **fractional Kelly** — bet a fraction of the estimated optimum to buy a margin of safety against estimation error, and accept slightly lower growth for dramatically lower ruin risk,
- **the penalty structure is asymmetric** — under-betting costs a little, over-betting can cost everything — so the prudent bias is *always toward betting less.*

This connects to the deepest spine of the curriculum: **respect uncertainty and prioritize survival** (Days 119 fat tails, 144 drawdown, 178 leverage/Quant Quake, 150 limits to arbitrage). The amateur thinks "I have an edge, so I should bet big to maximize returns"; the professional thinks "I have an *uncertain* edge, ruin is permanent, and the geometry of compounding punishes volatility — so I'll size *conservatively* (fractional Kelly), survive the inevitable losing streaks, and let the edge compound over time." This is also why **position sizing and risk management are where careers are made and lost** — far more traders are destroyed by *over-betting a real edge* than by *not having an edge*. The Kelly criterion gives the precise mathematics: there's an optimal bet size, over-betting past *twice* it turns a winning strategy into a losing one, and since you can't know your edge exactly, you should deliberately bet *below* the optimum. The unifying wisdom — **survive first, grow second; size for the edge you *have*, with a margin for the edge you *might not* have; and remember that the downside of over-betting is unbounded while the downside of under-betting is mild** — is one of the most valuable mental models a quant can possess, and it's why the best traders are often the most *conservative* sizers. **You can't compound if you're broke; bet to survive, and growth follows.**

**Hard puzzle:** Trader A and Trader B have the identical strategy with the same genuine edge. Trader A bets full Kelly; Trader B bets half-Kelly. Over a long career, who ends up wealthier, and what's the deeper lesson about the relationship between aggression and long-run success? (Counterintuitively, **Trader B (half-Kelly) very likely ends up wealthier — and almost certainly *happier and still in business* — despite betting less aggressively.** The reasons cut deep: (1) **Estimation error** (Day 149/191) — neither trader knows their *true* edge precisely; they're sizing off an *estimate*. If they *over*-estimate their edge (common — overfitting/optimism), then "full Kelly" on the estimate is actually *over*-betting the true edge, pushing toward the steep negative-growth side of the curve (Block 4). Half-Kelly's margin of safety protects against this — it's robust to over-estimating the edge, while full-Kelly is dangerously exposed. (2) **Drawdowns and survival** — full Kelly produces *brutal* drawdowns (routinely halving capital); a string of losses (which *will* happen, Day 121) can trigger a catastrophic drawdown that forces Trader A to **stop trading** (margin calls, investor redemptions, or psychological capitulation — Day 178), *removing them from the game* before the edge can compound. Trader B's smoother ride keeps them *in business* to compound over the long run. **Survival is the precondition for growth.** (3) **The flat top of the Kelly curve** (Block 4) — half-Kelly captures **~75% of the growth with ~50% of the volatility**; the growth sacrificed is small (the parabola is flat near the peak) while the risk reduction is large. So Trader B gives up little expected growth for a much safer path. (4) **Compounding rewards consistency** — Trader A's larger drawdowns mean larger *recoveries* needed (a 50% loss requires a 100% gain to recover — Day 144), so volatility drag hurts A more. **The deeper lesson:** **aggression and long-run success are *not* the same thing — and are often opposed.** Long-run wealth comes from *staying in the game and compounding steadily*, which favors *conservative* sizing, not maximal aggression. The trader who bets less, survives the inevitable bad streaks, and compounds an edge over decades beats the one who bets maximally, suffers a catastrophic drawdown, and is forced out. This is why "bet less than Kelly" is one of the most important rules in trading, and why the *best* traders are frequently the most *conservative* sizers — they understand that **the goal is not to maximize this bet's return but to maximize long-run compound growth subject to survival**, and that goal is best served by humility about your edge and respect for ruin. Aggression *feels* optimal; survival *is* optimal.)

**Career connection:** "you have a strategy with an edge — how much do you bet?" Strong answer: I'd use the **Kelly criterion** as a *ceiling* — `f* = μ/σ²` (or `p − (1−p)/b`) gives the growth-optimal fraction that maximizes long-run *compound* (log) wealth — but I'd deliberately bet a **fraction of Kelly** (typically half- or quarter-Kelly) because: (1) I never know my true edge precisely, and **over-estimating the edge → over-betting → the long-run growth rate goes *negative*** even with a real edge (the curve `g(f)=fμ−½f²σ²` is zero at `2f*` and negative beyond); (2) **fractional Kelly captures most of the growth with far less volatility and drawdown** (half-Kelly ≈ 75% of growth, 50% of vol) and protects against estimation error; and (3) **survival is the precondition for compounding** — ruin is absorbing, and most blowups are good edges *over-bet*, not bad edges. The asymmetry — under-betting costs a little, over-betting can cost everything — means I always bias toward betting *less*. Framing sizing as *growth-optimal-but-bet-below-it-for-survival*, with the Kelly math and the estimation-error/fractional-Kelly reasoning, is the senior risk answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · The Kelly growth curve & fractional Kelly
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

mu, sigma = 0.08, 0.20                           # edge: 8% excess return, 20% vol
f_kelly = mu / sigma**2                           # growth-optimal leverage
print(f"full Kelly fraction f* = {f_kelly:.2f}")

# long-run growth rate as a function of bet fraction (the inverted parabola)
def growth_rate(f): return f*mu - 0.5*f**2*sigma**2
for f in [0, 0.5*f_kelly, f_kelly, 1.5*f_kelly, 2*f_kelly, 2.5*f_kelly]:
    print(f"  f={f:.2f} ({f/f_kelly:.1f}x Kelly): long-run growth g={growth_rate(f)*100:+.2f}%")

# simulate wealth paths: full Kelly vs half Kelly (note the drawdowns)
def simulate(frac, T=2000, paths=5000):
    r = mu/252 + sigma/np.sqrt(252)*rng.standard_normal((paths,T))
    wealth = np.cumprod(1 + frac*r, axis=1)
    return wealth[:,-1], (wealth/np.maximum.accumulate(wealth,axis=1)).min(axis=1)
for frac,label in [(f_kelly,"full Kelly"),(0.5*f_kelly,"half Kelly")]:
    final, maxdd = simulate(frac)
    print(f"{label:10s}: median final wealth {np.median(final):.2f}, median maxDD {np.median(maxdd)-1:.0%}")
```
The growth curve peaks at Kelly, hits *zero* at 2×Kelly, and goes *negative* beyond — and half-Kelly delivers nearly the same median wealth with far smaller drawdowns. The math of "bet less than Kelly," demonstrated.

**Linux:** `python kelly.py | tee kelly.log`; push the fraction past 2×Kelly and watch median wealth *collapse* despite the positive edge — over-betting ruin, live.

## Quant Thinking Track — Size for survival, respect the asymmetry
The enduring lesson: in any repeated, reinvested, ruin-possible game, **how much you commit matters as much as whether you're right — and because ruin is permanent while under-committing only mildly costs growth, you should bias toward caution.** Maximize long-run *compound* growth, not single-bet expectation; bet below the theoretical optimum to survive estimation error and bad streaks. This asymmetry — bounded downside of caution, unbounded downside of over-aggression — is a master principle for decisions far beyond trading.
