## Block 1 — The building blocks: AR and MA
Two ways a series can depend on its past:
- **Autoregressive (AR(p)):** today depends on its own recent *values* —
```
x_t = c + φ₁x_{t−1} + … + φ_p x_{t−p} + ε_t
```
(an AR(1) is the discrete OU/mean-reversion process, Days 168/173.)
- **Moving Average (MA(q)):** today depends on recent *shocks* —
```
x_t = c + ε_t + θ₁ε_{t−1} + … + θ_q ε_{t−q}
```
(a shock's effect lingers for `q` periods then vanishes.)

Combine them → **ARMA(p,q)**. Add **differencing** (the "I" = integrated, order `d`, Day 142) to handle non-stationary trends → **ARIMA(p,d,q)**. This single family captures trends, momentum, and mean reversion in one framework.

*Why it matters:* ARIMA is the lingua franca of classical forecasting — and the baseline every fancier model must beat.

---

## Block 2 — The Box–Jenkins method
A disciplined 4-step recipe for fitting ARIMA:
1. **Identify** — make the series stationary (difference until ADF passes, Day 142), then read the **ACF** and **PACF** plots to guess `p` and `q` (Block 3).
2. **Estimate** — fit the model (maximum likelihood, Day 122) to get the `φ` and `θ` coefficients.
3. **Diagnose** — check the **residuals** are white noise (no leftover autocorrelation — Ljung–Box test); if not, the model missed structure → revise.
4. **Forecast** — once residuals are clean, project forward with confidence intervals.

It's the scientific method for time series: hypothesize structure, fit, *check the residuals*, iterate. The residual check is the integrity step — same spirit as purged CV (Day 193) and diagnostics everywhere.

---

## Block 3 — Reading ACF and PACF (model identification)
The two diagnostic plots tell you the order:
- **ACF (autocorrelation function)** — correlation with lag `k` (Day 141).
- **PACF (partial ACF)** — correlation with lag `k` *after removing* the intermediate lags' effects.

The classic signatures:
| Pattern | ACF | PACF | Model |
|---|---|---|---|
| AR(p) | decays gradually | **cuts off after lag p** | AR(p) |
| MA(q) | **cuts off after lag q** | decays gradually | MA(q) |
| ARMA | decays | decays | mixed |

So a PACF that cuts off at lag 2 with a slowly-decaying ACF → AR(2). This pattern-reading is the craft of Box–Jenkins (modern practice also uses **AIC/BIC** to select orders automatically, penalizing complexity — Day 128 bias–variance).

**Concept check:** the PACF spikes at lag 1 then drops to zero; the ACF decays slowly. What model? (**AR(1)** — PACF cutting off at lag 1 = one autoregressive term; the gradual ACF decay confirms it. The φ₁ is the mean-reversion/momentum coefficient.)

## Block 4 — Mathematics · stationarity, invertibility, and forecast decay
The conditions that make ARIMA well-behaved:
- **Stationarity (AR side):** the AR polynomial's roots must lie outside the unit circle — equivalently `|φ| < 1` for AR(1). If `φ = 1` you have a **unit root** (random walk, Day 142) → must difference. This is *why* the "I" exists.
- **Invertibility (MA side):** an analogous root condition so the MA part has a stable AR(∞) representation (identifiable parameters).
- **Forecast behavior:** for a *stationary* ARMA, forecasts **decay to the unconditional mean** geometrically — an AR(1) forecast `h` steps out is `μ + φ^h(x_t − μ)`, reverting at rate `φ`. The forecast's information **dies exponentially** with horizon (half-life `ln2/(−ln φ)`, Day 173). For a *differenced* (unit-root) series, forecasts are flat (a random walk's best forecast of all future values is today's value — Day 141).

The deep consequence for finance: since **returns are ~white noise** (Day 141), an ARIMA on returns finds `φ, θ ≈ 0` — *no forecastable mean structure*. The forecast collapses to "tomorrow ≈ the mean." This isn't ARIMA failing; it's ARIMA *correctly reporting* that the mean of returns is nearly unpredictable (Day 150). The action is elsewhere — in the **variance** (GARCH, Day 202).

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · know what a model can and cannot capture
ARIMA crystallizes a vital discipline: **understand what structure your model is *capable* of representing, so you know when it's the wrong tool.** ARIMA models **linear dependence in the conditional mean.** That's powerful for many series (economic data, demand, some spreads) but largely *empty* for asset returns, because:
- **return means are ~unpredictable** (efficient markets, Day 150) → little linear mean structure to fit,
- the real, *forecastable* structure in returns is in the **variance** (volatility clustering, Day 143) — which ARIMA-on-returns *cannot see* (it models the mean, not the variance),
- and genuine market structure is often **nonlinear** (regimes, Day 204) and **non-stationary** (Day 142), beyond ARMA's linear-stationary assumptions.

So ARIMA's main value in finance is often as a *baseline and a diagnostic*: it confirms returns are mean-unpredictable (pushing you toward variance/vol models and cross-sectional signals), and it genuinely models *mean-reverting spreads* (stat arb, Day 171 — an AR(1) on the spread *is* the OU model) and *economic/fundamental* series used for nowcasting (Day 209). The meta-lesson — **match the model's representational capacity to the structure that actually exists** — is the same one from Day 196 (deep learning) and runs through all of modeling: a tool isn't good or bad in the abstract, only well- or ill-matched to the structure present. ARIMA is excellent where linear conditional-mean structure exists, and *correctly* near-useless where it doesn't (return means) — and knowing *which* is the skill.

**Hard puzzle:** you fit an ARIMA model to daily stock *returns*, carefully follow Box–Jenkins, and the best model by AIC is ARIMA(0,0,0) — i.e., white noise with a constant. Your colleague says "the model failed." Are they right? (No — **the model succeeded by telling you the truth.** ARIMA(0,0,0) = "returns are white noise around a constant mean" — *no* autoregressive or moving-average structure in the conditional mean. That's not a failure; it's ARIMA **correctly detecting that return means are unpredictable**, exactly as efficient-market theory predicts (Day 150). The "failure" interpretation comes from expecting ARIMA to find mean-predictability that *isn't there*. The right response is to **redirect**: (1) the forecastable structure is in the **variance** → fit a **GARCH** model on the residuals (Day 202) — you'll find strong structure in `ε²`; (2) if you want *mean* predictability, look **cross-sectionally** (Day 176) or in **spreads** (where an AR(1) *does* find mean reversion, Day 171), not in a single series' returns. ARIMA(0,0,0) on returns is a *finding*, not a flop — it's the time-series confirmation that you must model volatility and relative value, not the level of a single return series. Misreading a correct null result as model failure is a classic beginner error.)

**Career connection:** "would you use ARIMA to forecast stock returns?" Strong answer: ARIMA models **linear conditional-mean** structure, which is largely **absent in asset returns** (they're ~white noise — efficient markets), so an ARIMA on returns typically finds near-zero coefficients — a *correct* result, not a failure, that redirects you to model the **variance (GARCH)** instead, or to look for mean structure in **spreads** (where AR(1) = OU mean reversion) and **cross-sectionally**. ARIMA *is* valuable for mean-reverting spreads, economic/fundamental series, and as a baseline/diagnostic. Knowing *what structure ARIMA can and can't capture* — and that an ARIMA(0,0,0) on returns is informative — signals real time-series maturity.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Fit ARIMA; see it work on a spread, fail on returns
```python
import numpy as np
from statsmodels.tsa.arima.model import ARIMA
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# (1) a mean-reverting SPREAD (AR(1)) — ARIMA SHOULD find structure
spread = np.zeros(1000)
for t in range(1,1000): spread[t] = 0.85*spread[t-1] + rng.standard_normal()
m1 = ARIMA(spread, order=(1,0,0)).fit()
print(f"SPREAD  AR(1) φ = {m1.arparams[0]:.3f}  (≈0.85 — real mean reversion found)")

# (2) stock RETURNS (white noise) — ARIMA should find ~nothing
ret = rng.standard_normal(1000)*0.01
m2 = ARIMA(ret, order=(1,0,0)).fit()
print(f"RETURNS AR(1) φ = {m2.arparams[0]:.3f}  (≈0 — no mean structure, as expected)")

# forecast: spread reverts toward mean; returns forecast ≈ constant
print(f"spread 5-step forecast decays toward mean: {m1.forecast(5).round(3)}")
print(f"returns 5-step forecast ≈ flat constant   : {m2.forecast(5).round(5)}")
```
ARIMA *finds* the AR(1) mean reversion in the spread (φ≈0.85) and *correctly finds nothing* in returns (φ≈0) — the model working as a truth-teller, and the spread forecast visibly reverting while the return forecast is flat.

## Quant Thinking Track — A model's silence is information
When a well-specified model finds "no structure," that's a *result*, not a defeat — it tells you where *not* to look and redirects you to where the structure actually lives. ARIMA's near-zero coefficients on returns are a clean signal: stop hunting mean-predictability in single-series returns, go model variance and relative value. Reading a model's null findings as informative — rather than as failure to be tuned away — is a mark of analytical maturity.
