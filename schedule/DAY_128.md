## Block 1 — The decomposition
Expected prediction error splits into three parts:
```
Error = Bias²  +  Variance  +  Irreducible noise
```
- **Bias:** error from wrong assumptions / too-simple model (underfitting).
- **Variance:** error from sensitivity to the particular training sample (overfitting).
- **Irreducible:** noise you can never remove (huge in markets).

*Why it matters:* you can't minimise both bias and variance at once — the art is the **tradeoff**, and the right point depends on how noisy your data is.

---

## Block 2 — The U-shaped curve
As model complexity rises: **bias falls, variance rises.** Total error is U-shaped — minimised at *moderate* complexity, not maximum.
- Too simple (high bias): a constant predicting the mean return.
- Too complex (high variance): a 50-parameter signal fit to 200 days.

**Concept check:** which way does *more data* shift the sweet spot? (Right — more data tames variance, so you can afford more complexity.)

---

## Block 3 — Why finance lives on the "simple" side
Markets have a **dominant irreducible-noise term** (low signal-to-noise). When noise dominates, **variance is the enemy**, so the bias-variance optimum sits at *low complexity*: linear models, few features, heavy regularization.

This is why, famously, **simple linear factor models often beat fancy ML** in live trading — the complex model mostly fits noise (variance), while the simple one captures the small real signal with low variance.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics (regularization = the dial)
Ridge's `λ` *is* the bias-variance knob: `λ=0` → low bias/high variance (OLS, can overfit); large `λ` → high bias/low variance (shrunk, stable). Cross-validate to find the `λ` minimising out-of-sample error.

**Exercise:** you add 30 features to a 1-factor model and out-of-sample Sharpe *drops.* What happened in bias-variance terms? (Variance shot up faster than bias fell — net worse generalization.)

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking (Occam's razor pays)

- **Prefer the simplest model** that captures the signal — it generalises and is debuggable.
- **Ensembling** (averaging many models) reduces variance without raising bias much — why random forests / model averaging are popular (and why averaging *signals* helps).
- **Shrinkage everywhere:** shrink means, covariances, and coefficients toward simple priors (Day 122/123) — bias-variance applied to estimation.

**Hard puzzle:** model A (linear, 3 features): IS Sharpe 1.2, OOS 1.0. Model B (deep net, 200 features): IS Sharpe 4.0, OOS 0.3. Which do you trade, and what does the gap reveal? (A — B's huge IS/OOS gap is classic high-variance overfit; A generalises.)

**Career connection:** "why might a simpler model outperform a complex one in trading?" → bias-variance + low signal-to-noise. A crisp answer signals you understand *why* most production quant models are deliberately simple.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · The U-curve
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
def oos_error(deg, n=40):
    x=np.sort(rng.random(n)); y=np.sin(2*np.pi*x)+rng.normal(0,0.4,n)
    c=np.polyfit(x,y,deg)
    xt=np.linspace(0,1,500); yt=np.sin(2*np.pi*xt)
    return np.mean((np.polyval(c,xt)-yt)**2)
for d in [1,3,5,9,15]:
    print(d, round(np.mean([oos_error(d) for _ in range(200)]),3))  # U-shape
```

## Quant Thinking Track — Complexity Must Earn Its Keep
Every added parameter must reduce *out-of-sample* error more than it adds variance. In high-noise domains like markets, the bar is brutal — which is why discipline favours simplicity.
