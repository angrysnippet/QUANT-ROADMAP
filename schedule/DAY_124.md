## Block 1 — Correlation ≠ causation
`Cov(X,Y) ≠ 0` says they *move together*, not that `X` causes `Y`. Three ways to get correlation:
1. **X causes Y** (real).
2. **Y causes X** (reverse).
3. **A confounder Z** drives both (spurious).
…plus pure **coincidence** (especially with enough variables tested).

*Why it matters:* a correlated "signal" only makes money if the relationship is *stable and exploitable* — coincidental and confounded correlations decay instantly.

---

## Block 2 — Spurious correlation
With enough series, some will correlate by chance (the "ice cream sales ↔ drownings" classic — confounder: summer heat). In markets, with thousands of candidate signals, **you will find** spuriously "predictive" ones. High correlation in a backtest is *necessary but nowhere near sufficient.*

**Concept check:** you test 1,000 random signals against returns. How many show `|correlation|` significant at 5% *by pure chance*? (~50 — the multiple-testing problem, Day 125.)

---

## Block 3 — Confounders & controls
A confounder `Z` influences both `X` and `Y`, faking a direct link. The fix is to **control for Z** (e.g. via multiple regression, or by neutralising known factors). In equity alpha research you routinely **neutralise** market/sector/size/momentum exposure to check whether a signal has *residual* predictive power or is just a repackaged known factor.

---

## Block 4 — Lookahead bias & data leakage (the silent killers)
The most dangerous "causation" illusion in quant isn't philosophical — it's a **bug**:
- **Lookahead bias:** using information not available at decision time (e.g. today's close to trade today's open).
- **Leakage:** a feature secretly contains the target (e.g. using revised/restated fundamentals, or future-adjusted prices).

These produce gorgeous backtests that **cannot be traded.** Suspiciously high Sharpe? Hunt for leakage first.

**Exercise:** list three ways a feature could "see the future" in a backtest. (Restated data, survivorship-cleaned universes, using close-to-compute-signal-then-trade-at-that-same-close.)

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking (Tradeable = causal-ish + stable)

- **Economic rationale first:** a signal you can *explain* (a real mechanism — risk premium, liquidity, behavioral bias) is far more likely to persist than one you merely *found.*
- **Out-of-sample & regime tests:** does it hold on data you didn't fit, and across different market regimes? Correlation that's stable across time/markets is more likely structural.
- **Causation you can't prove, but robustness you can demand.**

**Hard puzzle:** a signal correlates 0.3 with next-day returns in-sample, 0.0 out-of-sample. What happened, and what's the lesson? (Overfit/spurious — in-sample correlation is cheap; only out-of-sample stability counts. Always hold out data.)

**Career connection:** "why do you believe this signal will keep working?" is *the* alpha-research interview question. A strong answer cites a **causal mechanism + out-of-sample robustness**, not just historical correlation.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Manufacture a spurious correlation
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
# Two independent random walks often look correlated by eye
x = rng.normal(0,1,250).cumsum(); y = rng.normal(0,1,250).cumsum()
print(np.corrcoef(x, y)[0,1])     # frequently large in magnitude despite independence!
# Lesson: correlate *returns* (stationary), not *levels* (trending)
print(np.corrcoef(np.diff(x), np.diff(y))[0,1])  # ~0, as it should be
```
This demonstrates a real trap: correlating non-stationary *levels* fakes relationships (Day 132's stationarity preview).

**Linux:** `paste a.txt b.txt | awk '...'` to eyeball two series before trusting a correlation.

## Quant Thinking Track — Demand a Mechanism
Before trading any correlation, ask: *what is the causal story, and would it survive being widely known?* No mechanism → treat it as coincidence until proven otherwise out-of-sample.
