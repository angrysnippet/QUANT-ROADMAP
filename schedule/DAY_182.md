## Block 1 — The market maker's business
A **market maker (MM)** continuously posts a bid *and* an ask, standing ready to trade with anyone. If buys and sells arrive symmetrically, the MM **earns the spread** on each round trip — buy at the bid, sell at the ask, pocket the difference. Do this across thousands of trades a day and the small per-trade spread compounds into real revenue. The MM is the *supplier of immediacy* — they exist so that you don't have to wait for a natural counterparty.

*Why it matters:* market making is one of the largest, most systematic quant businesses (Citadel Securities, Jane Street, Virtu). And its core problem — quote to earn the spread while controlling the risk you accumulate — is a clean optimal-control problem that ties together everything from inventory to adverse selection.

---

## Block 2 — Inventory risk: the central problem
Order flow is *not* symmetric. If sellers dominate for a while, the MM keeps buying → accumulates a **long inventory** → now bears **price risk**: if the stock drops, the inventory loss can dwarf the spread earned. The MM's nightmare is **getting "run over"** — accumulating a large position right before an adverse move. So the real objective is two-sided:
```
maximize:  spread income
subject to:  controlling inventory (price) risk
```
This is a **risk–reward trade-off** (Day 144) at high frequency: quote tightly/aggressively to capture more flow (more spread income) but accumulate more inventory risk; quote wide/passively for less flow but safer inventory. The MM is *not* trying to predict direction — they're trying to **earn the spread while staying close to flat.**

---

## Block 3 — Quote skewing: steering your inventory
The key lever: **skew your quotes to lean against your inventory.** If you're already *long* (accumulated inventory you want to offload):
- **lower both quotes** — make your ask more attractive (sell faster) and your bid less attractive (buy less),
- this biases incoming flow toward *reducing* your position back to neutral.

Symmetrically, when short, raise both quotes to attract buyers of *your* offer less and hit *your* bid more. You're using price to **steer order flow toward flat inventory** — a feedback controller. The fair value (reservation price) you quote around isn't the mid; it's the mid *adjusted for your inventory and risk aversion* (Block 4). This is how MMs avoid getting run over: they continuously nudge themselves back toward neutral.

**Concept check:** you're a MM holding a large long inventory and risk-averse. Do you quote symmetrically around the mid? (No — you skew *down*: tighten/raise the ask to sell your excess, widen/lower the bid to stop accumulating more. You sacrifice some spread to reduce inventory risk — the trade-off in action.)

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the Avellaneda–Stoikov model
The canonical quantitative market-making model (Avellaneda–Stoikov, 2008) makes the intuition precise via stochastic optimal control (Day 138's MDP/control, at high frequency). Two key outputs:
- **Reservation (indifference) price** — your personal fair value, *shifted from the mid by your inventory*:
```
r = s − q·γ·σ²·(T−t)
```
where `s` = mid, `q` = inventory, `γ` = risk aversion, `σ` = volatility, `(T−t)` = time left. Long inventory (`q>0`) pushes your reservation price *below* the mid → you quote lower → you sell to flatten. **Inventory literally moves your fair value.**
- **Optimal spread** — how wide to quote around `r`, balancing fill rate (more flow) against inventory/adverse-selection risk:
```
spread ≈ γσ²(T−t) + (2/γ)·ln(1 + γ/κ)
```
wider when volatility `σ` is high, when you're more risk-averse `γ`, and when order flow is thin (small `κ`). The model *derives* both the **skew** (via `r`) and the **width** (via the spread) from first principles — turning "lean against inventory and widen in volatility" into equations you can run.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · market making is optimal control of a risk you create by trading
Market making crystallizes a beautiful idea: **you don't take a view; you provide a service (immediacy) and manage the risk that providing it forces upon you.** The conceptual stack:
- it's a **stochastic control problem** (Day 138): state = inventory + time + book, controls = your two quotes, objective = spread income minus inventory-risk penalty,
- the **inventory penalty is just risk aversion** (Day 144) — `γσ²` is the same risk-cost that prices everything else,
- **quote skewing is a feedback controller** — the MM is a thermostat continuously steering inventory toward zero,
- and the *true* enemy isn't symmetric inventory but **asymmetric, informed flow** (adverse selection, Day 183) — the case where the person hitting your quote *knows the price is about to move against you.*

This reframes liquidity provision as **the optimal management of a self-inflicted risk**: every trade that earns you spread also hands you inventory, and the art is keeping the second from eating the first. It's risk–reward, optimal control, and feedback — three pillars of the curriculum — fused into one high-frequency business.

**Hard puzzle:** a market maker earns the spread on every round trip and "never takes a directional view." How can such a business lose money — sometimes catastrophically? (Two ways, both about **inventory becoming directional against you**: (1) **Inventory risk** — order flow isn't symmetric, so during a one-sided move the MM keeps *accumulating the losing side* (buying as the price falls), and the price loss on the growing inventory dwarfs the spread income — getting "run over." (2) **Adverse selection** (Day 183) — the counterparties hitting your quotes are sometimes *informed*: they buy from you right before good news, sell to you right before bad news, so your inventory is *systematically* on the wrong side. The MM doesn't take a view, but *informed flow forces a losing view onto their inventory.* The defense — quote skewing, widening in volatility, and backing off when flow looks toxic — is exactly the Avellaneda–Stoikov control. A "viewless" business is still ruined by inventory it didn't choose.)

**Career connection:** "how does a market maker make money, and what's the main risk?" Strong answer: they quote both sides and **earn the spread** across huge volume, *without* a directional view; the main risk is **inventory risk** — asymmetric flow leaves them holding a position that moves against them — managed by **skewing quotes against inventory** (lean toward flat) and **widening in volatility**, formalized by the Avellaneda–Stoikov reservation price `r = s − qγσ²(T−t)` and optimal spread. Mentioning inventory-driven quote skewing and the AS model is the standout answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Avellaneda–Stoikov quotes & an inventory simulation
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

def as_quotes(mid, q, gamma, sigma, t_left, kappa):
    r = mid - q*gamma*sigma**2*t_left                 # reservation price (inventory-shifted)
    half = 0.5*(gamma*sigma**2*t_left + (2/gamma)*np.log(1+gamma/kappa))
    return r-half, r+half                             # bid, ask

# simulate a session: random flow, MM skews to control inventory
S, q, cash = 100.0, 0, 0.0
gamma, sigma, kappa = 0.1, 0.4, 1.5
N=2000; T=1.0
inv=[]
for i in range(N):
    t_left=max(T*(1-i/N),1e-3)
    bid,ask = as_quotes(S, q, gamma, sigma, t_left, kappa)
    S += sigma*np.sqrt(T/N)*rng.standard_normal()     # mid drifts
    # fill probability falls the further your quote is from mid
    if rng.random() < np.exp(-kappa*max(ask-S,0)): q-=1; cash+=ask   # someone buys our ask
    if rng.random() < np.exp(-kappa*max(S-bid,0)): q+=1; cash-=bid   # someone hits our bid
    inv.append(q)

pnl = cash + q*S                                       # mark inventory to market
print(f"final inventory: {q}   |  max |inventory|: {max(abs(np.array(inv)))}")
print(f"session P&L (spread income − inventory risk): {pnl:.2f}")
print(f"inventory stayed bounded? mean |q| = {np.mean(np.abs(inv)):.1f}  <- skew keeps it near flat")
```
The reservation-price skew keeps inventory *bounded* around zero while the MM harvests the spread — the controller steering itself back to flat, in code.

**Linux:** `python mm.py | tee mm.log`; raise `sigma` and watch quotes widen and inventory tighten — risk aversion responding to volatility.

## Quant Thinking Track — Provide a service, manage the byproduct
Market making teaches a general business pattern: **you get paid for providing something scarce (immediacy), and the discipline is managing the byproduct (inventory/risk) the service generates.** The same shape recurs in insurance (premium income, manage claims risk), lending (interest income, manage default risk), and vol selling (Day 159). Seeing "earn a premium, control the byproduct risk" as a repeatable structure lets you analyze whole categories of business at once.
