## Block 1 — The alternative-data universe
**Alternative data** = anything beyond traditional price/volume/fundamentals that might predict. The major categories:
- **Text:** news, earnings-call transcripts, SEC filings (10-Ks, 8-Ks), analyst reports, social media, Fed statements.
- **Satellite/geospatial:** parking-lot car counts (retail traffic), oil-tank shadows (storage levels), crop imagery, shipping.
- **Transaction:** anonymized credit-card spending (revenue nowcasting), app downloads, web traffic.
- **Sensor/IoT, supply-chain, weather, ESG**, and more.

The thesis: this data carries information about fundamentals **before** it shows up in reported numbers or prices. Count the cars in Walmart's lots and you can *nowcast* quarterly revenue weeks before the earnings release — an informational edge (Day 149) until everyone has it.

*Why it matters:* as price-based signals get crowded and decay, **data sourcing and processing become the new battleground for alpha.** The edge is increasingly in *what you can see*, not just *how you model it*.

---

## Block 2 — NLP: turning text into signal
Natural Language Processing extracts structured signal from text — the largest, richest alt-data category:
- **Sentiment analysis:** is this news/filing/tweet positive or negative for the asset? (classic, now table-stakes).
- **Event extraction:** detect M&A, guidance changes, lawsuits, management departures from filings/news.
- **Tone & complexity:** linguistic changes in earnings calls (hedging language, uncertainty words, readability of 10-Ks) predict future performance — managers reveal information in *how* they speak, not just what they report.
- **Novelty/similarity:** how much a new filing *differs* from prior ones (big changes are informative).

The evolution: bag-of-words/lexicons → word embeddings → **transformers (BERT/FinBERT)** → **large language models (LLMs)** that read and summarize filings, extract structured data, and answer questions about documents at scale. This is exactly where DL (Day 196) has a *decisive* edge over trees — representation learning on language is transformative.

---

## Block 3 — LLMs for finance (the current frontier)
Large language models are reshaping financial NLP:
- **document understanding** — read a 200-page 10-K and extract risk factors, sentiment, and structured facts,
- **summarization & Q&A** over filings, news, and transcripts at scale,
- **signal generation** — classify sentiment/events with far more nuance than lexicons,
- **research augmentation** — accelerate analysts, surface relevant documents.

Cautions specific to finance: **hallucination** (an LLM confidently inventing facts is *dangerous* with money on the line — verify outputs), **lookahead/training-cutoff leakage** (an LLM may "know" future events from its training data — a subtle but lethal backtest contaminant unique to LLMs, Day 141), **cost/latency**, and **non-determinism**. LLMs are powerful *processors* of unstructured data, but their outputs are signals to be **validated like any other** (purged CV, Day 193), not trusted blindly.

**Concept check:** you backtest an LLM-based news-sentiment signal over 2015–2020 using a model trained on data through 2023. What's the subtle, fatal flaw? (**Lookahead leakage via the training cutoff** — the LLM was trained on data through 2023, so when "reading" a 2017 news article it may *already know how the story turned out* (the company's later fate, the stock's move). Its 2017 "sentiment" is contaminated by future knowledge → the backtest is inflated and unrepeatable live. You must use **point-in-time models** (trained only on pre-period data) or carefully constructed evaluations. This LLM-specific leakage is invisible and devastating — a new twist on the oldest sin, Day 141.)

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the data alpha lifecycle and its decay
Alternative-data alpha follows a brutal lifecycle governed by *adoption*:
- **early adopters** capture large edge (few have the data, signal is strong, uncrowded),
- as **data vendors commercialize** it and more funds buy it, the edge **decays** (Day 149) — the same information in many hands gets arbitraged into prices,
- eventually it becomes **table-stakes** — necessary to avoid being *disadvantaged*, but no longer a source of *excess* return (everyone nowcasts revenue now).

The economics: **the first mover on a dataset earns the alpha; latecomers pay vendors for a commoditized signal.** This creates a relentless *arms race for novel data* — sourcing exclusive/raw data, processing it faster/better, and finding it *before* competitors. Quantitatively, the edge `∝ 1/(number of users)` roughly, and decays on a timescale set by how fast the data commoditizes. Other hard realities: alt-data is **expensive, messy, and short-history** (satellite data may have only a few years — tiny samples, Day 191), riddled with **survivorship/coverage biases** (Day 123), and **legally/ethically fraught** (privacy, MNPI/material-non-public-information — using improperly-sourced data can be *illegal*, e.g. expert-network insider-trading cases). Processing alt-data is as much a **data-engineering, legal, and sourcing** problem as a modeling one.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the edge is moving from modeling to data
The strategic insight of this day: **as modeling commoditizes, competitive advantage shifts upstream to data.** Everyone has `xgboost` and the same price history (Day 191), so the model is no longer the differentiator — *what you can see that others can't* increasingly is. This reshapes where quant value is created:
- **data sourcing** — securing exclusive, raw, or novel datasets *first* (the new moat),
- **data engineering** — cleaning, aligning, point-in-time-stamping messy unstructured data at scale (the unglamorous 80%, again — Day 192),
- **processing edge** — extracting signal from text/images *better or faster* than competitors (where DL/LLMs pay off, Day 196),
- **speed of adoption** — capturing the alpha *before* the dataset commoditizes (Day 149's decay clock is ticking from day one).

This is why modern quant funds employ armies of **data engineers, alternative-data scouts, and NLP/ML researchers**, and spend fortunes on data acquisition — the alpha has migrated from "who has the cleverest model" toward "who has the best data, processed best, first." It connects the whole arc: markets are near-efficient (Day 150), so edges are faint and decaying (Day 149); the durable response is to **continuously find new information** (alt-data) and **process it with the right tools** (NLP/DL where they shine, Day 196) under **honest validation** (Day 193) — all while racing the decay clock. The frontier of alpha is unstructured, expensive, legally-careful, and upstream of the model. **In a world where everyone can model, the winners are those who can *see*.**

**Hard puzzle:** your fund can buy a satellite dataset of retail-store parking-lot traffic for \$2M/year that nowcasts quarterly revenue. The vendor has sold it to ~30 other funds. Is it worth buying, and how do you think about it? (Probably **not as a source of alpha — but possibly as table-stakes defense.** The core issue is **crowding/decay** (Days 149, 178): with ~30 funds already using it, the signal is **largely arbitraged into prices** before earnings — you'd be a *latecomer* paying for a *commoditized* dataset whose edge has mostly decayed. The alpha went to the *early* adopters. Quantify: the edge roughly scales `∝ 1/users`, and with 30+ users the residual excess return likely won't clear \$2M + processing costs + the **capacity** limits (Day 179) of a crowded signal. **However**, two nuances: (1) it might be worth it *defensively* — if competitors trade on it, *not* having it leaves you systematically disadvantaged (table-stakes), and (2) you might extract *residual* edge if you process it **better** than others (finer geographic resolution, combining it with *other* data for an orthogonal signal — Day 177, where the *marginal/combination* value matters more than the standalone). The decision: buy it only if (a) it's defensively necessary, or (b) you have a *processing or combination edge* that the other 30 funds lack — not for the raw, decayed standalone signal. And always check the **legal/MNPI** status of the sourcing. The deeper lesson: *exclusive or novel data is the moat; commoditized data is just a cost* — the same crowding logic as the Quant Quake (Day 178), now about data instead of positions.)

**Career connection:** "where do you think alpha comes from today?" Strong answer: increasingly from **alternative data and the ability to process it**, not from modeling — because price signals are crowded and decayed (Day 149) and everyone has the same models, so the edge moves *upstream* to **what you can see**: news/filings/transcripts via **NLP and LLMs** (sentiment, event extraction, tone), satellite/transaction/web data for **nowcasting fundamentals** before they're reported. The catch is that data alpha **decays as it commoditizes** (the first mover earns it), alt-data is **messy, short-history, expensive, and legally fraught** (MNPI), and even LLMs introduce **training-cutoff lookahead leakage**, so it all needs honest validation and a sourcing/processing edge. Framing alpha as having migrated from *modeling to data* — with the decay, legal, and leakage caveats — is a sophisticated, current answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · A simple NLP sentiment signal
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# toy lexicon sentiment -> trade signal (real systems use FinBERT/LLMs, but the pipeline is the same)
pos = {"beat","strong","growth","raise","record","upgrade","surge"}
neg = {"miss","weak","decline","cut","loss","downgrade","probe","fraud"}

def sentiment(text):
    w = text.lower().split()
    s = sum(t in pos for t in w) - sum(t in neg for t in w)
    return s/max(len(w),1)                                  # normalized polarity

headlines = [
    ("AcmeCorp beats estimates, raises guidance on record growth",  +0.012),  # next-day ret
    ("AcmeCorp misses, warns of weak demand and guidance cut",       -0.018),
    ("AcmeCorp announces upgrade and strong subscriber surge",       +0.009),
    ("AcmeCorp under probe; reports loss and downgrade",             -0.022),
    ("AcmeCorp holds steady amid mixed quarter",                     +0.001),
]
sigs = np.array([sentiment(h) for h,_ in headlines])
rets = np.array([r for _,r in headlines])
print("sentiment scores:", sigs.round(3))
print(f"corr(sentiment, next-day return) = {np.corrcoef(sigs, rets)[0,1]:.2f}")
print("signal -> position: long positive-sentiment, short negative")
```
Even a crude lexicon extracts a directional signal from text; production systems swap in **FinBERT/LLMs** for nuance, but the pipeline — text → score → position, then **validate with purged CV** — is identical. The edge is in the *data and processing*, not the trade logic.

**Linux:** `python nlp.py | tee nlp.log`; the real work is sourcing point-in-time news with accurate timestamps (no lookahead) and scaling the processing — data engineering, not the model.

## Quant Thinking Track — Compete where others can't
When a capability commoditizes (modeling), competitive advantage migrates to whatever is *still scarce* (data, sourcing, processing, speed). The strategic thinker constantly asks "where is the *scarce* resource now?" and moves upstream toward it. In quant finance that's increasingly novel data and the engineering to exploit it first — a reminder that durable edge comes from doing what others *can't*, not what everyone *can*.
