## Block 1 — Greeks = partial derivatives of the price
An option price `V(S, t, σ, r)` depends on several inputs. Each Greek is a **sensitivity** — a partial derivative — that answers "if *this* input moves a little, how much does `V` move?"
```
Delta Δ = ∂V/∂S        Gamma Γ = ∂²V/∂S²
Theta Θ = ∂V/∂t        Vega ν  = ∂V/∂σ        Rho ρ = ∂V/∂r
```
Risk management = knowing your Greeks and neutralizing the ones you don't want. **You don't trade an option; you trade its Greeks.**

---

## Block 2 — Delta and Gamma (the price Greeks)
- **Delta (Δ):** change in option value per \$1 move in `S`. Call Δ ∈ [0,1], put Δ ∈ [−1,0]. Δ = `N(d₁)` for a call. Interpretations: hedge ratio (shares to hold), *and* ≈ risk-neutral probability of finishing ITM. ATM ≈ 0.5.
- **Gamma (Γ):** change in **delta** per \$1 move — the *curvature* (convexity, Day 151). High Γ means delta shifts fast, so a static hedge decays quickly. Γ is largest **ATM and near expiry**. **Long options = long gamma = long convexity:** you profit from big moves either way.

**Concept check:** you're long a call with Δ=0.5, Γ=0.04. Stock jumps +\$2. New delta? (≈ `0.5 + 0.04·2 = 0.58` — gamma makes you *more* long as the stock rises, the convexity that makes long options pleasant in trends.)

---

## Block 3 — Theta and Vega (the time and vol Greeks)
- **Theta (Θ):** value lost per day as expiry approaches — **time decay**. Long options have negative theta (you bleed daily); it's the *rent you pay for gamma/convexity*. Largest ATM near expiry. **The fundamental trade-off:** long gamma ⇔ negative theta. You pay theta to own convexity; you collect theta by being short convexity (and bearing the gap risk).
- **Vega (ν):** value change per 1-point change in **implied volatility**. Always positive for long options (more vol = more value, Day 151). Largest for long-dated ATM options. Vega is how you trade *volatility itself* (Day 159) without a directional view.

**Rho (ρ):** sensitivity to interest rates — usually the least important for short-dated equity options, more relevant for long-dated and rates products.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the Greeks satisfy the BS PDE
Rewrite the Black–Scholes PDE (Day 153) entirely in Greeks:
```
Θ + ½σ²S²·Γ + rS·Δ − rV = 0
```
Read it as a **balance equation**: theta + gamma-times-vol balance against the financing terms. For a **delta-hedged** position (Δ-neutral, so `rS·Δ` and `−rV` net to the carry), it collapses to the trader's mantra:
```
Θ ≈ −½σ²S²·Γ
```
**Theta and gamma are two sides of one coin.** Long gamma ⟹ negative theta of equal-and-opposite expected size. This single identity *is* the economics of options market-making (Day 155): you earn gamma from realized moves and pay it back as theta — net P&L = the gap between **realized** and **implied** vol.

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the position is a vector of Greeks
A senior options trader doesn't think "I own 100 calls." They think: "I'm **+\$50k delta, +\$8k gamma, −\$3k/day theta, +\$12k vega**." The whole book is a vector of exposures, and the job is to *shape* that vector — hedge unwanted Greeks, keep the ones you have an edge in. Want pure vol exposure? Zero out delta (Day 155) → you're left long vega/gamma, short theta = a clean bet that *realized vol > implied vol*. **Greeks turn a messy portfolio of contracts into a handful of intuitive risk numbers you can hedge independently** — the same decomposition discipline you've used all along, now applied to risk.

**Hard puzzle:** you're long a straddle (call + put, same strike) and *delta-hedged*. The stock doesn't move all week. Do you make or lose money, and which Greek explains it? (You **lose** — negative **theta**. Long gamma is worthless if nothing moves; you paid time decay for convexity that never got used. You profit only if *realized* moves exceed what the implied vol you paid priced in. This is the realized-vs-implied vol trade, Day 159, stated in Greeks.)

**Career connection:** "you're long an ATM call — what are your risks?" Strong answer enumerates the Greeks: "positive delta (directional), positive gamma (I like big moves), **negative theta** (I bleed if it sits still), positive vega (I want IV to rise), small rho. Net: I'm long convexity and short time." Speaking fluently in Greeks is *the* options-desk shibboleth.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Compute all the Greeks
```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import norm

def greeks(S, K, r, sigma, T, kind="call"):
    d1 = (np.log(S/K) + (r+0.5*sigma**2)*T)/(sigma*np.sqrt(T))
    d2 = d1 - sigma*np.sqrt(T)
    pdf = norm.pdf(d1)
    delta = norm.cdf(d1) if kind=="call" else norm.cdf(d1)-1
    gamma = pdf/(S*sigma*np.sqrt(T))
    vega  = S*pdf*np.sqrt(T) / 100                      # per 1 vol-point
    theta = (-S*pdf*sigma/(2*np.sqrt(T))
             - (r*K*np.exp(-r*T)*norm.cdf(d2 if kind=="call" else -d2)
                * (1 if kind=="call" else -1))) / 365   # per day
    return dict(delta=round(delta,3), gamma=round(gamma,4),
                vega=round(vega,3), theta=round(theta,3))

S,K,r,sig,T = 100,100,0.05,0.2,1.0
print("ATM call:", greeks(S,K,r,sig,T))

# verify Theta ≈ -½σ²S²Γ for a delta-hedged book (the PDE identity)
g = greeks(S,K,r,sig,T)
print("−½σ²S²Γ (per yr):", round(-0.5*sig**2*S**2*g['gamma'],2),
      " vs theta·365:", round(g['theta']*365,2))
```
The theta/gamma identity holds (up to the rate/carry terms) — the PDE made tangible. Bump each input by hand and confirm the Greeks predict the price change.

**Linux:** `python greeks.py | tee greeks.log`; tabulate Greeks across strikes/expiries to *see* gamma and theta spike ATM near expiry.
