## Block 1 — The term structure of interest rates
Plot yield against maturity → the **yield curve**. Each point is the rate for borrowing/lending over that horizon. Common shapes and what they signal:
- **Upward (normal):** long rates > short rates — the usual state; investors demand a term premium for tying up money longer.
- **Flat:** uncertainty/transition.
- **Inverted:** short > long — historically a powerful **recession predictor** (the market expects the central bank to *cut* rates in a downturn).
- **Humped/steepening/flattening:** the curve moves in ways beyond parallel shifts — which is exactly why single-duration hedging (Day 162) is incomplete.

*Why it matters:* the curve *is* the discounting machine. To value *any* cash flow at time `t`, you need the rate for `t` — which you read off the curve.

---

## Block 2 — Spot rates vs forward rates
- **Spot rate `z(t)`** — the yield on a *zero-coupon* bond maturing at `t`; the rate for money invested *today* through `t`. The spot curve gives discount factors directly: `DF(t) = e^{−z(t)·t}`.
- **Forward rate `f(t₁,t₂)`** — the rate, agreed *today*, for borrowing/lending over a *future* window `[t₁, t₂]`. It's *implied* by no-arbitrage from the spots:
```
(1+z₂)^{t₂} = (1+z₁)^{t₁} · (1 + f(t₁,t₂))^{t₂−t₁}
```
Investing to `t₂` directly must equal investing to `t₁` then rolling at the forward — or there's arbitrage (Day 152, again). **The forward is the curve's prediction of (risk-neutral) future short rates**, and it's what every rates derivative is priced off.

**Concept check:** 1-year spot = 3%, 2-year spot = 4%. The 1y-into-1y forward? (`(1.04)²/(1.03) − 1 ≈ 5.0%`. An upward-sloping spot curve implies forwards *above* the spot — the market pricing rising future rates.)

---

## Block 3 — Bootstrapping the curve
Market quotes give you coupon-bond prices and swap rates, not clean zeros. **Bootstrapping** extracts the spot/discount curve sequentially:
1. The shortest instrument (e.g. 6-month bill) → solve directly for `DF(0.5)`.
2. The 1-year bond uses `DF(0.5)` (known) and its price to solve for `DF(1.0)`.
3. Each longer instrument peels off one new discount factor using all the shorter ones already solved.

It's a forward-substitution (the same triangular-solve idea from linear algebra) that turns observable prices into the underlying discount-factor curve. Modern desks then **interpolate** (splines) and fit a smooth, arbitrage-free curve used to discount *everything*.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the curve *is* the set of discount factors
Everything ties together: the yield curve, spot rates, forward rates, and discount factors are **four views of one object**:
```
DF(t) = e^{−z(t)·t}                          (spot → discount factor)
f(t,t+Δ) = −[ln DF(t+Δ) − ln DF(t)]/Δ        (instantaneous forward = −d/dt ln DF)
z(t) = (1/t)∫₀ᵗ f(s) ds                        (spot = average of forwards)
```
The **instantaneous forward rate is the negative slope of the log-discount curve**, and the **spot rate is the running average of the forwards.** This is why an *upward-sloping* spot curve always has forwards *above* spots (an average below its latest values means the latest values are rising). Master this trio and the curve stops being a mysterious squiggle and becomes a precise, differentiable function you can manipulate.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the curve prices the whole world
Step back at the scale of this object: **the discount curve underlies the valuation of every fixed cash flow on earth** — bonds, swaps, mortgages, pension liabilities, the discounting leg of *every* derivative (including the `r` in Black–Scholes, which is really "read off the curve at expiry"). Two consequences:
- **Curve construction is foundational infrastructure** — get the curve wrong and *everything* downstream is mispriced. (Post-2008, the industry even split into *multiple* curves — separate discounting (OIS/SOFR) vs forecasting curves — after the crisis broke the old single-curve assumption.)
- **Forwards are tradeable forecasts:** "carry and roll-down" strategies harvest the difference between forwards and *realized* future rates — if the curve is upward-sloping and rates *don't* rise as much as the forwards implied, you earn the term premium. That's one of the oldest, most-studied fixed-income edges.

**Hard puzzle:** the 2-year yield is 5% and the 10-year is 3% (inverted curve). A pundit says "the market expects rates to be lower in the future." Is that the whole story? (Mostly, but incomplete. By the forward decomposition, an inverted curve implies **forward rates below current spots** → the market *does* price *falling* future short rates (recession/cut expectations). BUT observed yields also embed a **term premium** (compensation for duration risk), which is usually *positive* and would push long yields *up*. An inversion this deep means expected rate *cuts* outweigh the term premium — a strong recession signal. The nuance — separating *expectations* from *term premium* — is exactly Day 165.)

**Career connection:** "what's the difference between a spot rate and a forward rate, and why do forwards matter?" Strong answer: spot = today's rate to a future date; forward = today's agreed rate for a *future period*, implied by no-arbitrage from the spots — and forwards matter because they're the market's risk-neutral forecast of future rates and the basis for pricing *all* rate derivatives and carry trades. Deriving the forward from two spots on the spot proves you actually understand it.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Bootstrap a curve and imply forwards
```python
import numpy as np

# par yields observed in the market (annual, for simplicity)
mats  = np.array([1,2,3,4,5])
par   = np.array([0.030,0.035,0.038,0.040,0.041])

# bootstrap discount factors from par bonds (coupon = par yield -> price = 100)
DF = np.zeros(len(mats))
for i,(T,c) in enumerate(zip(mats,par)):
    coupon = 100*c
    pv_prev = sum(coupon*DF[j] for j in range(i))     # PV of earlier coupons
    DF[i] = (100 - pv_prev)/(100 + coupon)            # solve for this DF
spot = (1/DF)**(1/mats) - 1
print("maturity:", mats)
print("discount:", DF.round(4))
print("spot   :", (spot*100).round(3))

# implied 1-year forwards from the spot curve
fwd = (1+spot[1:])**mats[1:] / (1+spot[:-1])**mats[:-1] - 1
print("1y fwds :", (fwd*100).round(3), " <- forwards sit above spots on an upward curve")
```
You turn observable par yields into discount factors, spots, and the implied forward path — the exact pipeline every rates system runs at the start of each day.

**Linux:** `python curve.py | tee curve.log`; invert the input `par` to a downward slope and watch the forwards drop *below* spots — the recession-signal mechanics.
