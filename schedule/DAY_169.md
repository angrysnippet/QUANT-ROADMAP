## Block 1 — FRA: the forward rate, traded
A **Forward Rate Agreement (FRA)** locks in an interest rate for a single future period — the tradeable form of the forward rate (Day 164). One party pays fixed, the other pays the floating rate that materializes; they settle the difference. An FRA is the *atom*: a swap is just a **strip of FRAs** chained across many periods. Pricing is pure no-arbitrage — the fair FRA rate *is* the forward rate implied by the curve.

*Why it matters:* FRAs are the conceptual bridge from "the curve has forward rates" to "you can trade each one."

---

## Block 2 — The interest-rate swap (the giant)
A **plain-vanilla swap:** one party pays a **fixed** rate, the other pays a **floating** rate (e.g. SOFR), on a notional, periodically, for years. No principal is exchanged — only the net interest difference. Uses:
- a firm with floating-rate debt **swaps to fixed** to lock its costs (hedging),
- an investor expresses a rate view with huge capital efficiency (no principal outlay),
- it's the **benchmark** instrument — swap rates *are* the modern risk-free-ish curve (Day 164).

Economically: **receive-fixed swap ≈ long a bond** (you profit if rates fall); **pay-fixed ≈ short a bond**. So a swap is a leveraged, capital-light way to take the duration exposure of Day 162.

---

## Block 3 — Pricing a swap: two legs, one curve
Value the swap as **fixed leg vs floating leg**, each a stream of discounted cash flows (Day 161):
- **Floating leg** ≈ par at reset (a beautiful result: the PV of a floating leg paying the curve's own rates equals notional × (1 − final discount factor)).
- **Fixed leg** = the coupon stream discounted on the curve.

The **swap rate** is the fixed rate that makes the two legs equal at inception (so the swap is worth ~0 to enter):
```
Swap rate = (1 − DF(T_N)) / Σ DF(tᵢ)·τᵢ
```
It's a discount-factor-weighted average of forward rates — *entirely* determined by the curve. After inception, as rates move, the swap gains/loses value (mark-to-market), and its **DV01** (Day 162) is how the desk risk-manages it.

## Block 4 — Mathematics · caps, floors & swaptions (options on rates)
Now add **optionality** (Phase 8 returns):
- **Caplet** = a call option on a forward rate: pays `max(rate − K, 0)` for one period. A **cap** = a *strip of caplets* — it caps a floating borrower's rate. (A **floor** = puts on the rate.) Each caplet is priced with **Black's formula** (the BS sibling for forwards), using the forward rate as the underlying and a quoted *rate* volatility.
- **Swaption** = an option to *enter a swap* at a preset fixed rate. A payer swaption ≈ a put on a bond / a call on rates; it's how you trade the *volatility of the curve itself*. Priced with Black's formula on the swap rate, or a term-structure model (Day 168) for consistency across the surface.

So the rates world has its **own volatility surface** — cap/floor vols and the swaption vol *cube* (expiry × tenor × strike) — with skew and term structure exactly analogous to the equity smile (Day 156). Everything you learned about Greeks, implied vol, and hedging (Days 154–156) transfers directly; only the underlying changed from a stock to a rate.

Day 169 is where the curriculum visibly fuses:

- **short-rate/curve models** (Day 168) keep the vol surface arbitrage-free,
- **no-arbitrage replication** (Day 152) underlies *all* of it.

A rates quant isn't using a separate toolkit — they're applying the *same* probability → discounting → replication → optionality stack to a different underlying. **Once you see that the instrument changes but the principles don't, finance stops being a list of products and becomes a single, reusable way of thinking.** That transferability *is* the payoff of the whole roadmap.

**Hard puzzle:** a company has a \$100M floating-rate loan and fears rising rates. Compare hedging with (a) a pay-fixed swap vs (b) buying an interest-rate cap. What's the trade-off? (Both hedge rising rates. **(a) Pay-fixed swap** — *free* to enter (rate set to zero NPV) but **symmetric**: it locks the rate, so if rates *fall* the company is stuck paying fixed and *loses* the benefit (it's short the downside — like a forward/future). **(b) Cap** — costs an upfront **premium** but is **asymmetric**: it caps the max rate while letting the company *still benefit if rates fall* (it's *long* optionality — convexity, Day 151). So swap = cheaper but gives up the upside; cap = pay for insurance and keep the good outcome. It's the classic **forward-vs-option hedge** trade-off — linear/free/symmetric vs convex/costly/asymmetric — now in rates. Same decision a fuel-hedging airline faces with futures vs calls.)

**Career connection:** "how would a company hedge floating-rate debt, and how would you price the hedge?" Strong answer: enter a **pay-fixed swap** (free, symmetric, value the two legs off the curve and solve the par swap rate) or buy a **cap** (a strip of caplets priced with Black's formula on the forward rates, costs premium but preserves downside) — choosing between them is the forward-vs-option / linear-vs-convex trade-off. Pricing the swap as fixed-leg-vs-floating-leg and the cap as a caplet strip shows you can actually build the instruments.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Price a swap rate and a caplet
```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import norm

# discount curve (annual DFs), built earlier by bootstrapping (Day 164)
mats = np.array([1,2,3,4,5])
spot = np.array([0.030,0.034,0.037,0.039,0.040])
DF   = (1+spot)**(-mats)
tau  = np.ones(len(mats))                       # annual accruals

# par swap rate = (1 - DF_last) / sum(DF*tau)
swap_rate = (1 - DF[-1]) / np.sum(DF*tau)
print(f"5y par swap rate = {swap_rate*100:.3f}%")

# implied forward rates (the floating leg's expected payments)
fwd = np.empty(len(mats)); fwd[0]=spot[0]
fwd[1:] = DF[:-1]/DF[1:] - 1
print("forward rates:", (fwd*100).round(3))

# price a 1y-into-1y caplet with Black's formula (vol on the forward rate)
def black_caplet(F,K,vol,T_exp,DF_pay,tau=1.0,notional=1e6):
    d1=(np.log(F/K)+0.5*vol**2*T_exp)/(vol*np.sqrt(T_exp)); d2=d1-vol*np.sqrt(T_exp)
    return notional*tau*DF_pay*(F*norm.cdf(d1)-K*norm.cdf(d2))

cap = black_caplet(F=fwd[1], K=0.035, vol=0.25, T_exp=1.0, DF_pay=DF[1])
print(f"1y-into-1y caplet (K=3.5%) ≈ ${cap:,.0f}")
```
The swap rate falls out of the discount factors; the caplet uses **Black's formula** on the forward rate — Phase 8's option machinery, now pricing a rate option.
