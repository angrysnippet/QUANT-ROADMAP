## Block 1 — Assembling the strategy
The full pipeline for one pair:
1. **Universe & pairing:** select candidate pairs by economic link (sector, supply chain, ETF-vs-basket), then filter by **cointegration** (Day 172).
2. **Signal:** rolling hedge ratio → spread → lookahead-safe **z-score** (Day 171).
3. **Entry/exit:** `|z| > entry` to open, `|z| < exit` to close, plus a **stop** (Block 4) for when the relationship breaks.
4. **Sizing:** scale the position so each leg is dollar- (and beta-) neutral, and so risk per trade is controlled (Block 2).
5. **Costs:** subtract realistic transaction costs and slippage on every rebalance.
6. **Aggregate** across *many* pairs (the breadth that makes the LLN work, Day 171).

*Why it matters:* a strategy is a *system*, and each component (pairing, signal, sizing, costs) can quietly make or break it. The backtest tests the whole chain.

---

## Block 2 — Position sizing & neutrality
For a pair, hold `1` unit of A against `β` units of B so the spread is the only exposure. Then size the *whole pair position* for risk:
- **dollar-neutral:** equal long/short dollar value (cancel market beta, Day 171),
- **volatility-targeted** (Day 143): scale so each pair contributes similar risk → no single pair dominates,
- **risk-parity across pairs** (Day 146): allocate so pairs contribute equally to portfolio variance.

Never equal-weight by accident — a high-vol pair will silently dominate your P&L and drawdowns. Sizing *is* risk management, applied before the trade.

---

## Block 3 — Transaction costs: the strategy killer
Stat arb is **high-turnover**, so costs are existential, not a footnote. Model all of them:
- **commissions/fees** (small but real),
- **bid-ask spread** (you cross it on entry *and* exit — pay it twice per round-trip),
- **slippage / market impact** (your order moves the price, worse at size — Day 179),
- **shorting costs** (borrow fees, especially for the short leg).

A strategy with a gross Sharpe of 2.0 can have a **net Sharpe near 0** once realistic costs hit a fast-reverting spread. **Always backtest net of costs** — a gross-only backtest is marketing, not research. Rule of thumb: if the per-trade edge isn't comfortably larger than the round-trip cost, there's no strategy.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the backtest pitfalls (memorize these)
The graveyard of stat-arb backtests. Each makes returns look spectacular and vanish live:
- **Lookahead bias** (Day 141): using the full-sample mean/std, or today's data to trade today — fix with `.shift(1)` and point-in-time data.
- **Survivorship bias** (Day 123): testing on *today's* surviving names misses the pairs where one leg went to zero (a delisting/bankruptcy is the *worst* case for a short-the-spread trade).
- **Overfitting / data-snooping** (Day 124): tuning entry/exit thresholds, lookbacks, and pair selection until the backtest sings → the **deflated Sharpe** (Day 145) for the number of trials.
- **Ignoring costs** (Block 3): the most common silent killer.
- **No stop-loss for breakdown:** cointegration ends (mergers, regime shifts); a spread that *stops* reverting and trends will hand you an unbounded loss while your z-score screams "even better opportunity!" — you need a **time-stop (≫ half-life) and a divergence stop**.
- **Reversion of the unconditional mean vs conditional:** re-test cointegration on a rolling basis; don't assume a 2018 pair still holds in 2024.

The discipline: **walk-forward / out-of-sample** — fit parameters on a window, trade the *next* window, roll forward. If it only works in-sample, it doesn't work.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the backtest is an adversary, not an ally
The deepest lesson of this whole module: **your backtest wants to lie to you, and your job is to interrogate it like a hostile witness.** Every degree of freedom (which pairs, which thresholds, which lookback, which period) is an opportunity to *fit the past instead of finding an edge*. The professional process inverts the naive one:
- decide the **rules and validation scheme first**, *then* test (pre-registration mindset),
- minimize **free parameters**; prefer robust-across-settings over optimal-at-one-setting,
- always hold out **truly untouched** data,
- judge by **out-of-sample, cost-adjusted, trial-deflated** performance only,
- demand an **economic reason** the edge exists and should persist (Day 149).

This is Day 124 (overfitting), Day 145 (Sharpe traps), and Day 149 (skill vs luck) **all cashing out at once** in a real strategy. The quants who survive are not the ones with the best backtests — they're the ones who trust backtests *least* and validate *hardest*.

**Hard puzzle:** your pairs backtest shows a Sharpe of 3.5 over 2015–2023. Before risking a dollar, list the five checks that would most likely reveal it as a mirage. (1) **Net of realistic costs?** — re-run with full bid/ask + slippage + borrow; a fast spread often dies here. 2) **Out-of-sample / walk-forward?** — did you tune thresholds on the same data you're reporting? Re-test on untouched data. 3) **Survivorship?** — does the universe include delisted/bankrupt names; what happens when a short leg gaps to zero? 4) **Multiple testing?** — how many pairs/parameters did you try; apply the deflated Sharpe. 5) **Breakdown handling?** — is there a stop for when cointegration ends, or does the backtest assume reversion always wins? Any one of these can turn 3.5 into 0. *That's* the senior pre-trade checklist.)

**Career connection:** "you've backtested a stat-arb strategy with a great Sharpe — would you trade it?" Strong answer: **not yet** — first I'd verify it's net of realistic transaction costs, validated walk-forward / out-of-sample, free of survivorship bias, deflated for the number of pairs and parameters tried, and equipped with a breakdown stop; and I'd want an economic reason the edge persists. Leading with *skepticism and a validation checklist* rather than excitement is exactly what desks want to hear.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · An honest, cost-aware, walk-forward pairs backtest
```python
import numpy as np, pandas as pd
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# simulate a cointegrated pair (shared trend + OU spread)
T=2000; common=np.cumsum(rng.standard_normal(T)*0.01)
sp=np.zeros(T)
for t in range(1,T): sp[t]=0.96*sp[t-1]+rng.standard_normal()*0.4
A=pd.Series(100*np.exp(common+0.01*sp)); B=pd.Series(100*np.exp(common))

def backtest(A,B, entry=2.0, exit=0.3, cost_bps=5, lb=60, train=1000):
    beta=(A.rolling(lb).cov(B)/B.rolling(lb).var())
    spread=np.log(A)-beta*np.log(B)
    z=(spread - spread.rolling(lb).mean().shift(1))/spread.rolling(lb).std().shift(1)
    pos=np.zeros(len(A))
    for t in range(1,len(A)):
        if pos[t-1]==0: pos[t]=-1 if z[t]>entry else (1 if z[t]<-entry else 0)
        else:           pos[t]=0 if abs(z[t])<exit else pos[t-1]
    ret=pos[:-1]*spread.diff().shift(-1).values[:-1]
    cost=(cost_bps/1e4)*np.abs(np.diff(pos))                # round-trip cost on rebalances
    net=ret-cost
    oos=slice(train,len(net))                                # walk-forward: report OOS only
    g=np.nansum(ret[oos]); n=np.nansum(net[oos])
    sh=np.nanmean(net[oos])/np.nanstd(net[oos])*np.sqrt(252)
    return round(g,3), round(n,3), round(sh,2)

g,n,sh = backtest(A,B)
print(f"OUT-OF-SAMPLE  gross={g}  net-of-cost={n}  net Sharpe={sh}")
```
Note the deliberate honesty: lookahead-safe z (`.shift(1)`), **costs on every rebalance**, and **out-of-sample-only** reporting. Flip to in-sample or drop the cost term and watch the numbers inflate — that's the mirage you're learning to resist.

**Linux:** `python pairs_bt.py | tee bt.log`; sweep `entry`/`cost_bps` and notice how fragile the Sharpe is to costs and thresholds — fragility *is* the overfitting warning.

## Quant Thinking Track — Systems and second-order honesty
A strategy is a system of components, but the *meta-skill* is **intellectual honesty about your own results.** Every quant can build a backtest; few can resist the seduction of a good one. The career-defining habit is to treat every impressive backtest as guilty until proven innocent through out-of-sample, cost-aware, trial-corrected validation. Honesty isn't a virtue here — it's the edge.
