## Block 1 — The two numbers that describe any asset
For a return series we summarize with:
- **Expected return** `μ = E[r]` — the *reward*.
- **Risk** `σ = std(r)` — the *uncertainty*, the most common first proxy.

A scatter of assets in (σ, μ) space is the entire mental model of investing: you want to move **up and to the left** — more return, less risk. Nothing is free; the market roughly prices a positive slope (the *risk premium*).

*Why it matters:* a return number alone is meaningless. "I made 30%" — with what volatility, what worst loss, what leverage? Risk-adjustment is the only honest comparison.

---

## Block 2 — Why standard deviation is a *flawed* risk measure
σ is convenient (one number, plugs into the math) but it:
1. punishes **upside** volatility the same as downside (you don't fear big *gains*),
2. assumes a roughly symmetric/thin-tailed world — false (Day 119 fat tails, crashes),
3. says nothing about **path** — two assets with equal σ can have wildly different worst drawdowns.

Better/complementary measures: **semi-deviation** (downside only), **VaR / CVaR** (Day 139 — tail loss), and **max drawdown** (peak-to-trough). Senior quants quote σ *and* a tail/drawdown number.

---

## Block 3 — Arithmetic vs geometric return (the trap that sinks accounts)
- **Arithmetic mean** `μ` = average of period returns — what you'd expect *per period in expectation*.
- **Geometric mean** `g` = the *compounded* growth you actually realize: `(∏(1+rₜ))^{1/n} − 1`.

`g ≈ μ − ½σ²` (volatility drag). **+50% then −50% leaves you at 0.75×** — down 25%, despite a 0% arithmetic average. The bigger the swings, the more geometric trails arithmetic. **Wealth compounds geometrically; risk is the gap.**

**Concept check:** an asset returns +100% and −50% in alternating years. Arithmetic mean? (+25%.) Geometric? (0% — you end where you started.) Which do you live on? (Geometric.)

---

## Block 4 — Drawdown: the risk you actually feel
**Drawdown(t)** = how far below the running peak you are. **Max drawdown** is the worst such gap over the period. It's the number that:
- triggers redemptions and margin calls,
- determines whether you (or your investors) *survive to compound*,
- is highly path-dependent and not captured by σ.

A strategy with a great Sharpe but a 60% max drawdown is, for most real capital, untradeable. **Survival is a precondition for returns** — ruin is absorbing (Day 125 / optional stopping, Day 140).

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · risk is multi-dimensional; pick the metric for the decision
There is no single "risk number." Match the metric to the question:
- comparing strategies of similar shape → **σ / Sharpe** (Day 145),
- worried about blowups → **CVaR, max drawdown**,
- compounding capital → **geometric return**,
- tail-hedging → **skew/kurtosis, left-tail probability**.

**Hard puzzle:** Fund A: μ=12%, σ=10%, maxDD=15%. Fund B: μ=12%, σ=10%, maxDD=45%. Same mean and same volatility — why prefer A, and what does that prove about σ? (Path/tail risk: equal σ can hide very different drawdowns; σ is blind to autocorrelation of losses and fat tails. Always pair it with a drawdown/tail measure.)

**Career connection:** "how do you measure risk?" A weak answer says "standard deviation." A strong answer says "σ for a first pass, but I'd report downside deviation, CVaR, and max drawdown, and I'd compound on the *geometric* mean" — that breadth signals real desk experience.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Risk dashboard for a return series
```python
import numpy as np, pandas as pd
rng = np.random.default_rng(3)
r = rng.standard_normal(2520) * 0.01 + 0.0003     # ~10yr daily, small drift

def stats(r):
    mu_a  = r.mean()*252
    sig_a = r.std()*252**0.5
    geo_a = (np.prod(1+r))**(252/len(r)) - 1
    eq    = (1+r).cumprod()
    dd    = eq/np.maximum.accumulate(eq) - 1
    cvar  = r[r <= np.quantile(r, 0.05)].mean()    # 5% expected shortfall (daily)
    return dict(arith=round(mu_a,3), geo=round(geo_a,3), vol=round(sig_a,3),
                drag=round(mu_a-geo_a,3), maxDD=round(dd.min(),3),
                CVaR5_daily=round(cvar,4))

print(stats(r))
```
Watch `arith − geo ≈ ½σ²` (the drag) and note `maxDD` is far worse than σ alone would suggest.
