## Block 1 — What alpha actually is
Alpha is the **intercept** of the factor regression — the average return *not explained* by your exposures:
```
α = realized return − (return predicted by beta/factor exposures)
```
Positive, persistent, statistically significant α = genuine skill (or an un-modeled risk premium you're harvesting). Crucially, alpha is **relative to a model**: "alpha" against CAPM may vanish once you add size/value/momentum factors (Day 148) — it was just *factor beta in disguise*. **Real alpha survives after you've stripped out every known cheap factor.** Much "alpha" sold by managers is repackaged beta.

---

## Block 2 — Where alpha comes from (the four edges)
You only beat an efficient-ish market if you have a real, defensible edge:
1. **Informational** — you know something others don't (legally: better data, satellite imagery, faster parsing). Decays as data commoditizes.
2. **Analytical** — same data, better model (the quant's home turf — superior estimation, ML done right).
3. **Behavioral** — exploit persistent human biases (overreaction → reversal, underreaction → momentum). These are slow to arbitrage because they're wired into people.
4. **Structural** — rules, frictions, mandates create forced flows (index-rebalance trades, liquidity provision, regulatory constraints) you can monetize.

If you can't name *which* of these your edge is and *why it persists*, you probably don't have alpha — you have an overfit backtest (Day 124).

---

## Block 3 — Skill vs luck: the hardest question in finance
With thousands of funds, **some will beat the market for a decade by pure chance** (Day 121 LLN, Day 124 multiple testing). Distinguishing skill from luck needs:
- **Statistical significance:** is the α t-stat large *after* honest correction for the number of strategies tried (deflated Sharpe, Day 145)?
- **Sample size in *bets*, not years:** 30 years of one annual bet ≈ 30 observations — weak. A daily strategy gives thousands of quasi-independent bets — far stronger evidence.
- **Out-of-sample / live track record:** in-sample alpha is nearly worthless; alpha that persists *after publication/deployment* is the real test.
- **An economic story:** a mechanism for *why* the edge exists and *why it isn't already arbitraged*.

**Concept check:** a manager beat the S&P for 9 straight years. Skill? (Maybe — but with thousands of managers, ~several will do this by luck alone. Demand the t-stat, the number of peers/trials, and the economic mechanism before concluding skill.)

---

## Block 4 — Alpha decays (the arms race)
Alpha is **competed away**: once an edge is known, capital floods in, prices adjust, and the excess return shrinks toward zero. Evidence: many published anomalies *weaken or vanish after publication* (McLean & Pontiff). Consequences:
- alpha is a **depreciating asset** — you must keep researching just to stand still,
- **capacity limits:** an edge that works on \$10M may vanish at \$10B (your own trading moves the price — market impact),
- **secrecy** has real value — the best edges aren't published,
- crowded trades unwind violently together (Aug 2007 quant quake).

**Hard puzzle:** your backtested signal has a fabulous Sharpe over 2015–2020, but live performance since deployment is flat. Three distinct explanations — and how do you tell which? (1) *Overfit* — never real; check if it held out-of-sample *before* you traded it. 2) *Alpha decay/crowding* — was real, got arbitraged; check if peers run the same signal and if it weakened post-publication. 3) *Capacity/impact* — real but your size moves the market; check fills vs mid and performance net of slippage. The diagnostics: out-of-sample vs live split, crowding indicators, and transaction-cost attribution.)

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the Fundamental Law of Active Management
Grinold's law ties it together:
```
IR ≈ IC · √Breadth
```
Your **Information Ratio** (risk-adjusted alpha, Day 145) ≈ **skill per bet (IC**, the correlation between your forecast and the outcome) × **√(number of independent bets per year)**. Two routes to a great strategy:
- **high IC, low breadth** — be very right, rarely (concentrated fundamental bets),
- **low IC, high breadth** — be slightly right, *thousands* of times (the quant/stat-arb path: an IC of 0.05 across 1,000s of independent positions compounds into a strong IR).

This is *why* quant funds diversify across thousands of small, weakly-predictable bets: **breadth manufactures Sharpe from a tiny edge.** It also says raising breadth (more assets, higher frequency, more independent signals) is often easier than raising skill.

**Career connection:** "you have a signal with a 0.03 correlation to next-day returns — is it useless?" Strong answer: invoke `IR = IC·√Breadth` — a 0.03 IC across thousands of independent daily bets is a *real business*; the question isn't the per-bet edge, it's the breadth and independence of the bets. Tiny but persistent and broad beats large but rare and fragile. Knowing this law instantly marks you as someone who understands how quant alpha is actually built.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Is the alpha real? IC, breadth, and a luck baseline
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(4)

# a weak but REAL signal: IC ~ 0.05 across many names each day
days, names = 1000, 200
true_ic = 0.05
signal  = rng.standard_normal((days, names))
noise   = rng.standard_normal((days, names))
fwd_ret = true_ic*signal + np.sqrt(1-true_ic**2)*noise        # next-day returns

# realized IC = daily corr(signal, return)
ic = np.array([np.corrcoef(signal[d], fwd_ret[d])[0,1] for d in range(days)])
print(f"mean IC = {ic.mean():.3f}  (built 0.05)")

# Fundamental Law: IR ≈ IC * sqrt(breadth)
breadth = names                      # ~independent bets per day
print(f"implied per-day IR ≈ {ic.mean()*np.sqrt(breadth):.2f}")

# luck baseline: shuffle to destroy the signal, see the null IC distribution
null = np.array([np.corrcoef(rng.permutation(signal[d]), fwd_ret[d])[0,1]
                 for d in range(days)])
print(f"null IC mean = {null.mean():.3f}, std = {null.std():.3f}  -> z = "
      f"{ic.mean()/null.std():.1f}")
```
The shuffled "null" shows what IC looks like with *no* skill — your real IC sitting many std-devs above it is how you argue **skill, not luck**.

**Linux:** `python alpha.py | tee alpha.log`; rerun with `true_ic=0` to confirm a genuinely skill-less signal collapses into the null distribution.

## Quant Thinking Track — Falsification
The alpha mindset is relentless *self-falsification*: assume your edge is luck or overfit until the data — out-of-sample, deflated for trials, with an economic mechanism — forces you to concede it's real. The market punishes self-deception with money. **Extraordinary alpha claims require extraordinary (out-of-sample) evidence.**
