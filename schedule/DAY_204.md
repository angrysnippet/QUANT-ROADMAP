## Block 1 — Markets live in regimes
Financial markets exhibit **distinct regimes** with different behavior:
- **bull vs bear** — different drift, different correlations,
- **low-vol vs high-vol** (calm vs crisis) — volatility clusters into persistent states (Day 143),
- **risk-on vs risk-off** — correlations *change* across regimes (the diversification-breakdown of Day 146),
- **trending vs mean-reverting** — the same asset behaves differently in different periods.

A model fit on *pooled* data estimates a blurry *average* of these regimes — fitting none well. Regime-switching models instead say: *the world is in one of `K` hidden states, each with its own parameters, and it transitions between them.* This directly addresses non-stationarity (Day 142): the series isn't one drifting process but a *switching* between several stationary ones.

*Why it matters:* "my strategy stopped working" is often "the regime changed" — and regime models make that diagnosable and sometimes predictable.

---

## Block 2 — The Hidden Markov Model
A **Hidden Markov Model (HMM)** formalizes regimes:
- a **hidden state** `S_t ∈ {1, …, K}` (the regime — e.g. calm/crisis) that you *can't observe directly*,
- the state evolves as a **Markov chain** (Day 137): a **transition matrix** gives `P(S_t = j | S_{t−1} = i)` — regimes are persistent (high probability of staying), with occasional switches,
- each state emits observations from its *own* distribution — e.g. calm regime: low-vol Normal returns; crisis regime: high-vol, negatively-skewed returns.

You observe only the returns; the regime is **hidden** (it's a *discrete-state* state-space model, Day 203 — the cousin of the continuous Kalman state). The HMM infers, from the observed returns, *which regime you're probably in* and *the regime parameters* — all without ever labeling regimes by hand.

**Concept check:** an HMM on equity returns finds two states: State 1 (vol 10%, mean +8%) and State 2 (vol 35%, mean −20%), with State 2 being "sticky" (85% chance of persisting). What are these? (State 1 = **calm bull** regime, State 2 = **crisis/bear** regime — high vol, negative drift, and persistent (crises cluster, Day 143). The HMM discovered the calm/crisis structure *unsupervised*, from returns alone — and the stickiness says regimes don't flip day-to-day.)

## Block 3 — Markov-switching models & regime detection
- **Markov-switching (Hamilton) models:** regime-switching applied to a *model's parameters* — e.g. a Markov-switching AR (the AR coefficients differ by regime) or Markov-switching GARCH (volatility dynamics differ by regime). The mean, variance, *and* relationships all switch.
- **Regime detection (inference):** given the model, compute `P(regime = k | data)`:
  - **filtered** probability (using data up to now — lookahead-safe, for live use, Day 203),
  - **smoothed** probability (using all data — for historical analysis only).
- **Estimation:** the **Baum–Welch / EM algorithm** (expectation-maximization) learns the transition matrix and per-regime parameters; the **Viterbi algorithm** finds the most likely *sequence* of hidden states.

Practically, regime detection feeds **adaptive strategies**: trade momentum in trending regimes, mean-reversion in ranging ones; cut leverage when the crisis-regime probability rises; switch risk models by regime.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the EM algorithm and the inference problem
HMM estimation faces a chicken-and-egg problem: to find the regime parameters you need to know which regime each point is in, but to know the regimes you need the parameters. **Expectation-Maximization (EM / Baum–Welch)** solves it by iterating:
- **E-step:** given current parameter guesses, compute the *probability* each observation belongs to each regime (the forward–backward algorithm — a discrete-state analogue of Kalman filtering/smoothing, Day 203),
- **M-step:** given those soft regime-assignments, *re-estimate* each regime's parameters (mean, vol) and the transition matrix by weighted maximum likelihood (Day 122),
- **iterate** until convergence (EM monotonically increases the likelihood).

Key mathematical cautions:
- **EM finds local optima** → sensitive to initialization; run from multiple starts.
- **choosing `K`** (number of regimes) is a model-selection problem (AIC/BIC; usually 2–3 regimes — more overfits, Day 191) — and over-fitting regimes is a real danger in low-signal data.
- **regimes are only identified *probabilistically* and with a lag** — you know you're *probably* in a crisis only after enough crisis-like data has accumulated, so regime models are better at *confirming* a regime than *predicting* the switch.

The inference output — a time series of regime probabilities — is itself a *feature* (Day 192) you can feed to other models or trade directly. And the whole structure is the **discrete-state special case of state-space** (Day 203): hidden state, Markov transition, noisy emission — Kalman's combinatorial sibling.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · regimes reframe non-stationarity as a feature
Regime-switching offers a *profound* reframing of the curriculum's recurring villain — non-stationarity (Day 142). Instead of "the market is annoyingly non-stationary, so models break," regime models say: **the market is a *switching mixture of stationary regimes*, and the switching is itself structure you can model.** This turns the enemy into an asset:
- it **explains strategy decay** (Day 149) mechanistically — your strategy was fit to (or works in) one regime, and it "stops working" when the regime switches, not because the edge was fake,
- it **explains correlation breakdown** (Day 146) — correlations are regime-dependent (low in calm, →1 in crisis), so diversification fails *because the regime changed*, and a regime model anticipates it,
- it **explains the Quant Quake** (Day 178) and most "surprises" — they're regime transitions the pooled model couldn't see,
- it enables **adaptive strategies** — detect the regime and switch behavior (momentum in trends, reversion in ranges, de-risk in crises), and **regime-conditional risk models** (different Σ per regime).

But the deepest lesson is *epistemic humility about regimes themselves*: regimes are **detected with a lag and only probabilistically**, the *number* of regimes is a modelling choice prone to overfitting, and a *new* kind of regime (one not in your training data) can always appear — the market can enter a state it's never been in (2008, COVID, negative rates). So regime models are powerful for *understanding and adapting*, but they don't abolish uncertainty — they **organize** it. This connects to the whole arc: markets are non-stationary (142), so edges decay (149) and risks cluster (143, 178); regime-switching gives you a *language and a tool* for that non-stationarity — not a way to escape it, but a way to model, monitor, and adapt to it. The mature quant uses regime models to **know what world they're probably in and to adapt** — while never forgetting that the next regime might be one no model has seen. **Non-stationarity isn't noise to be eliminated; it's structure to be modeled — and humility to be retained.**

**Hard puzzle:** a momentum strategy delivers a Sharpe of 1.5 for years, then suffers a sharp, sudden drawdown ("momentum crash"), then recovers. A regime-switching analysis would explain this how — and could it have *prevented* the loss? (A regime model would identify that momentum strategies have **regime-dependent performance**: they earn steadily in *calm/trending* regimes but suffer **violent crashes during regime *transitions*** — specifically, sharp reversals when a crisis regime flips back to recovery (the classic "momentum crash" of 2009, where beaten-down losers rocketed and the short-loser leg of momentum was crushed). The pooled Sharpe of 1.5 *averages* the steady gains and the rare crashes, hiding the regime structure. **Could it have prevented the loss? Partially, not fully:** (1) A regime model that detected the **high-volatility/crisis regime** could have **cut momentum exposure or hedged** when the crisis-regime probability spiked — momentum crashes occur disproportionately in high-vol regimes, and **volatility-scaling momentum** (sizing inversely to forecast vol, Days 143/202) demonstrably reduces crash severity. So *yes*, regime/vol awareness could have *mitigated* it. (2) **But not fully prevented** — regimes are detected **with a lag and probabilistically** (Block 4), and the crash happens *at the transition*, often faster than detection; the model confirms the crisis regime partly *after* the damage. So the realistic claim is **mitigation via regime-conditional sizing/hedging, not prevention** — you reduce exposure as crisis-regime probability rises and especially scale down momentum in high-vol regimes, cushioning but not eliminating the transition loss. The deeper lesson: the drawdown wasn't the edge "failing" — it was a **regime transition**, an inherent feature of momentum's payoff (it's *short* a kind of convexity/insurance, paying steadily then crashing — the negative-skew pattern of Days 145/150/165), and regime models make that *understandable, monitorable, and partly manageable* rather than a mysterious blowup. Understanding *why* via regimes is what lets you size it sanely.)

**Career connection:** "why do strategies that work for years suddenly stop working?" Strong answer: usually a **regime change** — markets live in distinct regimes (calm/crisis, bull/bear, trending/ranging) with different drifts, volatilities, *and correlations*, and a strategy fit to or suited for one regime decays or crashes when the regime switches (it's not that the edge was fake). I'd model this with a **Hidden Markov / Markov-switching model** — a hidden regime state following a Markov chain, each regime with its own parameters, estimated via **EM/Baum–Welch** — and use the **filtered regime probability** (lookahead-safe) to **adapt**: switch between momentum and mean-reversion, or cut leverage and hedge as crisis-regime probability rises. The caveats: regimes are detected **with a lag and probabilistically**, `K` is prone to overfitting (use 2–3), and a genuinely *new* regime can always appear. Framing strategy decay as regime-switching — and proposing regime-conditional adaptation *with* the humility about detection lag — is a sophisticated, senior answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · An HMM discovers calm/crisis regimes
```python
import numpy as np
from hmmlearn import hmm                          # pip install hmmlearn
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# simulate returns switching between calm (low vol) and crisis (high vol, neg drift)
T=2000; state=0; rets=[]; states=[]
P = np.array([[0.98,0.02],[0.10,0.90]])           # sticky regimes
params = [(0.0005, 0.008), (-0.001, 0.030)]       # (mean, vol) per regime
for t in range(T):
    state = rng.choice(2, p=P[state]); states.append(state)
    mu,sig = params[state]; rets.append(rng.normal(mu,sig))
rets = np.array(rets).reshape(-1,1)

# fit a 2-state Gaussian HMM (unsupervised — never told the regimes)
model = hmm.GaussianHMM(n_components=2, covariance_type="diag", n_iter=100, random_state=0)
model.fit(rets)
post = model.predict_proba(rets)                  # regime probabilities
vols = np.sqrt(model.covars_.flatten())
crisis = np.argmax(vols)                           # the high-vol state = crisis
print(f"recovered regime vols: {vols.round(3)}  (one calm, one crisis)")
print(f"transition matrix:\n{model.transmat_.round(3)}")
print(f"P(crisis) on the last 5 days: {post[-5:,crisis].round(2)}")
print(f"agreement with true regime: {np.mean((post[:,crisis]>0.5)==np.array(states)):.0%}")
```
The HMM *unsupervised* recovers the two regimes (calm/crisis vols), the sticky transition matrix, and a real-time crisis probability — turning non-stationarity into a usable, lookahead-safe signal.

**Linux:** `python hmm.py | tee hmm.log`; feed real index returns and watch `P(crisis)` spike in 2008/2020 — regimes the model finds on its own.

## Quant Thinking Track — Turn the obstacle into the model
The reframe at the heart of this day is a transferable thinking move: **when something keeps breaking your models, ask whether *that very thing* can be modeled.** Non-stationarity broke pooled models — so model the switching itself (regimes). The obstacle becomes the object of study. Across domains, the breakthrough often comes not from eliminating the messy complication but from *promoting it to a first-class part of the model* — and frequently it turns out to carry the most useful structure.
