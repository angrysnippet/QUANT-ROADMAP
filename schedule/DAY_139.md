## Block 1 — The core idea
To estimate `E[f(X)]`, draw many samples `X₁…X_N`, compute `f`, and average:
```
E[f(X)] ≈ (1/N) Σ f(Xᵢ)
```
By the LLN it converges to the true value; by the CLT the **error shrinks like `1/√N`** (Day 121). Quadruple the simulations → halve the error.

*Why it matters:* most realistic derivatives and risk measures have **no closed form** — simulation is the practical answer.

---

## Block 2 — Worked example: estimate π
Throw random points in a unit square; the fraction inside the quarter-circle ≈ π/4. Crude, but it shows the whole pattern: *express the quantity as an expectation, then sample.*

**Concept check:** to get one more decimal digit of accuracy (10× less error), how many more samples? (100× — because error ~ 1/√N.)

---

## Block 3 — Pricing by simulation (the quant use)
A European option's price is the **discounted expected payoff** under the risk-neutral measure:
```
price ≈ e^(−rT) · (1/N) Σ payoff(S_T^(i))
```
Simulate many terminal prices `S_T` (e.g. via geometric Brownian motion — Day 140), average the payoffs, discount. This handles **path-dependent** and **multi-asset** options that have no formula — Monte Carlo's killer app.

---

## Block 4 — Variance reduction (working smarter)
Plain MC needs many samples for tight estimates. Pros cut variance without more samples:
- **Antithetic variates:** pair `Z` with `−Z` (cancels noise).
- **Control variates:** use a correlated quantity with a known answer to correct the estimate.
- **Common random numbers / Sobol (quasi-MC):** better-spread samples → faster convergence.

**Exercise:** why does antithetic sampling help most when the payoff is roughly *monotone* in the random driver? (The paired errors are negatively correlated → their average has lower variance.)

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking (MC across the desk)

- **Derivatives pricing:** exotics, path-dependent (Asian, barrier), basket options.
- **Risk:** Monte-Carlo **VaR/CVaR** — simulate the P&L distribution of a portfolio, read off the tail.
- **Backtesting under uncertainty:** bootstrap returns to get confidence bands on Sharpe/drawdown.
- **Model validation:** when you derive a closed form, *also* simulate to check it (Day 118's habit).

**Hard puzzle:** your MC option price has a standard error of ±0.05 with 10,000 paths, but you need ±0.005. How many paths, and is brute force wise? (100× = 1,000,000 paths — or use **variance reduction** to get there with far fewer. Senior answer: reduce variance, don't just brute-force N.)

**Career connection:** "how would you price a path-dependent option?" → Monte Carlo with risk-neutral GBM paths + variance reduction. "How would you compute portfolio VaR?" → simulate P&L, take the quantile. Both are standard.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · MC option price + error bar
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
S0,K,r,sigma,T,N = 100,100,0.02,0.2,1.0,200_000
Z = rng.standard_normal(N)
ST = S0*np.exp((r-0.5*sigma**2)*T + sigma*np.sqrt(T)*Z)   # risk-neutral GBM
payoff = np.maximum(ST-K, 0)
price = np.exp(-r*T)*payoff.mean()
stderr = np.exp(-r*T)*payoff.std(ddof=1)/np.sqrt(N)
print(round(price,3), "±", round(1.96*stderr,3))          # compare to Black–Scholes ~8.9
```
Bonus: add antithetic variates (`Z` and `−Z`) and watch the standard error drop.

**Linux:** `time python mc.py` — MC is embarrassingly parallel; note runtime vs N.

## Quant Thinking Track — Always Report the Error Bar
A Monte-Carlo number without its standard error is half an answer. The `±1.96·SE` band tells you whether more samples (or variance reduction) are needed — professional MC always quotes it.
