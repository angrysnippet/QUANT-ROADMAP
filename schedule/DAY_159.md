## Block 1 — The variance swap: pure, clean vol exposure
Recall the problem from Day 155: a single delta-hedged option's variance exposure is weighted by **dollar-gamma `Γ·S²`**, which changes along the path — messy. A **variance swap** fixes this. Its payoff is *linear in realized variance*:
```
Payoff = Notional × ( σ²_realized − K_var )
```
where `K_var` is the fixed **strike variance** agreed today (≈ implied variance). No delta to hedge, no path-weighting — you receive realized variance and pay the strike. **It is the purest bet on "how much will the market move?"** Long variance = long fear; short variance = collect the premium and pray.

*Why it matters:* it cleanly separates the *vol view* from the *direction view* and from the *timing* — the thing options traders were always really trading, now packaged.

---

## Block 2 — How it's built (the static-replication insight)
The beautiful result (Demeterfi–Derman–Kamani–Zou, 1999): a variance swap is **statically replicated by a strip of options across all strikes**, weighted by `1/K²`, plus a dynamic delta-hedge. Intuitively, summing options of *every* strike with `1/K²` weights produces a portfolio with **constant dollar-gamma** — exactly the path-independence Day 155 said we needed. So variance is *manufacturable* from vanilla options (replication again, Day 152). This `1/K²` weighting is also precisely the recipe behind the next block.

---

## Block 3 — The VIX: fear in one number
The **VIX** is the market-implied 30-day volatility of the S&P 500, computed *model-free* from a `1/K²`-weighted strip of SPX options (the variance-swap formula, Block 2) — **not** from Black–Scholes. Properties:
- **Spikes in crashes** (it's dominated by the expensive OTM-put skew, Day 156) → the "fear gauge."
- **Strongly negatively correlated with the S&P** (vol up when stocks down — the leverage effect, Day 143) → which makes *long vol* a portfolio **diversifier/hedge**.
- **Mean-reverts** (Day 142): vol is high in storms, low in calm, and pulls back — the basis for vol term-structure trades.

VIX futures, options, and ETPs (VXX, etc.) make this directly tradeable — though the *roll cost* of a typically-upward-sloping curve quietly bleeds long-vol holders.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the variance risk premium
**Implied variance systematically exceeds subsequent realized variance**: `E[σ²_implied] > E[σ²_realized]`, persistently and by a meaningful margin. This **variance risk premium (VRP)** is compensation paid to those who *sell* variance (insurance) and bear the crash risk. Quantify it as `VRP = implied² − realized²` (e.g. VIX² minus realized S&P variance). Empirically:
- short-variance / short-vol strategies earn a **high Sharpe in normal times** (Day 145),
- with **brutally negative skew** — they collapse in vol spikes (Feb 2018 "Volmageddon" wiped out short-VIX ETPs in a day).
So the VRP is real, large, *and* exactly the "selling nickels in front of a steamroller" payoff of Day 150 — a risk premium, not free money. The trade is **short a fat, correlated left tail**; size it like insurance, not like a Sharpe-maximizer.

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · vol completes the asset picture
Volatility as an asset reframes everything you've built:
- **Direction** is traded with stocks/forwards (linear).
- **Variance/vol** is traded with options/var-swaps/VIX (convex, Day 151).
These are *separate, independently priced risk dimensions.* A modern book holds views on *both* — e.g. long the market but long vol as a hedge, or market-neutral but harvesting the VRP. And because vol is **anti-correlated with equities and spikes exactly in crises**, long-vol is one of the few things that pays off when diversification fails (Day 146's correlation breakdown). It's the natural *tail hedge* — expensive to carry (negative VRP for the buyer), cheap insurance when it matters. **The full quant worldview: price, time, *and* volatility are three tradeable axes — and this module taught you the third.**

**Hard puzzle:** short-variance strategies show a Sharpe of ~1.5 for years, then lose 80% in a week. Is the strategy "good"? (It's a **risk premium harvest with a catastrophic left tail**, not skill (Day 149) — the high Sharpe is the VRP, and Sharpe is blind to the negative skew/kurtosis (Day 145). It can be a *sound* allocation if sized as insurance-selling with strict tail limits and capital you can afford to lose; it's *ruinous* if levered to maximize Sharpe. Same trade, opposite verdict depending on sizing — the lesson the whole risk module has been building toward.)

**Career connection:** "how do you trade volatility directly, and why would you?" Strong answer: variance swaps or VIX products for clean vol exposure (no path-dependent dollar-gamma); you'd go *long* vol as a crash hedge/diversifier (it's anti-correlated with equities and spikes in stress) or *short* vol to harvest the variance risk premium — while respecting that short-vol has a fat, correlated left tail that demands insurance-style sizing. Tying var-swaps → VIX → VRP → tail-hedging is a complete vol-desk narrative.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Realized vs implied variance and the VRP
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# simulate daily returns; "implied" variance is what the market charges (with a premium)
days = 252*5
real_vol = 0.16
ret = rng.standard_normal(days)*real_vol/np.sqrt(252)
realized_var = ret.var()*252

# market charges implied variance ABOVE realized on average (the VRP)
vrp = 0.04**2                                  # ~4 vol-points of premium, squared scale
implied_var = realized_var + vrp

print(f"realized vol = {np.sqrt(realized_var):.3f}")
print(f"implied  vol = {np.sqrt(implied_var):.3f}")
print(f"variance risk premium (impl²−real²) = {implied_var-realized_var:.4f}")

# short-variance P&L: collect (implied − realized) each period... until a vol spike
spike = np.r_[ret[:-5], rng.standard_normal(5)*0.08]      # a violent final week
shorted = implied_var/252 - spike**2*252/252             # per-day short-var carry
print("short-var: avg daily carry =", round(shorted[:-5].mean(),5),
      " | worst day =", round(shorted.min(),4), "<-- the steamroller")
```
Steady positive carry from the VRP, then a single catastrophic day — the short-vol payoff shape, in code.

**Linux:** `python varswap.py | tee var.log`; overlay realized vs implied on real VIX/SPX data to *see* the persistent (but occasionally inverting) premium.
