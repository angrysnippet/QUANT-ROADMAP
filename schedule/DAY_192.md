## Block 1 — Features: the real source of edge
A model can only learn from what you feed it. **Feature engineering** — turning raw market/alt data into informative, point-in-time, lookahead-safe inputs — is where domain knowledge becomes alpha. The toolkit (all functions of the *past* only, Day 141):
- **price-based:** returns over horizons, momentum, rolling vol (Day 143), z-scores, RSI-type oscillators,
- **volume/microstructure:** order-flow imbalance (Day 184), bid-ask spread, volume profile,
- **cross-sectional:** ranks, factor-neutralized residuals (Day 176),
- **fundamental / alt-data:** valuation ratios, sentiment, satellite/credit-card data (Day 197).

The discipline: every feature must be **stationary-ish** (Day 142), **lookahead-safe** (`.shift(1)`), and **economically motivated** (a reason it should predict, Day 149) — not data-mined. Garbage features → garbage model, no matter how fancy the algorithm.

---

## Block 2 — Fractional differentiation: stationarity *with* memory
A core tension (Day 142): prices are non-stationary (can't model directly), but full differencing (returns) makes them stationary at the cost of **erasing all memory** — returns are nearly memoryless, throwing away the level information that might predict. **Fractional differentiation** (López de Prado) resolves it: difference by a *fractional* order `d` (e.g. 0.4) instead of a whole `1`:
- `d = 0` → the raw price (full memory, non-stationary),
- `d = 1` → returns (stationary, no memory),
- `d ≈ 0.3–0.5` → the **minimum differencing that achieves stationarity while *retaining* maximum memory.**

You get a feature that's *both* stationary (passes ADF) *and* still carries predictive long-memory structure — the best of both. This single technique often improves financial ML features more than any model change.

**Concept check:** why not just use returns for everything? (Returns are stationary but ~memoryless (Day 141) — you've discarded the price *level* and long-range structure that may carry signal. Fractional differentiation keeps just enough memory to be predictive while still being stationary enough to model. It's the Goldilocks transform.)

## Block 3 — Labeling: the triple-barrier method
How do you define what the model predicts? The naive "will the return over the next `N` days be positive?" (the **fixed-horizon** label) is *badly* flawed — it ignores risk, path, and how you'd actually trade. The **triple-barrier method**: for each observation, set three barriers and label by *which is hit first*:
- **upper barrier** (profit-take) → label **+1**,
- **lower barrier** (stop-loss) → label **−1**,
- **vertical barrier** (time limit) → label by the sign at expiry (or 0).

This labels each event by the **realistic trading outcome** — did the profit-target or the stop hit first? — incorporating risk management (the stop) and path-dependence (Day 158) directly into the target. The barriers are usually scaled by **volatility** (Day 143) so they adapt to each regime. Far more tradeable than a fixed-horizon return.

## Block 4 — Mathematics · meta-labeling and sample weights
Two more professional techniques that punch above their weight:
- **Meta-labeling:** split the problem in two. A **primary model** (or a simple rule) decides the *side* (long/short); a **secondary ML model** decides *whether to act and how big* — it predicts the *probability the primary model is right*. This:
  - separates **direction** (hard, low-signal) from **bet-sizing/precision** (where ML adds a lot),
  - improves the **precision/F1** of the strategy (fewer false bets), and naturally produces a **position size** (∝ predicted probability, toward Kelly, Day 213),
  - lets you bolt ML onto an *existing* signal to filter its bad trades — a hugely practical pattern.
- **Sample weights for overlapping labels:** triple-barrier labels **overlap in time** (today's and tomorrow's outcomes share future price bars), so observations are **not independent** — violating a core ML assumption and inflating apparent performance. Fix by **weighting samples by their uniqueness** (down-weight overlapping labels) and **sequentially bootstrapping** — so the model isn't dominated by redundant, correlated examples. This directly addresses the "tiny effective sample" problem of Day 191.

These aren't exotic — they're the standard professional fixes for the ways financial labels differ from clean ML benchmarks.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the model is a commodity; the data prep is the craft
The defining mindset of professional finance ML: **the algorithm is the easy, commoditized part — `sklearn`/`xgboost` give you a world-class model in one line — while features and labels are the hard, high-value, domain-specific craft.** This inverts the amateur's instinct (obsess over the model, slap on raw features and naive labels). The professional spends 80% of effort on:
- **features** that are stationary-with-memory (fractional diff), lookahead-safe, economically motivated,
- **labels** that reflect *how you actually trade* (triple-barrier with vol-scaled stops, not arbitrary fixed horizons),
- **sample weights** that respect the *non-independence* of overlapping financial events,
- **meta-labeling** to separate direction from sizing.

This is the **López de Prado** thesis made concrete: most quant-ML failures trace to **bad labels and leaky features**, not weak algorithms. Garbage-in is the dominant failure mode, and the cure is craftsmanship in data preparation, not a fancier net. The recurring lesson of the whole roadmap — **the framework is the easy 20%, robust application is the hard 80%** (Day 147) — reaches its sharpest form here: in ML, *whoever prepares the data best wins*, and the model is almost an afterthought.

**Hard puzzle:** a colleague trains an excellent classifier to predict "will the 10-day forward return be positive?" with carefully tuned hyperparameters, but it loses money live. The features and CV are fine. What's likely wrong with the *labels*, and how would you fix it? (The **fixed-horizon label is the problem**, in several ways: (1) it **ignores the path** — a trade that hits a painful stop-loss on day 3 before recovering to a positive day-10 return is labeled +1, but you'd have been *stopped out* live → the label doesn't match tradeable reality (fix: **triple-barrier**, labeling by which of profit-take/stop/time-limit hits *first*). (2) It **ignores risk/volatility** — a fixed 10-day horizon means very different risk in calm vs volatile regimes (fix: **vol-scale the barriers**, Day 143). (3) The 10-day labels **overlap heavily** — consecutive observations share 9 of 10 future days → labels are highly correlated, the *effective* sample is tiny, and CV/performance are inflated (fix: **uniqueness sample weights + purged CV**, Days 191/193). (4) It mixes *direction* and *conviction* (fix: **meta-labeling** — let a primary rule pick the side, ML decide whether/how much to bet). The model was fine; the **target it was trained to predict didn't correspond to a real, risk-managed, tradeable outcome.** Relabeling with triple-barrier + meta-labeling + uniqueness weights often turns a losing classifier into a working strategy *without changing the model at all* — the clearest proof that labeling, not modeling, is the craft.)

**Career connection:** "you have price data and want to build an ML trading model — where do you start?" Strong answer: **features and labels, not the model.** Engineer stationary-but-memory-preserving features (**fractional differentiation**), all lookahead-safe and economically motivated; define labels with the **triple-barrier method** (vol-scaled profit-take/stop/time barriers) so the target matches a *tradeable, risk-managed* outcome; weight samples by **uniqueness** because overlapping labels aren't independent; and consider **meta-labeling** to separate side from size. The model itself is almost a commodity — `xgboost` with hard regularization. Leading with labeling/feature craft (citing triple-barrier and frac-diff) signals you've read *Advances in Financial ML* and understand where the real work is.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Fractional differentiation & triple-barrier labels
```python
import numpy as np, pandas as pd
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
price = pd.Series(100*np.exp(np.cumsum(rng.standard_normal(2000)*0.01)))

# --- fractional differentiation (fixed-width window) ---
def frac_diff(series, d, thresh=1e-4):
    w=[1.0]                                              # binomial weights
    k=1
    while abs(w[-1])>thresh and k<100:
        w.append(-w[-1]*(d-k+1)/k); k+=1
    w=np.array(w[::-1])
    out=series.rolling(len(w)).apply(lambda x: np.dot(w, x), raw=True)
    return out
fd = frac_diff(np.log(price), d=0.4).dropna()
from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller
print(f"ADF p-value of log-price : {adfuller(np.log(price))[1]:.3f}  (non-stationary)")
print(f"ADF p-value of frac-diff : {adfuller(fd)[1]:.3f}  (stationary, but keeps memory)")
print(f"corr(frac-diff, log-price)= {np.corrcoef(fd, np.log(price)[-len(fd):])[0,1]:.2f}  (memory retained!)")

# --- triple-barrier labels (vol-scaled) ---
ret = np.log(price).diff()
vol = ret.rolling(50).std()
def triple_barrier(price, vol, t, horizon=20, mult=2.0):
    p0=price[t]; up=p0*(1+mult*vol[t]); dn=p0*(1-mult*vol[t])
    for h in range(1, horizon+1):
        if t+h>=len(price): break
        if price[t+h]>=up: return 1
        if price[t+h]<=dn: return -1
    return int(np.sign(price[min(t+horizon,len(price)-1)]-p0))
labels=[triple_barrier(price.values, vol.values, t) for t in range(60, 1900)]
print("triple-barrier label distribution:", pd.Series(labels).value_counts().to_dict())
```
Fractional differencing yields a feature that's *stationary* (ADF rejects) yet still strongly correlated with the price level (**memory retained**), and triple-barrier labels each event by the *first barrier hit* — a tradeable, risk-aware target.

**Linux:** `python features.py | tee feat.log`; sweep `d` and find the *minimum* that achieves stationarity — that's the memory-preserving sweet spot.

## Quant Thinking Track — Garbage in, garbage out (the eternal law)
Across all of engineering and science, the quality of inputs caps the quality of outputs — and nowhere is the leverage of *input craftsmanship* higher than in low-signal domains like finance. The discipline of investing your effort where it compounds most — features and labels, not model tweaking — is a hallmark of the experienced practitioner. Make the data tell the truth about how you'd actually trade, and even a simple model can win.
