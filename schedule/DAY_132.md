## Block 1 — The loss defines the goal
Training minimises a loss `L(prediction, truth)`. Change the loss → change what the model considers "good." Choosing it well is a modelling decision, not a default.

*Why it matters:* a model with 99% directional accuracy can still **lose money** if its rare wrong calls are huge — accuracy ≠ profit.

---

## Block 2 — The standard menu
| Loss | Formula | Use / behaviour |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| **MSE** | `mean((ŷ−y)²)` | regression; punishes big errors hard → sensitive to outliers |
| **MAE** | `mean|ŷ−y|` | regression; robust to outliers |
| **Huber** | MSE near 0, MAE in tails | best of both — common for noisy finance data |
| **Cross-entropy** | `−Σ y log ŷ` | classification (up/down); penalises confident wrong calls |

**Concept check:** returns have fat tails/outliers — MSE or MAE/Huber? (MAE/Huber — MSE lets a few extreme days dominate the fit.)

---

## Block 3 — Why accuracy is a trap in trading
Directional accuracy ignores **magnitude** and **asymmetry**. A strategy right 70% of the time on tiny gains but catastrophically wrong 30% on huge losses is a disaster. The right objective is tied to **P&L / risk-adjusted return**, not hit-rate.

---

## Block 4 — Custom losses for finance
Quants often design losses that reflect economics:
- **Return-weighted loss:** weight errors by trade size / volatility.
- **Asymmetric loss:** penalise losses more than missed gains (risk aversion).
- **Directly optimise (negative) Sharpe** or P&L of the implied strategy.

**Exercise:** you predict returns to *trade* them. Why might minimising MSE be suboptimal vs maximising the strategy's Sharpe? (MSE treats all errors equally; it may nail small moves and fumble the big, tradeable ones. Optimise the thing you actually earn.)

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking (Optimise what you're paid for)

- **"You get what you optimise."** If you train on accuracy, you get accuracy — not money.
- **Regularization is part of the objective:** `loss + λ·penalty` (Day 127) bakes "don't overfit" into the goal.
- **Beware metric–objective mismatch:** the classic failure is a model that scores great on the loss but loses money because the loss didn't model costs/slippage/risk.

**Hard puzzle:** two models — A minimises MSE of returns; B maximises backtested Sharpe net of costs. Same data. Which would you deploy, and what's the risk with B? (B is aligned with the goal — but it's *easier to overfit* a complex P&L objective, so guard with walk-forward + few trials.)

**Career connection:** "what loss would you use to train a return predictor you intend to trade?" → discuss P&L/Sharpe-aligned losses, robustness to fat tails (Huber), and costs — not a reflexive "MSE."

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Loss choice changes the fit
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
y = rng.normal(0,1,200); y[:5] = 25     # a few outliers
# MSE-optimal estimate = mean; MAE-optimal = median
print("mean (MSE):", round(y.mean(),3), " median (MAE):", round(np.median(y),3))
```
The mean is dragged by outliers; the median (MAE-optimal) ignores them — concrete proof the loss picks the answer.

**Linux:** log both training loss *and* the business metric (Sharpe) each epoch — they can diverge.

## Quant Thinking Track — The Objective Is a Choice
The most consequential modelling decision is often *what you optimise*, not *how*. Align the loss with the real-world payoff, or the model will cheerfully optimise the wrong thing.
