## Block 1 — The time value of money
Money has a rental price: the **interest rate**. So cash flows at different times aren't comparable until you move them to a common date:
```
Future value:   FV = PV · (1 + r)^n          (or PV·e^{rt} continuously)
Present value:  PV = FV / (1 + r)^n
```
The **discount factor** `DF(t) = 1/(1+r)^t` (or `e^{−rt}`) is the price *today* of \$1 delivered at time `t`. Every valuation in finance — bonds, stocks (DCF), options, projects — is *the sum of future cash flows times their discount factors.* Master this and you can value anything.

*Why it matters:* "what's it worth?" always reduces to "what are the cash flows, and what do I discount them at?" Fixed income is where that question is purest, because the cash flows are *contractually fixed*.

---

## Block 2 — A bond is a stream of cash flows
A standard **coupon bond** pays periodic coupons `C` and returns the **face/par value** `F` at maturity `T`:
```
Price = Σ_{t=1}^{N}  C/(1+y)^t   +   F/(1+y)^N
```
- **Coupon rate** — the contractual `C/F` (fixed at issue).
- **Face value** — usually \$100 or \$1000, repaid at maturity.
- **Zero-coupon bond** — no coupons, just `F` at `T`; price `= F/(1+y)^N`. The purest discount instrument and the building block for everything (Day 164).

**Concept check:** a 1-year zero with face \$100 trades at \$95.24. Its yield? (`100/95.24 − 1 ≈ 5.0%`. The price *is* the discount factor times face — the market quoting you `DF(1)`.)

---

## Block 3 — Yield to maturity (YTM)
**YTM** is the single discount rate `y` that makes the present value of all the bond's cash flows equal its market price — the bond's *internal rate of return* if held to maturity (and coupons reinvested at `y`). It's the bond-market analogue of implied vol (Day 156): a *quoting convention*, one number summarizing a price. You solve for it numerically (no closed form), exactly like IV. Higher price ⇒ lower YTM, and vice versa — they're two ways of saying the same thing.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the price–yield relationship
Price is a **decreasing, convex** function of yield:
```
dP/dy < 0   (price falls as yields rise — the fundamental inverse)
d²P/dy² > 0 (the curve bends upward — convexity, Day 163)
```
Why inverse? A bond's coupons are *fixed*; if market rates rise, your fixed coupons look less attractive, so the price must drop until the bond's yield matches the market. Three corollaries you must own:
- **Par:** price = face ⟺ coupon rate = YTM.
- **Premium:** price > face ⟺ coupon > YTM (you pay up for above-market coupons).
- **Discount:** price < face ⟺ coupon < YTM.
- **Pull to par:** as `t → T`, price → face regardless of yield path.

This inverse price–yield relationship is the single most important fact in fixed income — *every* rates trade is ultimately a bet on it.

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · discounting unifies all of finance
Step back: **a stock's DCF value, a bond's price, and an option's premium are the same operation** — sum of (cash flow × discount factor), where for risky/optional payoffs you take a *risk-neutral expectation* first (Day 153). Fixed income is the cleanest classroom because the cash flows are known; once you can price a bond, a DCF equity model is "the same but with uncertain cash flows," and an option is "the same but with a payoff that depends on a distribution." **Valuation = forecast the cash flows, then discount them.** Everything else is detail about *which* cash flows and *which* discount rate.

**Hard puzzle:** two bonds, same issuer, same maturity. Bond A has a 2% coupon, Bond B a 8% coupon. Rates rise sharply. Which loses *more* value in percent, and why? (Bond A. Its value is concentrated in the *distant* face-value payment (low coupons mean little cash comes early), so it has **longer duration** (Day 162) — more sensitivity to rates. High coupons return cash sooner, shortening effective maturity and cushioning the drop. This is the seed of duration: *when* the cash flows arrive determines rate risk, not just *how much*.)

**Career connection:** "if interest rates rise, what happens to bond prices, and which bonds are hit hardest?" Strong answer: prices fall (the inverse price–yield relation — fixed coupons must reprice to market), and the hardest hit are **long-maturity, low-coupon** bonds because their cash flows are furthest out, i.e. longest duration. Tying the intuition to *timing of cash flows* (not just maturity) signals real understanding.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Price a bond and solve its YTM
```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import brentq

def bond_price(face, coupon_rate, ytm, n, freq=2):
    c = face*coupon_rate/freq
    t = np.arange(1, n*freq+1)
    return np.sum(c/(1+ytm/freq)**t) + face/(1+ytm/freq)**(n*freq)

def ytm(price, face, coupon_rate, n, freq=2):
    return brentq(lambda y: bond_price(face,coupon_rate,y,n,freq)-price, 1e-6, 1.0)

# 5-year, 4% coupon, semiannual
P = bond_price(100, 0.04, 0.05, 5)
print(f"price at 5% YTM = {P:.4f}  (discount bond: coupon 4% < yield 5%)")
print(f"recovered YTM   = {ytm(P,100,0.04,5):.4%}")

# the inverse price–yield curve
for y in (0.02,0.04,0.06,0.08):
    print(f"YTM={y:.0%}  price={bond_price(100,0.04,y,5):7.3f}")
```
You'll see price fall as yield rises, par exactly when coupon = yield (4%), and the YTM solver invert the price — the bond-market mirror of the implied-vol workflow.

**Linux:** `python bond.py | tee bond.log`; plot price vs yield to *see* the downward, convex curve that the rest of this module dissects.
