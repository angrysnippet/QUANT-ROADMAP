## Block 1 — The credit spread
A corporate (or any risky) bond yields **more** than a government bond of the same maturity. That extra yield is the **credit spread**:
```
Corporate yield = Risk-free yield + Credit spread
```
The spread compensates for **expected loss** (default probability × loss given default) **plus** a **credit risk premium** (compensation for the *uncertainty* and its bad timing — defaults cluster in recessions, when your other assets are also hurting). As a first approximation:
```
Spread ≈ PD × LGD    (+ risk premium)
```
where `PD` = probability of default, `LGD` = loss given default = `1 − recovery rate`. Wider spread ⇒ market sees more default risk (or demands more premium for it).

*Why it matters:* the entire corporate bond, loan, and CDS market is the business of pricing this spread — and getting `PD`, `LGD`, and the premium right.

---

## Block 2 — Default probability and recovery
- **Probability of default (PD):** the chance the issuer fails to pay over a horizon. Two flavors: **risk-neutral PD** (backed out of bond/CDS prices, used for *pricing*) vs **real-world PD** (from rating agencies / historical default rates, used for *risk*). Risk-neutral PD > real-world PD (the credit risk premium again — the Q-vs-P gap, Day 153).
- **Recovery rate:** what you reclaim in default (historically ~40% for senior unsecured) → `LGD ≈ 60%`. Seniority matters: secured > senior > subordinated.

**Concept check:** a 1-year bond yields 6%, the risk-free rate is 4%, recovery is 0%. Implied risk-neutral 1-year PD? (Spread = 2%; with zero recovery, `PD ≈ spread/(1) ≈ 2%`. With 40% recovery, `PD ≈ spread/LGD = 2%/0.6 ≈ 3.3%` — lower recovery means a given spread implies *less* default; you must scale by LGD.)

## Block 3 — The hazard rate (reduced-form view)
Model default as a *random arrival* with intensity **`λ`** — the **hazard rate**, the instantaneous probability of defaulting given survival so far (a Poisson process). Survival to time `t` is `e^{−λt}`, so:
```
Survival probability S(t) = e^{−λt}
Risky discount factor    ≈ e^{−(r+λ·LGD)t}
```
Default just adds `λ·LGD` to the discount rate — **credit risk is an extra spread on top of the curve.** This **reduced-form** approach (Jarrow–Turnbull) is the workhorse for pricing CDS and credit bonds: calibrate `λ` (the hazard/default-intensity curve) from market spreads, exactly as you bootstrap a yield curve (Day 164).

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the Merton model (a bond is an option!)
The **structural** view (Merton, 1974) is one of the most elegant ideas in finance. Model the firm's assets `V` as a GBM (Day 140). The firm defaults at maturity if assets fall below the debt face `D`. Then:
- **Equity = a call option on the firm's assets**, strike `D`: shareholders get `max(V − D, 0)` (limited liability — they walk away if `V < D`).
- **Risky debt = risk-free debt − a put on the assets**, strike `D`: bondholders are *short a put* — they absorb the downside if the firm's value collapses.
```
Risky bond = Risk-free bond − Put(V, D)
Equity     = Call(V, D)
```
So **Black–Scholes prices credit.** Default probability = `N(−d₂)` (the put's ITM probability, Day 153), the credit spread comes straight out of the option value, and **leverage + asset volatility** drive default risk. This is the engine behind **Moody's KMV / distance-to-default** models. The whole curriculum converges: probability + bonds + options are *one subject*, and a corporate balance sheet is an options position.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · everything is a contingent claim
Merton's reframe is the deepest unification you'll meet: **a company's capital structure is a portfolio of options on its assets.** Equity holders are long a call (upside, limited downside), debt holders are short a put (they eat the tail), and this single picture explains:
- why **equity vol rises as a firm levers up** (the call gets more OTM-sensitive),
- why **credit spreads widen when equity vol spikes** (the put is worth more) — the equity-credit linkage that capital-structure arbitrage trades,
- why **distress is non-linear** (convexity in the firm's value, Day 151).
Once you see balance sheets as option positions, credit, equity, and vol stop being separate desks and become *one risk* viewed from different seats. **That is quant thinking at its most powerful: a change of representation that collapses three problems into one.**

**Hard puzzle:** a firm's stock price is volatile and falling, and its bonds simultaneously sell off (spreads widen) even though no default has occurred. Connect the two with Merton. (Falling, more-volatile equity ⇒ the firm's *asset value* is dropping toward the debt barrier `D` and *asset volatility* is rising. In Merton, bondholders are **short a put** on the assets — lower assets + higher vol both make that put *more valuable*, so the debt is worth *less* and spreads widen. Equity and credit are two claims on the *same* asset process, so they move together in distress — the basis for **capital-structure arbitrage** (e.g. hedging bonds with equity/equity-options). The non-obvious link is that *equity volatility* is a credit signal.)

**Career connection:** "how do you think about a corporate bond's risk?" Strong answer: decompose the yield into risk-free + spread, where spread ≈ PD × LGD + a risk premium; model default either reduced-form (a hazard rate `λ` calibrated from market spreads) or structural (**Merton**: equity = a call on assets, risky debt = risk-free debt minus a put, default = `N(−d₂)`), which ties credit spreads to leverage and asset volatility. Bringing up Merton's "debt = short a put" unprompted is a standout signal.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Merton distance-to-default & implied spread
```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import norm

def merton(V, D, r, sigma_V, T):
    d1 = (np.log(V/D) + (r+0.5*sigma_V**2)*T)/(sigma_V*np.sqrt(T))
    d2 = d1 - sigma_V*np.sqrt(T)
    equity = V*norm.cdf(d1) - D*np.exp(-r*T)*norm.cdf(d2)   # call on assets
    pd     = norm.cdf(-d2)                                   # risk-neutral default prob
    debt   = V - equity                                      # risky debt value
    rf_debt= D*np.exp(-r*T)
    spread = -np.log(debt/D)/T - r                           # credit spread
    return dict(equity=round(equity,2), default_prob=round(pd,4),
                dist_to_default=round(d2,2), spread_bps=round(spread*1e4,1))

# firm: assets 120, debt 100, asset vol 25%, 1y
print("healthy firm :", merton(120,100,0.03,0.25,1))
print("distressed   :", merton(105,100,0.03,0.45,1))   # closer to barrier + higher vol
```
The distressed firm — assets nearer the debt barrier *and* higher asset vol — shows a far higher default probability and a much wider spread, exactly the equity-vol→credit linkage of Block 5.

**Linux:** `python merton.py | tee merton.log`; raise `sigma_V` alone and watch the spread widen with *no* change in leverage — volatility *is* credit risk.
