## Block 1 — Types of forecasts
A forecast is more than a single number:
- **point forecast** — the best single estimate (`E[x_{t+h} | data]`),
- **interval / density forecast** — a *distribution* or prediction interval capturing uncertainty (e.g. "90% chance the return is in [−2%, +3%]"). **Far more useful** than a point — finance is about *risk*, so you need the *uncertainty*, not just the central estimate (GARCH gives you exactly this for variance, Day 202),
- **multi-horizon** — forecasts at different horizons `h`, often with widening uncertainty (Day 201: forecast information decays with horizon).

In finance, the **density/interval forecast is usually what matters** — VaR (Day 139), option pricing, and position sizing all need the *distribution* of outcomes, not a point. A point forecast of return without its uncertainty is nearly useless for decision-making.

*Why it matters:* the whole value of a forecast for risk and sizing is in its *uncertainty quantification* — a model that says "+1% ± 0.1%" and one that says "+1% ± 5%" demand completely different actions.

## Block 2 — Evaluation metrics
How good is a forecast? Standard metrics:
- **MSE / RMSE** — mean (root) squared error; penalizes large errors heavily (sensitive to outliers/tails).
- **MAE** — mean absolute error; more robust to outliers, treats errors linearly.
- **MAPE** — percentage error; scale-free but breaks near zero.
- **directional accuracy** — fraction of times you got the *sign* right (often what matters for trading — you can have poor RMSE but profitable directional calls, and vice versa).
- **for density forecasts:** **log-likelihood / log-score**, **CRPS** (continuous ranked probability score), and **calibration** checks (do your 90% intervals contain the truth 90% of the time?).

The crucial point: **choose the metric that matches your objective.** For trading, *directional accuracy* and *economic P&L* often matter more than RMSE — a forecast can have a worse RMSE but make money because it gets the *direction and the big moves* right. Optimize and evaluate on what you actually care about (Day 145's "the metric frames the judgment").

## Block 3 — Honest evaluation: respect the arrow of time
Time-series forecast evaluation has its own version of the lookahead/overfitting traps (Days 141, 193, 199):
- **out-of-sample only** — *never* judge a forecast on the data it was fit to (in-sample fit is meaningless, Day 191),
- **walk-forward / expanding-window** — train on the past, forecast the next period, roll forward — the *only* realistic simulation of live forecasting; you can *never* shuffle time-series folds (Day 193),
- **respect time strictly** — no future information in features, no peeking; the forecast at `t` uses *only* data through `t` (lookahead, Day 141),
- **account for multiple models tried** — if you tested many models and picked the best forecaster, deflate for the search (Day 199's selection bias applies to forecasting too).

The discipline is identical to the rest of the curriculum: **the only forecast performance that means anything is genuine, walk-forward, out-of-sample, trial-adjusted.** Everything else flatters.

**Concept check:** your model has fantastic in-sample fit (R² = 0.9) but poor walk-forward out-of-sample performance. What happened, and which number do you trust? (Classic **overfitting** (Day 191) — the model memorized in-sample noise that doesn't generalize. **Trust the walk-forward out-of-sample** number completely; the in-sample R² is meaningless for predicting live performance. High in-sample + low OOS is the universal signature of a model fit to noise.)

## Block 4 — Mathematics · benchmarks, the Diebold–Mariano test, and forecast combination
Three rigorous tools for honest forecast evaluation:
- **The naïve benchmark is brutal:** for many financial series, the **random walk** ("tomorrow = today") or the **historical mean** is *shockingly hard to beat* — because returns are ~unpredictable (Day 141/150). So the real question is never "is my forecast good?" but "**does it beat the naïve benchmark out-of-sample?**" A complex model that can't beat "tomorrow = today" has *negative* value (it cost effort and adds risk). Always benchmark against the random walk / mean.
- **Diebold–Mariano test:** are two forecasts' accuracies *statistically significantly* different, or is the difference just noise? It tests whether one model genuinely forecasts better than another (or the benchmark) — preventing you from chasing a model that's "better" only by luck (the significance discipline of Day 124).
- **Forecast combination:** *averaging* multiple forecasts often **beats any individual one** — the same diversification principle as portfolios (Day 146), signals (Day 177), and ensembles (Day 194). Combining decorrelated forecasts cancels their idiosyncratic errors. The "forecast combination puzzle": a *simple average* is frustratingly hard to beat with fancy optimal weights (the 1/N lesson again, Day 147) — because estimating optimal combination weights overfits. **When in doubt, average your forecasts equally.**

The unifying mathematics: forecasting is subject to the *same* statistical disciplines as everything else — beat a benchmark, test significance, diversify (combine), and deflate for trials. The metrics change but the integrity requirements don't.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · a forecast without honest evaluation is worse than no forecast
The capstone discipline of forecasting — and a fitting near-end to the time-series module — is that **a forecast you haven't honestly evaluated is worse than no forecast at all**, because it gives *false confidence* that drives bad decisions with real money. This crystallizes the module's (and curriculum's) deepest theme: **the rigor of your evaluation matters more than the cleverness of your model.** Concretely:
- **always benchmark** against the naïve forecast (random walk / mean) — in finance these are brutally strong, and a model that can't beat them is *negative-value* (Day 141),
- **evaluate out-of-sample, walk-forward, respecting time** — in-sample fit is self-deception (Days 191/193),
- **test significance** (Diebold–Mariano) — don't chase noise-level "improvements" (Day 124),
- **match the metric to the objective** — directional accuracy/P&L for trading, calibration for risk, not just RMSE (Day 145),
- **quantify uncertainty** — a point forecast without its interval is nearly useless for risk/sizing (the whole value is the distribution),
- **deflate for models tried** — picking the best of many forecasters inflates apparent skill (Day 199),
- **combine forecasts** — a simple average diversifies error and usually beats any single model (Days 146/177).

This is the *same* intellectual-honesty spine that runs through honest backtesting (174), purged CV (193), the deflated Sharpe (199), and skill-vs-luck (149): **the central skill is not building forecasts but correctly judging whether they're real** — and in finance, where signal is faint and benchmarks are strong, most forecasts honestly evaluated turn out to add little or nothing, which is itself the most valuable finding (it stops you from trading noise). The forecaster's maturity is measured by their willingness to discover — and *accept* — that their clever model doesn't beat "tomorrow = today," and to act on that finding rather than rationalize it away. A forecast is a *decision input*; an unevaluated forecast is a *liability dressed as an asset*. **Evaluate honestly, benchmark ruthlessly, quantify uncertainty, combine, and be willing to conclude "no edge."** That discipline — not the model — is what makes forecasting trustworthy.

**Hard puzzle:** you build a sophisticated LSTM (Day 196) to forecast next-day S&P 500 returns. Walk-forward out-of-sample, it achieves an RMSE *slightly lower* than a random walk and 51.5% directional accuracy. Your colleague is excited. How do you evaluate whether this is real and tradeable? (Careful, layered skepticism — a "slightly better than random walk" result is *exactly* what noise produces, so the burden of proof is high: **(1) Is the improvement statistically significant?** Run a **Diebold–Mariano test** vs the random-walk benchmark — a marginally lower RMSE over a finite sample is very likely **within noise**; if DM doesn't reject, there's *no* evidence of real skill (Day 124). 51.5% directional accuracy is *barely* above coin-flip and needs a significance test against 50% given the sample size — it's probably not significant. **(2) Deflate for the search** (Day 199): how many architectures/hyperparameters/features were tried to land on this LSTM? If you searched extensively, a 51.5%/marginal-RMSE result is *expected under pure noise* (`√(2 ln N)` selection bias) → deflated, it's likely zero. **(3) Is it tradeable net of costs?** (Day 179) Even *if* 51.5% directional accuracy is real, the **edge per trade is tiny** — does it survive transaction costs and the bid-ask spread (Day 181)? A 1.5% directional edge on daily returns almost certainly **doesn't clear costs** → untradeable even if statistically real (the signal lives below the spread, Day 181). **(4) Is it robust across regimes?** (Day 204) Or driven by one period? Check sub-period stability. **(5) Economic rationale?** (Day 149) Why should an LSTM on raw returns predict the most-efficient index on earth? Without a mechanism, presume overfit. **(6) Right benchmark?** Beating random-walk RMSE on *returns* is a low bar that even noise clears occasionally; the meaningful test is *risk-adjusted P&L net of costs, out-of-sample, deflated*. **Verdict:** almost certainly **not real or not tradeable** — a marginal RMSE improvement and 51.5% accuracy on daily index returns, especially after a model search, is the textbook signature of noise/overfit (the LSTM-on-returns warning of Day 196), and even if marginally real, the edge is too small to beat costs. The *correct, mature conclusion* is likely "**no tradeable edge**" — and the discipline is to *accept that* rather than let excitement and sunk effort manufacture a strategy from noise. The colleague's excitement is the danger; honest evaluation is the antidote. This single problem applies the entire module's evaluation discipline.)

**Career connection:** "you have a time-series forecast — how do you know it's good?" Strong answer: **benchmark it ruthlessly out-of-sample** — first against the **naïve forecast** (random walk / historical mean), which in finance is brutally hard to beat (returns are ~unpredictable); evaluate **walk-forward, respecting the arrow of time** (never shuffle, no lookahead); use the **metric that matches the objective** (directional accuracy / net P&L for trading, calibration for risk — not just RMSE); test whether the improvement is **statistically significant** (Diebold–Mariano) rather than noise; **deflate for the number of models tried**; quantify **uncertainty** (intervals, not just points); and consider **combining** forecasts (a simple average usually beats any single one). Crucially, I'd be willing to conclude **"no edge"** — most forecasts honestly evaluated don't beat the naïve benchmark net of costs, and accepting that is the valuable finding. Framing evaluation rigor (benchmark, walk-forward, significance, deflation, combination) — and the willingness to conclude no-edge — as *more important than the model* is the senior answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Walk-forward evaluation vs the naïve benchmark
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# a series with WEAK real structure (slight AR(1)) + noise
T=2000; x=np.zeros(T)
for t in range(1,T): x[t]=0.05*x[t-1] + rng.standard_normal()   # tiny predictability

def walk_forward(x, model_fn, start=1000):
    preds, naive, actual = [], [], []
    for t in range(start, len(x)-1):
        preds.append(model_fn(x[:t+1]))      # forecast x[t+1] using data through t
        naive.append(x[t])                   # naive: "tomorrow = today" (random walk)
        actual.append(x[t+1])
    return map(np.array, (preds, naive, actual))

ar1 = lambda h: 0.05*h[-1]                    # the (true) AR(1) forecaster
preds, naive, actual = walk_forward(x, ar1)
rmse_model = np.sqrt(np.mean((preds-actual)**2))
rmse_naive = np.sqrt(np.mean((naive-actual)**2))
print(f"model RMSE : {rmse_model:.4f}")
print(f"naive RMSE : {rmse_naive:.4f}  (random walk benchmark)")
print(f"model beats naive? {rmse_model < rmse_naive}  by {(1-rmse_model/rmse_naive)*100:.1f}%")
print(f"directional accuracy: {np.mean(np.sign(preds)==np.sign(actual)):.1%}")

# Diebold–Mariano-style check: is the RMSE difference significant?
d = (naive-actual)**2 - (preds-actual)**2
dm = d.mean()/(d.std()/np.sqrt(len(d)))
print(f"DM stat: {dm:.2f}  ({'significant' if abs(dm)>1.96 else 'NOT significant'} edge over naive)")
```
The walk-forward harness benchmarks the model against the random walk and tests whether any improvement is *statistically significant* (DM) — the honest evaluation that separates real forecasting skill from noise.

**Linux:** `python forecast_eval.py | tee fc.log`; set the AR coefficient to 0 (pure noise) and watch the model *fail* to beat naive — evaluation correctly reporting no edge.

## Quant Thinking Track — The benchmark is the truth-teller
The single most clarifying question in forecasting (and much of analysis) is: **"does it beat the naïve benchmark, significantly, out-of-sample?"** The naïve benchmark — random walk, historical mean, simplest baseline — is a ruthless truth-teller that deflates most clever models. Cultivating the reflex to *always* compare against the dumb baseline, and to *accept* when you don't beat it, prevents an enormous fraction of self-deception. Sophistication that can't beat simplicity isn't sophistication; it's expensive noise.
