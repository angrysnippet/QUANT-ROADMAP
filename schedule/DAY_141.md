## Block 1 — What a time series *is*
A time series is `{x₁, x₂, …, x_T}` where order matters and observations are *not* i.i.d. — that's the whole point. Markets give us prices `Pₜ`, but we almost always model **returns**:
- Simple return: `rₜ = Pₜ/Pₜ₋₁ − 1`
- Log return: `ℓₜ = ln(Pₜ/Pₜ₋₁)` — additive across time (`ℓ₁+ℓ₂ = ln P₂/P₀`), symmetric, and what the math (GBM) is built on.

*Why it matters:* prices are non-stationary (they wander); returns are closer to stationary and comparable across assets and scales. **Rule: model returns, report prices.**

---

## Block 2 — Autocorrelation (the first thing you measure)
Autocorrelation at lag `k` = correlation of the series with itself shifted by `k`:
```
ρ(k) = Corr(xₜ, xₜ₋ₖ)
```
- A pure **random walk in prices** → returns have `ρ(k) ≈ 0` for all `k ≥ 1` (unpredictable).
- **Real return series:** ρ on returns is tiny (markets are efficient-ish), but ρ on **|returns|** or **returns²** is strongly positive and slow-decaying → *volatility clusters* (the GARCH story, Day 143).

*Why it matters:* the ACF is your first diagnostic — it tells you whether there's any linear memory to exploit, and it instantly distinguishes "no signal in the mean, lots of structure in the variance."

---

## Block 3 — Signal vs noise: the researcher's daily fight
Decompose conceptually: `observed = trend + seasonality + cycle + noise`. In finance the **noise dominates** and the signal is faint (an information ratio of 0.1 per trade can be a great strategy). The job is not to "predict the price" — it's to find a *tiny, persistent, statistically real* edge in a sea of noise without fooling yourself (Day 124 overfitting).

**Concept check:** if you fit a flexible model and it explains 60% of daily return variance in-sample, what's your prior? (Near-certain overfit / lookahead leak — real daily R² edges are often <1%.)

---

## Block 4 — Stationarity preview & lag features
A series is (weakly) **stationary** if its mean, variance, and autocovariance don't change over time — the precondition for most models to even make sense. Prices fail it; returns roughly pass it. Tomorrow (Day 142) makes this rigorous.

**Feature engineering = building from lags:** moving averages, momentum (`Pₜ/Pₜ₋ₙ − 1`), rolling volatility, rolling z-scores. Every classic signal is a function of the past — and must use **only** the past (no lookahead).

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the lookahead trap
The #1 way researchers lie to themselves: using information at time `t` that wasn't available until `t+1`. Examples: normalising by the full-sample mean/std, using today's close to trade today's close, survivorship-filtered universes (Day 123).

**Discipline:** every feature at time `t` must be computable from data up to and including `t` only. Backtests that ignore this look spectacular and lose money live.

**Hard puzzle:** you z-score returns using the *whole sample's* mean and std, then find the "extreme" days mean-revert. Why is the backtest worthless? (The mean/std embed the future — a point is only "extreme" relative to information you didn't have yet.)

**Career connection:** "how do you know your signal is real?" → out-of-sample, walk-forward, point-in-time data, and an economic reason it should exist. Time-series discipline *is* the quant-research interview.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Returns, ACF, and a lookahead-safe rolling z-score
```python
import numpy as np, pandas as pd
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
# simulate a price as a random walk in log space
ret = rng.standard_normal(2000) * 0.01
price = 100 * np.exp(np.cumsum(ret))
s = pd.Series(price)

logret = np.log(s).diff().dropna()

def acf(x, k):           # autocorr at lag k
    return np.corrcoef(x[:-k], x[k:])[0, 1]

print("ACF of returns  lag1:", round(acf(logret.values, 1), 4))   # ~0
print("ACF of |returns| lag1:", round(acf(np.abs(logret.values), 1), 4))

# lookahead-SAFE rolling z-score (uses only the past via shift)
roll = logret.rolling(50)
z = (logret - roll.mean().shift(1)) / roll.std().shift(1)
print("safe z-score sample:", z.dropna().iloc[:3].round(2).tolist())
```
The `.shift(1)` is the entire ballgame: it guarantees the statistic at time `t` excludes `t`.

**Linux:** `python ts.py | tee ts.log`; keep a `data/` dir with point-in-time CSVs so a re-run can't accidentally see the future.
