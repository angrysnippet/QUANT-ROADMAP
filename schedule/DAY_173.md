## Block 1 — The OU process *is* your spread model
Model the cointegrated spread `x_t` as Ornstein–Uhlenbeck — the exact same SDE as the Vasicek short rate (Day 168), now describing a tradeable spread:
```
dx = θ(μ − x) dt + σ dW
```
- `μ` = the **equilibrium level** the spread reverts to (≈ 0 for a demeaned spread),
- `θ` = the **speed of mean reversion** (bigger θ = snappier),
- `σ` = the spread's volatility.

The `θ(μ−x)` drift is the restoring force: above `μ` it pushes down, below it pushes up. **This is the continuous-time version of the ECM** — `θ` is the speed of adjustment `γ` from Day 172. Your whole strategy is now a parametrized model you can fit and optimize.

*Why it matters:* once the spread is an OU process, "how long will the trade take?" and "how extreme is extreme?" become formulas, not guesses.

---

## Block 2 — Half-life: your holding period
The single most useful number from OU is the **half-life** — the expected time for a deviation to decay halfway back to the mean:
```
half-life = ln(2) / θ
```
Estimate `θ` by regressing the spread's *change* on its *lagged level* (the discrete ECM):
```
Δx_t = a + b·x_{t−1} + ε   →   θ ≈ −b,   half-life = −ln(2)/b
```
Interpretation:
- **short half-life** (days) → fast reversion → high-turnover, more trades, smaller edge per trade, costs matter a lot,
- **long half-life** (months) → slow reversion → fewer trades, more capital tied up, more breakdown risk.

The half-life sets your **rebalance frequency, capital allocation, and whether the edge survives transaction costs** (Day 179).

**Concept check:** you fit `b = −0.05` (daily). Half-life? (`−ln2 / −0.05 ≈ 14 days`. Expect ~2-week round-trips; a spread with a 6-month half-life would be far less attractive — slow, and more likely to break before reverting.)

---

## Block 3 — How far does it wander? (the stationary distribution)
An OU process has a **stationary distribution** — Normal with mean `μ` and variance:
```
Var(x) = σ² / (2θ)
```
So the spread fluctuates within a predictable band; the z-score (Day 171) is just `x` standardized by this stationary std. This tells you:
- a `z = +2` deviation is genuinely a ~2-sigma stretch you expect to revert,
- the *equilibrium variance* `σ²/2θ` caps how wide the spread "should" get — a deviation far beyond it signals **the model is breaking**, not a bigger opportunity (the OU assumptions no longer hold → exit, don't double down).

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · optimal entry and exit
With an OU model you can move from ad-hoc "|z|>2 enter, z=0 exit" to *optimized* thresholds. The trade-off:
- **wider entry threshold** (e.g. z>2.5) → fewer trades, but each has a larger expected reversion (bigger edge per trade) and higher hit-rate,
- **narrower entry** (z>1) → more trades, smaller edge each, more turnover/costs,
- **exit** can be at the mean (z=0) or earlier (capture most of the reversion, free capital sooner, lower variance).

The formal version maximizes **expected profit per unit time** (or a Sharpe-like objective), netting **transaction costs** and the **half-life** — there are closed-form/dynamic-programming solutions (optimal double-threshold policies). Key insight: **the right thresholds depend on θ, σ, *and* costs together** — a fast, cheap-to-trade spread wants tight thresholds; a slow, expensive one wants wide ones. This is the Day 138 MDP/optimal-control mindset applied to one spread.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the same OU process, three times
Notice the beautiful recurrence: the **Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process now appears as (1) the Vasicek interest-rate model (Day 168), (2) the error-correction dynamics of a cointegrated pair (Day 172), and (3) the spread you trade today.** One piece of mathematics — mean reversion to an equilibrium with Gaussian shocks — underlies rates modeling, econometrics, *and* stat-arb. This is the payoff of a principles-first education: you learn the OU process *once* and it pays dividends across rates, time series, and trading. When you meet a new "it pulls back to a level" phenomenon, you already have the half-life, the stationary variance, and the optimal-threshold machinery ready. **Deep transferable structure beats memorizing a hundred special cases.**

**Hard puzzle:** spread A reverts with a 3-day half-life; spread B with a 60-day half-life. Both have the same z-score volatility and the same per-trade transaction cost. Which is the better strategy, and what could flip the answer? (Usually **A** — faster reversion means more independent round-trips per year → more *breadth* (`IR = IC·√Breadth`, Day 149) → higher Sharpe, *and* less time exposed to the relationship breaking (Day 178). BUT it flips if **costs dominate**: A's high turnover may bleed the edge away in transaction costs/impact (Day 179), while B's few, large reversions clear costs easily. So the answer depends on **edge-per-trade vs cost-per-trade × turnover** — the half-life is only attractive if the per-trade reversion beats the round-trip cost. Fast is better *until* it's too fast to pay for.)

**Career connection:** "you've found a cointegrated spread — how do you decide your holding period and entry levels?" Strong answer: fit the spread as an **OU process**, compute the **half-life** (`ln2/θ`) to set the time scale, use the stationary variance for z-score bands, and optimize entry/exit thresholds by trading off edge-per-trade against turnover and transaction costs — wide thresholds for slow/expensive spreads, tight for fast/cheap ones. Naming the OU half-life and the cost-vs-turnover trade-off is the practitioner's answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Fit OU, get half-life, simulate the trade
```python
import numpy as np, pandas as pd
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# simulate an OU spread
theta, mu, sigma, T, dt = 0.05, 0.0, 0.5, 2000, 1.0
x = np.zeros(T)
for t in range(1,T):
    x[t] = x[t-1] + theta*(mu-x[t-1])*dt + sigma*np.sqrt(dt)*rng.standard_normal()

# estimate theta & half-life by regressing Δx on lagged x  (the discrete ECM)
dx, xl = np.diff(x), x[:-1]
b = np.polyfit(xl, dx, 1)[0]
half_life = -np.log(2)/b
print(f"true θ={theta}  estimated θ≈{-b:.4f}  half-life≈{half_life:.1f} days")
print(f"stationary std (σ/√2θ) = {sigma/np.sqrt(2*theta):.3f}")

# z-score signal + simple P&L (enter |z|>2, exit at 0), net of cost
s = pd.Series(x); z = (s - s.rolling(60).mean().shift(1))/s.rolling(60).std().shift(1)
pos = np.zeros(T)
for t in range(1,T):
    if pos[t-1]==0:
        pos[t] = -1 if z[t]>2 else (1 if z[t]<-2 else 0)
    else:
        pos[t] = 0 if abs(z[t])<0.2 else pos[t-1]          # exit near mean
pnl = pos[:-1]*np.diff(x) - 0.001*np.abs(np.diff(pos))     # minus turnover cost
print(f"gross Sharpe ≈ {np.nanmean(pos[:-1]*np.diff(x))/np.nanstd(pos[:-1]*np.diff(x))*np.sqrt(252):.2f}")
```
You recover `θ` and the half-life from data, see the stationary band, and run a cost-aware threshold strategy on the simulated spread — OU theory turned into a P&L.

**Linux:** `python ou.py | tee ou.log`; vary `theta` and watch the half-life and optimal thresholds shift — fast spreads want tight bands.

## Quant Thinking Track — Parametrize, then optimize
OU upgrades stat arb from heuristics ("|z|>2") to a *parametrized model* you can fit and optimize. That's a universal escalation in quant work: replace a rule of thumb with a model whose parameters you estimate, so you can *reason* about half-life, variance, and optimal actions instead of guessing. A fitted model turns a strategy into something you can improve systematically.
