## Block 1 — Two kinds of impact
When you trade, the price reaction has two distinct components:
- **Temporary impact** — the *immediate, transient* cost of demanding liquidity. You consume depth and pay worse prices, but once you stop trading, the book **refills** and the price partly *recovers*. This is a **cost of immediacy** that you can *reduce by trading slower.*
- **Permanent impact** — the *lasting* shift in the price level because your trade **revealed information** (Kyle, Day 183) or genuinely moved the supply/demand equilibrium. This *doesn't* recover — the market now believes the fair value has changed.

```
Total impact = Temporary (recovers) + Permanent (stays)
```
**Why it matters:** temporary impact you can manage with execution speed; permanent impact you mostly can't. Optimal execution (Day 185) is about *minimizing the temporary part you're paying unnecessarily* while accepting the permanent part.

---

## Block 2 — Resilience: how fast the book heals
After you take liquidity, **resilience** is how quickly the order book refills and the price reverts toward its prior level. A resilient market (deep, many liquidity providers) heals fast → temporary impact decays quickly → you can trade again soon at low cost. An illiquid one heals slowly → you must *wait* between child orders or pay escalating impact. Resilience sets the **rhythm of execution**: trade, let the book recover, trade again. Trade faster than the book heals and you're climbing your own impact.

**Concept check:** you split a big buy into 10 child orders. Why pace them out rather than fire all at once? (Firing at once = walking deep into the book, paying large temporary impact on the whole order. Pacing lets the book **refill between children** (resilience), so each child hits a replenished book at lower marginal impact — you pay closer to the natural price. The cost of patience is *exposure to price drift* while you wait — the core execution trade-off, Day 185.)

---

## Block 3 — Order-flow imbalance predicts short-term moves
The book is informative *before* trades even complete. **Order-flow imbalance (OFI)** — the net pressure of buys vs sells, and the relative depth on the bid vs ask — is one of the most robust short-horizon predictors:
- more volume/depth building on the **bid** than the ask → upward pressure → price likely ticks up,
- aggressive market buys depleting the ask → imminent up-move.

This is the bread-and-butter **signal of high-frequency market making and execution** (Days 182, 186): read the book's imbalance, anticipate the next micro-move, and quote/trade ahead of it. It's the microstructure-level version of momentum, operating over milliseconds to seconds, and it's *why* the order book itself is alpha, not just cost (Day 181).

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the impact laws
Two empirical regularities govern impact, and they describe *different* things:
- **Cross-sectional / metaorder square-root law** (Day 179): the *total* impact of executing a large "metaorder" of size `Q` scales as
```
Impact ≈ Y·σ·√(Q/V)
```
sub-linear in size — doubling the order less-than-doubles per-share impact. Remarkably **universal** across markets, assets, and eras.
- **Linear (Kyle) instantaneous impact** (Day 183): the *marginal* price move per unit of net flow is `λ·(flow)` — linear at the tick level.

These reconcile via **impact decay**: each child order has an instantaneous (≈linear) impact that then **decays** over time (temporary part relaxing as the book heals), and the *aggregate* of many decaying child impacts over a metaorder produces the *concave √-shaped* total. Models like **transient/propagator impact** (Bouchaud) make this precise: `price moves = Σ G(t−s)·(flow_s)`, where the propagator `G` decays with time — capturing both that trades move price *and* that the move partly reverts. The √-law and Kyle's λ aren't contradictory; they're the *macro* and *micro* views of the same decaying-impact process (the recurring "same thing at two resolutions" theme, Days 140, 157).

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · impact is the cost of your own footprint
The unifying insight: **impact is the market reacting to *you*** — to the liquidity you consume (temporary) and the information you might carry (permanent). This makes execution fundamentally about **managing your footprint**:
- **trade slower** → less temporary impact (let resilience heal the book) but more **exposure to price drift/risk** while you wait — the precise trade-off Almgren–Chriss optimizes (Day 185),
- **disguise your flow** → less *perceived* information → less permanent impact and adverse selection (Day 183); break orders up, randomize, trade across venues (Day 189),
- **read the book** → use order-flow imbalance to *time* your trades into moments of natural liquidity, paying less.

The deep connection: **temporary impact is a liquidity cost (Day 181's spread, scaled up); permanent impact is an information cost (Day 183's λ).** Every large trade pays both, and execution is the discipline of minimizing the part that's avoidable (temporary, by patience and stealth) while accepting the part that isn't (permanent, the price of genuinely moving the market). Your trades leave footprints; the art is leaving the *smallest possible* one.

**Hard puzzle:** you need to buy a position equal to 30% of a stock's daily volume. You execute it all in the first 10 minutes and notice the price jumped 2%, then fell back 1.2% over the next hour. Decompose what happened and what it implies for next time. (The **2% jump = total impact at the moment of trading**; the **1.2% recovery = the temporary component** (you exhausted the book's depth demanding immediacy; once you stopped, **resilience** refilled it and the price reverted); the remaining **0.8% that *stuck* = permanent impact** (the market inferred information from your aggressive buying — Kyle's λ — and/or you genuinely shifted the equilibrium). Implication: you **paid 1.2% in avoidable temporary impact** by trading too fast relative to the book's resilience. Next time, **spread the order over hours** (e.g. Almgren–Chriss, Day 185) so each child hits a replenished book — converting much of that 1.2% temporary cost back into your pocket — accepting only the unavoidable ~0.8% permanent cost. The trade-off you accept for patience is *price-drift risk* over the longer execution window. This decomposition — and the speed/risk trade-off it reveals — *is* the execution problem.)

**Career connection:** "what happens to the price when you execute a large order, and how do you think about the cost?" Strong answer: impact splits into **temporary** (a transient cost from consuming liquidity that *recovers* as the book heals — its speed is **resilience**) and **permanent** (a lasting move because your trade revealed information, ~Kyle's λ); total metaorder impact follows the **square-root law** in size. So execution minimizes the *avoidable temporary* part by trading patiently and stealthily (less footprint) while accepting the permanent part — trading off impact against the price-drift risk of a slower schedule. Naming temporary/permanent, resilience, and the √-law is the complete answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Temporary vs permanent impact with a propagator
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# transient (propagator) impact model: each trade's impact decays over time
def simulate(child_flow, perm=0.3, decay=0.7):
    price = 100.0; prices=[]; transient=0.0
    for q in child_flow:
        transient = decay*transient + (1-perm)*0.01*q   # temporary part, decays each step
        permanent = perm*0.01*q                          # permanent part, sticks
        price += permanent
        prices.append(price + transient)                 # observed = level + transient bump
        price += 0                                       # permanent already folded in
    return np.array(prices)

# Strategy A: dump 3000 shares in 3 steps.  Strategy B: spread over 30 steps.
A = simulate(np.r_[np.full(3,1000), np.zeros(20)])
B = simulate(np.r_[np.full(30,100),  np.zeros(20)][:23])
print(f"FAST: peak price {A.max():.3f}, settles to {A[-1]:.3f}  (big temporary bump that reverts)")
print(f"SLOW: peak price {B.max():.3f}, settles to {B[-1]:.3f}  (smaller footprint, same permanent)")
print(f"avoidable temporary cost paid by FAST ≈ {A.max()-A[-1]:.3f} per share")
```
Both strategies move the price *permanently* by the same amount (same total size → same information), but the **fast** execution pays a large **temporary** bump that reverts — money lost to impatience, which the **slow** schedule avoids.

**Linux:** `python impact.py | tee impact.log`; raise `decay` (more resilient book) and watch the temporary cost of fast trading shrink — resilience rewards patience.

## Quant Thinking Track — Separate the recoverable from the permanent
A recurring analytical move: **decompose a cost into the part you can recover and the part you can't.** Temporary vs permanent impact, transient vs persistent shocks (Day 142's unit root), reversible vs irreversible decisions — in each case the strategy is *manage the recoverable aggressively, accept the permanent gracefully.* Knowing which is which is half of good decision-making.
