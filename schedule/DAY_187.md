## Block 1 — The order-type toolkit
Beyond plain market/limit, exchanges offer a tactical menu:
- **Market / Limit** — the basics (Day 181).
- **Marketable limit** — a limit priced to execute immediately (a limit at or through the opposite quote) — control your worst price while still taking liquidity.
- **Stop / stop-limit** — trigger an order when price crosses a level (risk control; but stops can cascade in a crash).
- **IOC (Immediate-Or-Cancel) / FOK (Fill-Or-Kill)** — take whatever's available now, cancel the rest (IOC) or require the whole size at once (FOK) — used to probe liquidity without leaving a resting footprint.
- **Iceberg / hidden** — show only a small piece of a large order (disguise size → reduce information leakage / impact, Day 184).
- **Pegged / mid-point** — track the bid/ask/mid automatically — common in dark pools (Day 189).

Each is a tool for a specific trade-off between **execution certainty, price, and information leakage.** Choosing the order type *is* tactical alpha.

## Block 2 — Queue position: first-in, first-out
At a given price level, orders fill by **price–time priority (FIFO)**: better price first, then *earlier arrival* first. So at the best bid, **where you stand in the queue** determines your fill probability:
- post early → near the front → you fill before the level trades away,
- post late → back of the queue → the price may move before you reach the front, and you *don't* fill (or fill only when the price is moving *against* you — adverse selection, Day 183).

**Queue position is a real, tradeable asset.** HFT firms compete fiercely for it (post the instant a level forms; some exchanges/order-types help hold priority). For a passive (maker) execution, getting good queue position can be the difference between earning the spread and missing the trade. This is why *speed* (Day 186) matters even for patient strategies — first to the queue, first to fill.

**Concept check:** you post a limit buy at the best bid, but you're last in a long queue. The price ticks up. Did you fill, and is that good or bad? (Probably **didn't fill** — the queue ahead of you didn't fully execute before the price rose, so the level emptied upward past you. That's the *adverse-selection* problem of queue position: you tend to fill *when you'd rather not* (price moving against you) and *miss* when you'd want to (price moving your way). Good queue position mitigates this; bad position means you're systematically selected against.)

## Block 3 — Reading order flow (the book as a signal)
The order book and the tape are *information*, and the tactical trader reads them:
- **Order-flow imbalance (OFI)** (Day 184) — net buy vs sell pressure and bid/ask depth ratio → short-term price-move predictor.
- **Trade signing** — classify each trade as buyer- or seller-initiated (did it hit the bid or lift the ask?) to gauge directional pressure.
- **Book pressure / depth** — large resting size on one side (support/resistance); thinning depth → impending move.
- **Cancellation patterns / spoofing awareness** — rapid posting-and-cancelling can be *fake* liquidity meant to mislead (spoofing is illegal but the patterns exist) — don't trust displayed depth blindly.

These micro-signals drive HFT market making and smart-order-routing decisions: *when* to post vs take, *where* in the book, and *how fast*. The book isn't just where you trade — it's a real-time forecast.

## Block 4 — Mathematics · maker–taker fees and the economics of posting
Exchanges use a **maker–taker fee model** that directly shapes tactics:
- **makers** (post resting liquidity that gets filled) often receive a small **rebate**,
- **takers** (cross the spread to execute now) pay a **fee**.

So your *true* execution cost includes the fee/rebate, not just the spread:
```
effective cost (take) = half-spread + taker fee
effective cost (make) = −rebate + (queue/adverse-selection risk + non-fill risk)
```
This creates a real optimization: **posting (making)** earns the spread *plus* a rebate but risks not filling and being adversely selected; **taking** guarantees the fill but pays the spread *plus* a fee. The decision depends on your **urgency** (Almgren–Chriss `λ_risk`, Day 185), the **fill probability** (queue position), and the **fee schedule**. Whole strategies ("rebate arbitrage") exist purely to harvest maker rebates. And fragmentation across venues with *different* fee schedules (Day 189) turns order *routing* into an optimization over price, fees, and fill probability simultaneously. The tactical layer is a genuine constrained optimization — small per-trade edges that, at high volume, are a business.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · alpha lives in the details at every scale
The lesson of the tactical layer: **edge exists at every resolution, and the finest scale is where execution alpha hides.** You've now seen the full zoom:
- **macro:** which assets/strategies (portfolio, stat arb — Phases 7, 10),
- **meso:** how fast to trade the whole order (Almgren–Chriss, Day 185),
- **micro:** which order type, which venue, where in the queue, when to post vs take (today).

Each level is a real optimization with real money at stake, and *they interact* — a great signal executed with poor tactics can underperform a mediocre signal executed well. This is why elite firms invest as heavily in *execution* as in *alpha research*: at scale, **basis points of execution improvement compound into more P&L than another marginal signal.** The meta-skill is recognizing that "implementation details" are not beneath you — at the cross-section of high volume and thin margins, the details *are* the edge. Queue position and order-type selection sound trivial; across millions of trades they're worth more than most signals. **Respect the last mile.**

**Hard puzzle:** a strategy's backtest assumes it always earns the spread by posting passive limit orders (acting as a maker, collecting the rebate). Why is this optimistic, and what determines whether it's realistic? (It assumes **you always fill at the favorable price**, but passive orders have two problems the backtest ignores: (1) **non-fill / queue risk** — when the price moves *your way* (the favorable case the backtest counts), the level often trades through *before you reach the front of the FIFO queue*, so you **don't fill on the good trades**; (2) **adverse selection** — you preferentially fill when the price is moving *against* you (informed flow lifts/hits your stale quote, Day 183), so your *realized* fills are systematically worse than "earn the spread." Whether posting is actually profitable depends on your **queue position** (speed/priority — Day 186), the **fill probability vs adverse-selection trade-off**, and the **rebate** offsetting it. A realistic backtest must model *which* orders fill and *when* — not assume the spread is free income. This is the maker's version of the lookahead trap: assuming you get the good fills and avoid the bad ones.)

**Career connection:** "you want to buy passively to earn the spread instead of paying it — what are the risks?" Strong answer: **non-fill and adverse selection** — with price–time (FIFO) priority, your **queue position** determines fill probability, and passive orders tend to *miss* when the price moves your way (you don't reach the front in time) and *fill* when it moves against you (informed flow picks off your quote). So "earning the spread" is offset by queue risk and adverse selection, partly compensated by the **maker rebate**. Whether to post or take depends on urgency, fill probability, and the fee schedule. Naming queue position, adverse selection, and maker–taker economics is the tactical-depth answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Queue position and fill probability
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# simulate a price level: your fill depends on queue position vs how much trades before price moves
def fill_outcome(my_queue_pos, level_size, n=50000):
    filled_good, filled_bad, missed = 0,0,0
    for _ in range(n):
        traded = rng.integers(0, level_size)             # how much executes at this level
        price_moves_up = rng.random()<0.5
        if traded >= my_queue_pos:                       # queue ahead of you cleared -> you fill
            if price_moves_up: filled_bad += 1           # filled as price rises against your buy
            else:              filled_good += 1
        else:
            missed += 1
    return filled_good/n, filled_bad/n, missed/n

for pos in (50, 500, 2000):                              # front, middle, back of a 2000-size level
    g,b,m = fill_outcome(pos, level_size=2000)
    print(f"queue pos {pos:5d}: good-fill {g:.0%}  adverse-fill {b:.0%}  missed {m:.0%}")
```
Near the **front** of the queue you fill often and more symmetrically; near the **back** you mostly *miss* — and the fills you do get are skewed toward adverse moves. Queue position materially changes execution quality.

**Linux:** `python queue.py | tee queue.log`; the front-of-queue advantage is exactly what HFT speed (Day 186) buys you.
