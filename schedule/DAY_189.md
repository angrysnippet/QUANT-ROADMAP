## Block 1 — Fragmentation: many venues, one stock
A single stock trades simultaneously across many venues:
- **Lit exchanges** (NYSE, Nasdaq, Cboe, LSE, …) — public limit order books with visible quotes.
- **Dark pools** — private venues that *don't* display quotes (Block 2).
- **Wholesalers / internalizers** — firms that execute retail flow in-house (much US retail order flow is "internalized").
- **ECNs / MTFs / alternative venues.**

So liquidity for one name is **scattered across many books.** This **fragmentation** is a double-edged sword: competition among venues tightens spreads and lowers fees (good), but it **splits liquidity** so no single book shows the whole picture (you must aggregate across venues to see true depth) and it creates cross-venue latency-arbitrage opportunities (Day 186).

*Why it matters:* you can't get best execution by looking at one venue. Routing across the fragmented landscape *is* modern execution.

---

## Block 2 — Lit vs dark
- **Lit venues:** quotes are **displayed** — pre-trade transparency. You see the bids/asks, but so does everyone — including those who might **front-run** or fade a large visible order (information leakage, Day 184).
- **Dark pools:** **no pre-trade transparency** — orders are hidden; trades print only *after* execution. The purpose: let institutions trade **large blocks without revealing intent**, minimizing the market impact of showing a big order (Day 184). Typically execute at the lit **mid-price** (price improvement vs crossing the lit spread).

The trade-off: dark pools reduce **information leakage** for big orders, but offer **less certainty of execution** (you only trade if a counterparty is also there) and raise concerns about **adverse selection** (who else is in the dark with you? are you being picked off?). Most institutional execution blends both: probe dark venues for size, work the remainder lit.

**Concept check:** a pension fund must sell 500,000 shares — 5% of daily volume. Why route to a dark pool first? (To **avoid information leakage**: displaying that size on a lit book would signal a large seller, inviting others to fade/front-run and pushing the price down *before* they're done (permanent + temporary impact, Day 184). A dark pool hides the intent — they trade at the mid against whoever's there, with minimal market signal. The cost is uncertain fill (the size may not be available in the dark) and possible adverse selection from informed counterparties also lurking there.)

## Block 3 — Smart order routing
With liquidity fragmented, a **Smart Order Router (SOR)** is essential: an algorithm that decides, in real time, **which venue(s) to send each child order to** — optimizing across:
- **price** (best available bid/ask across all venues — the consolidated best price),
- **liquidity/depth** (where the size actually is),
- **fees/rebates** (maker–taker differs by venue, Day 187),
- **fill probability and latency** (Day 186),
- **information leakage** (lit vs dark).

The SOR slices a parent order and routes pieces to wherever execution is best *right now*, often probing dark pools then sweeping lit venues. It's a live, multi-objective optimization — the practical engine that makes a fragmented market *usable*, and a serious piece of quant engineering.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the regulation that shapes the structure
Market structure is *engineered by regulation* — the rules are part of the system you must model:
- **Reg NMS (US, 2007):** the **Order Protection Rule** forbids trading through a better-displayed price on another venue (you must route to the best quote, the **NBBO** — National Best Bid and Offer). This *created* much of the fragmentation and the SOR industry (you must check all venues) and the latency arms race (be first to the best quote, Day 186).
- **MiFID II (Europe, 2018):** mandates **best execution**, **transparency** (caps on dark trading), and **unbundling** of research from execution — reshaping European liquidity.
- **Best execution duty:** brokers are *legally obligated* to seek the best outcome for clients across price, cost, speed, and likelihood — which is *why* SORs and TCA (Day 188) exist (you must *prove* best execution).
- **Tick-size regimes, the trade-through rule, payment for order flow (PFOF)** — each rule changes incentives and where/how liquidity forms.

The deep point: **the matching rules, priority rules, and regulations are not a neutral backdrop — they are design choices that determine spreads, fragmentation, the value of speed, and who profits.** A quant who treats market structure as fixed misses that it's *engineered*, and that rule changes (a new tick size, a dark-pool cap, a batch-auction proposal) *move the alpha*. This is **market design** — mechanism thinking (Day 186) applied to the rules themselves.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the market is a designed system, not a force of nature
The capstone insight of the whole module: **the market is an engineered mechanism whose rules you can — and must — reason about.** Prices, spreads, liquidity, and even the value of speed are *consequences of design choices*: the limit-order-book matching rule (Day 181), price–time priority (Day 187), maker–taker fees (Day 187), Reg NMS routing (today). Change the rules and you change the outcomes:
- **frequent batch auctions** (instead of continuous trading) would neutralize the speed race (Day 186) — a proposed redesign,
- **different tick sizes** change queue dynamics and spreads,
- **dark-pool caps** shift where institutions can hide size.

This *market-design* perspective is the mature endpoint of microstructure: you stop seeing the market as a given natural phenomenon and start seeing it as a **human-built system with designers, incentives, failure modes, and reform proposals** — which means you can (1) **execute better** by exploiting the current rules, (2) **anticipate** how rule changes will move liquidity and alpha, and (3) **reason about** whether a structure is efficient or fragile (Day 186's balanced judgment). It's the same shift the whole roadmap trains — from taking things as given to understanding the *mechanism that produces them* — now applied to the marketplace itself. The market is not the weather; it's a machine someone built, and you can read the blueprints.

**Hard puzzle:** Reg NMS's Order Protection Rule was meant to guarantee investors the best price by forcing routing to the best-quoted venue. Critics argue it *worsened* some problems it aimed to solve. How can a rule that mandates "best price" backfire? (Several mechanism-level ironies: (1) **It fueled the latency arms race** (Day 186) — forcing everyone to route to the **NBBO** made *being first to see and reach the best quote* hugely valuable, so firms spent fortunes on speed, and fast traders can **pick off slow ones' quotes across venues** before they update. (2) **It entrenched fragmentation** — protecting *every* venue's best quote gave many small venues a reason to exist, splitting liquidity and forcing complex SORs. (3) **It optimizes the *displayed* price, not the *all-in* outcome** — chasing the best *quoted* price can incur worse *impact/timing* cost (Day 188) and can be gamed with fleeting quotes; "best price" at an instant ≠ best execution over a parent order. (4) It interacts with **PFOF and internalization** in ways that route retail flow away from lit books. The lesson: a rule targeting one visible metric (displayed best price) reshapes the *whole incentive landscape*, often creating new costs (speed waste, fragmentation, adverse selection) elsewhere — **mechanism design has unintended equilibria.** Evaluating rules requires tracing their *systemic* incentive effects, not just their stated goal — the same mechanism-first discipline as Day 186.)

**Career connection:** "why does a single stock trade on so many venues, and how do you get best execution?" Strong answer: **fragmentation** — driven by venue competition and **Reg NMS** (which protects the best displayed quote, the NBBO) — scatters liquidity across lit exchanges, dark pools, and internalizers, so best execution requires a **smart order router** optimizing price, depth, fees, fill probability, and information leakage across venues (probe dark for size, sweep lit for the rest), with **TCA** to *prove* best execution per the regulatory duty. Bonus: note that the rules themselves (Reg NMS, MiFID II, tick sizes) are *design choices* that shape spreads, the speed race, and where alpha lives. Framing the market as an engineered, regulated, fragmented system is the senior microstructure answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · A smart order router across fragmented venues
```python
import numpy as np

# fragmented market: each venue has its own best ask, available size, and taker fee
venues = [
    {"name":"NYSE", "ask":10.02, "size":300, "fee_bps":3.0},
    {"name":"Nasdaq","ask":10.02, "size":200, "fee_bps":2.5},
    {"name":"Cboe", "ask":10.03, "size":500, "fee_bps":2.0},
    {"name":"Dark", "ask":10.015,"size":150, "fee_bps":1.0},   # mid-price, hidden
]

def smart_route(venues, qty):
    # rank by ALL-IN cost (price + fee), sweep cheapest first
    ranked = sorted(venues, key=lambda v: v["ask"]*(1+v["fee_bps"]/1e4))
    remaining, total_cost, plan = qty, 0.0, []
    for v in ranked:
        take = min(remaining, v["size"])
        if take<=0: break
        cost = take*v["ask"]*(1+v["fee_bps"]/1e4)
        total_cost += cost; remaining -= take
        plan.append((v["name"], take, v["ask"]))
        if remaining<=0: break
    return plan, total_cost/(qty-remaining), remaining

plan, avg_px, unfilled = smart_route(venues, 700)
for name,take,px in plan: print(f"  route {take:3d} shares to {name:7s} @ {px}")
print(f"all-in avg price = {avg_px:.4f}   unfilled = {unfilled}")
```
The router sweeps venues by **all-in cost** (price *and* fees) — taking the cheap dark fill and best-priced lit venues first — exactly what a real SOR does to satisfy best execution across a fragmented market.

**Linux:** `python sor.py | tee sor.log`; change fees/sizes per venue and watch the optimal routing shift — fragmentation makes routing a live optimization.
