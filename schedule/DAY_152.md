## Block 1 — The one principle: no-arbitrage
**Arbitrage** = a risk-free profit with zero net investment. The foundational axiom of pricing: *in a functioning market, arbitrage cannot persist* — if two portfolios have **identical payoffs in every future state, they must have identical prices today.** This "law of one price" is stronger and more reliable than any statistical model: it doesn't care about probabilities, preferences, or forecasts. **Derivatives pricing is mostly the art of replicating a payoff with simpler instruments, then invoking no-arbitrage.**

---

## Block 2 — Deriving put–call parity by replication
Consider two portfolios, European options, strike `K`, expiry `T`:
- **Portfolio A:** long one call + cash `K·e^{−rT}` (a bond paying `K` at `T`).
- **Portfolio B:** long one put + one share of stock `S`.

At expiry, check **every** case:
- If `S_T > K`: A = `(S_T−K) + K = S_T`. B = `0 + S_T = S_T`. ✅ equal.
- If `S_T ≤ K`: A = `0 + K = K`. B = `(K−S_T) + S_T = K`. ✅ equal.

Same payoff in *all* states → same price today:
```
C + K·e^{−rT} = P + S
```
That's **put–call parity**. (With dividends `q`: replace `S` by `S·e^{−qT}`.) No model, no volatility — pure replication.

---

## Block 3 — Synthetic positions (rearrange the identity)
Parity is a recipe for *building any instrument from the others*:
- **Synthetic call:** `C = P + S − K·e^{−rT}` (a put + stock + borrowing).
- **Synthetic put:** `P = C − S + K·e^{−rT}`.
- **Synthetic stock:** `S = C − P + K·e^{−rT}` (long call + short put = forward exposure).
- **Synthetic bond:** `K·e^{−rT} = P + S − C`.

**Concept check:** long call + short put, same `K` and `T` — what's your net payoff at expiry? (`(S_T−K)`: you're synthetically *long the forward*. This `C − P = S − K·e^{−rT}` relation is parity rearranged, and it's how desks lock in forward exposure.)

---

## Block 4 — Trading the violation (arbitrage mechanics)
Suppose `C + K·e^{−rT} < P + S` (the call side is *cheap*). Recipe for risk-free profit:
1. **Buy** the cheap side: buy call, invest `K·e^{−rT}` in bonds.
2. **Sell** the rich side: short the put, short the stock.
3. Net cash today is **positive** (you sold the dearer package); at `T` the two legs cancel exactly (proven in Block 2) → you keep the difference, risk-free.

In reality these gaps are tiny and fleeting — transaction costs, borrow fees, and the discrete `e^{−rT}` discounting eat them — but parity is the *reference line* every options desk monitors. Persistent deviations flag dividend/borrow/funding assumptions that are off, not free money.

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · replication is the master key
Put–call parity is a *static* replication (buy-and-hold). Black–Scholes (Day 153) is the same idea made *dynamic*: replicate an option by continuously rebalancing stock and cash. Both rest on one move: **if you can build the payoff from tradeable pieces, its price is the cost of the building blocks — full stop.** This is why derivatives pricing is often *preference-free and forecast-free*: you're not predicting `S_T`, you're costing a manufacturing recipe.

**Hard puzzle:** a stock pays no dividends and `r > 0`. Show from parity that a European call is worth *more* than its intrinsic value `S − K`, hence an American call on a non-dividend stock is *never* optimally exercised early. (From parity `C = S − K·e^{−rT} + P ≥ S − K·e^{−rT} > S − K` since `e^{−rT}<1` and `P≥0`. The live call is worth strictly more than exercising — you'd throw away time value *and* the interest on `K`. So early exercise is suboptimal; American = European here. Puts differ, because exercising a put *gives* you cash early.)

**Career connection:** "derive the relationship between a call and a put." Reproducing put–call parity by the replication argument — *constructing two portfolios and showing equal payoffs in every state* — is a classic interview filter. The senior follow-up is the early-exercise corollary above; nailing both marks you as someone who reasons by no-arbitrage, not memorization.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Verify parity and detect an "arb"
```python
import numpy as np
S, K, r, T = 100.0, 100.0, 0.05, 1.0
# pretend these are observed market prices
C, P = 10.45, 5.57

lhs = C + K*np.exp(-r*T)          # call + PV(K)
rhs = P + S                       # put + stock
print(f"C + PV(K) = {lhs:.4f}")
print(f"P + S     = {rhs:.4f}")
print(f"parity gap = {lhs-rhs:+.4f}  -> ", "arb!" if abs(lhs-rhs) > 0.01 else "consistent")

# synthetic call from put+stock+bond, compare to quoted call
synthetic_call = P + S - K*np.exp(-r*T)
print(f"synthetic call = {synthetic_call:.4f}  vs quoted C = {C:.4f}")

# payoff check across states (replication proof, numerically)
ST = np.array([60, 90, 100, 110, 140])
A = np.maximum(ST-K,0) + K            # call + bond(matures to K)
B = np.maximum(K-ST,0) + ST           # put + stock
print("A payoff:", A, "\nB payoff:", B, "\nequal:", np.allclose(A, B))
```
The payoff arrays match in every state — replication proven by computation, and any nonzero parity gap is your arbitrage signal (net of costs).

**Linux:** `python parity.py | tee parity.log`; feed it real option-chain quotes and watch how close to zero the gap sits after accounting for dividends and borrow.

## Quant Thinking Track — Invariants
No-arbitrage is an **invariant**: a relationship that must hold regardless of how prices move. Quants hunt for invariants because they're model-free anchors — parity, triangular FX consistency, forward-spot relations. When an invariant breaks, either there's free money (rare) or your assumptions (dividends, rates, borrow) are wrong (usual). Either way it's *information*.
