## Block 1 — The real cost of trading
Every trade leaks money through several channels:
- **Commissions/fees** — small, fixed-ish.
- **Bid–ask spread** — you buy at the ask, sell at the bid; crossing it costs *half the spread each way*.
- **Market impact** — your order *itself* pushes the price (buying lifts it, selling depresses it), so you fill at progressively worse prices. This dominates at size.
- **Slippage / delay** — the price moves between decision and execution.
- **Opportunity cost** — trading slowly to reduce impact means missing some of the alpha (it decays while you wait).

**Total cost = spread + impact + slippage + fees.** Impact is the one that *grows with your size* and ultimately caps the strategy.

---

## Block 2 — Market impact and the square-root law
The empirical regularity desks live by — impact grows **sub-linearly** with order size, roughly as a **square root**:
```
Impact ≈ k · σ · √(Q / V)
```
where `Q` = order size, `V` = daily volume (ADV), `σ` = volatility, `k` ≈ O(1). Read it:
- impact rises with the **fraction of daily volume** you demand (`Q/V`) — trading 10% of ADV is far costlier per share than 1%,
- it scales with **volatility** — illiquid, volatile names punish size,
- the **√** means doubling size *less-than-doubles* per-share impact, but **total** cost still rises faster than alpha (Block 4).

This square-root law (from the same convexity/liquidity microstructure you'll formalize in Phase 11) is *the* governing equation of capacity.

---

## Block 3 — Turnover: cost per unit of alpha
**Turnover** = how much of the book you replace per period. Stat arb is high-turnover (fast-decaying signals → frequent rebalancing). The brutal arithmetic:
```
Net return = Gross alpha − (turnover × cost per trade)
```
A signal with **gross** IC/alpha that looks great can have **net** alpha near zero once turnover × cost is subtracted. Levers to improve net:
- **trade slower / let signals overlap** (lower turnover) — but you give up fast-decaying alpha,
- **netting** — combine signals so offsetting trades cancel before hitting the market (don't sell what another signal wants to buy),
- **smart execution** — VWAP/TWAP, passive limit orders, trade-scheduling to minimize impact (Almgren–Chriss, Day 185),
- **trade the liquid names** / cap participation rate.

The art is capturing alpha *before* it decays while paying as little impact as possible — a direct tension.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · capacity, where alpha meets impact
**Capacity** = the AUM at which the strategy stops adding value. The logic:
- gross alpha (dollars) scales **linearly** with capital `\$`,
- but cost from impact scales **super-linearly** — total impact cost ~ `\$ · √(\$/V) ∝ \$^{1.5}`.

So **net profit rises, peaks, then falls** as you add capital — there's an optimal size, and beyond it you destroy your own edge:
```
Net \$ profit ≈ alpha·\$  −  c·\$^{1.5}/√V      → maximized at a finite \$*
```
Consequences that define the industry:
- **fast/high-IC signals are often low-capacity** (you can't trade them at size before impact eats the edge) — great for a small fund, irrelevant for a giant,
- **slow/low-IC factor signals are high-capacity** (low turnover, tradeable in size) — which is why huge funds gravitate to lower-turnover factor strategies,
- **alpha decay + capacity together** mean every edge is *finite in both time and size*. There is no infinitely scalable money machine — a profound, humbling constraint.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the backtest's fatal omission, made concrete
Day 178 warned that backtests can't see crowding; Day 179 names the *other* great omission: **your backtest assumes infinite liquidity at the observed price.** It fills every order at the printed close with zero impact — a fantasy. At research size that's harmless; at deployment size it's the difference between profit and loss. So the professional always asks **three questions of any signal, together**:
1. **Net of realistic costs**, is there still an edge? (spread + impact + fees)
2. **At what capacity** does it stop working? (the `\$*` above)
3. **How does turnover interact with decay** — can I capture the alpha before it fades *and* before impact eats it?

This reframes "is the signal good?" into "**is the signal good, net, at the size I need to run?**" — a fundamentally different, and far more useful, question. A high-Sharpe, low-capacity signal and a modest-Sharpe, high-capacity signal are *different products* for different-sized firms. Understanding that **alpha is not free, not infinite, and not scalable** is the final maturation of a quant: the market charges admission (costs), enforces a maximum occupancy (capacity), and closes the show eventually (decay).

**Hard puzzle:** a researcher shows a signal with a Sharpe of 4.0 and wants to run \$5B on it. It trades the smallest, least-liquid 20% of stocks with daily turnover of 50%. What's your concern, and how would you test it? (**Capacity / impact.** A 4.0 Sharpe in *illiquid* names at *50% daily turnover* almost certainly assumed near-zero transaction costs. At \$5B, the orders would be a large fraction of those names' ADV → the **square-root impact** (`k·σ·√(Q/V)`) would be enormous, especially given high `σ` (illiquid) and huge `Q/V` (big size, small names). Net of realistic impact, the 4.0 likely collapses toward — or below — zero. Test it by: (1) re-backtesting with an impact model calibrated to each name's ADV and the intended order sizes; (2) computing the **capacity curve** (net profit vs AUM) to find `\$*` — which may be \$50M, not \$5B; (3) measuring **participation rate** (`Q/V`) per name and capping it. The signal may be excellent at \$50M and worthless at \$5B — *same signal, different product.*)

**Career connection:** "your backtest shows a great Sharpe — what's the first thing you check before scaling it up?" Strong answer: **transaction costs and capacity** — re-run net of realistic spread + **square-root market impact** calibrated to each name's ADV and my intended size, compute the capacity curve (net profit vs AUM) to find where impact overtakes alpha, and check how turnover interacts with signal decay. A high-turnover signal in illiquid names can have a beautiful gross Sharpe and zero net alpha at scale. Leading with costs/capacity rather than the headline Sharpe is the practitioner's instinct.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Net alpha and the capacity curve
```python
import numpy as np
# signal economics
gross_alpha_bps = 8.0          # per rebalance, in basis points (gross)
turnover        = 0.5          # 50% of book traded each rebalance
spread_bps      = 2.0          # half-spread cost per trade
sigma, ADV      = 0.02, 5e6    # name volatility, $ daily volume
k               = 0.5          # impact coefficient

def net_profit(aum):
    # gross $ profit scales with capital
    gross = gross_alpha_bps/1e4 * aum
    # cost = spread (linear) + square-root impact on the traded notional
    traded = turnover*aum
    spread_cost = spread_bps/1e4 * traded
    impact = k*sigma*np.sqrt(traded/ADV) * traded        # ~ traded^1.5 / sqrt(ADV)
    return gross - spread_cost - impact

for aum in [1e7, 5e7, 1e8, 5e8, 1e9, 5e9]:
    print(f"AUM ${aum/1e6:6.0f}M  net profit/rebalance = ${net_profit(aum):12,.0f}")

# find approximate capacity (AUM that maximizes net profit)
grid = np.linspace(1e6, 1e10, 100000)
star = grid[np.argmax([net_profit(a) for a in grid])]
print(f"\napprox capacity AUM* = ${star/1e6:,.0f}M  (beyond this, impact destroys the edge)")
```
Net profit rises, peaks at a finite `AUM*`, then *falls* as square-root impact (`∝ traded^1.5`) overtakes the linear gross alpha — the capacity ceiling, computed.

**Linux:** `python capacity.py | tee cap.log`; raise `ADV` (more liquid names) and watch capacity grow — liquidity *is* capacity.

## Quant Thinking Track — Constraints are the real problem
A pure signal lives in an idealized world of free, infinite, instant trading. The *real* problem is always **the signal subject to costs, capacity, and decay** — a constrained optimization, not an unconstrained one (the theme since Day 144). The quant who internalizes that **edge is finite in time and size** asks better questions, builds tradeable strategies, and avoids the classic trap of a gorgeous backtest that the market will never let you actually run.
