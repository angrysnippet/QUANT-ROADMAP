## Block 1 — From two names to the whole cross-section
Instead of one spread, build a **signal for every stock** at each point in time and trade them *relative to each other*. The canonical example, **short-term reversal:** stocks that recently *over*-performed their peers tend to give some back, and recent *under*-performers bounce. Each day:
1. compute every stock's recent residual return,
2. **rank** the cross-section,
3. go **long the losers, short the winners**, dollar-neutral.

You're no longer betting on one relationship — you're betting that, **on average across hundreds of names**, the ranking predicts the next move. Individual bets are near-coin-flips; the *portfolio* of them has a real edge (LLN, Day 121).

*Why it matters:* this is breadth (`√Breadth`, Day 149) industrialized — the structural reason large quant funds can run modest per-name edges into strong, stable Sharpes.

---

## Block 2 — Factor neutralization (the key step)
A raw cross-sectional signal is contaminated by **factor exposure**: your "cheap losers" might just all be small-cap value energy stocks, so you're unknowingly making a *factor bet* (Day 148), not a stat-arb bet. The fix: **neutralize** the signal against known factors (market, size, value, momentum, sector, industry) so what remains is the **idiosyncratic residual**:
```
residual = stock return − (exposure to market, size, value, sector, ...)
```
You trade the **residual**, not the raw return. This makes the strategy market-neutral, sector-neutral, and factor-neutral — isolating the pure alpha (Day 149) and removing the cheap beta you don't want to pay for. Operationally it's a **cross-sectional regression each day**: regress returns on factor exposures, keep the residuals.

---

## Block 3 — Residual reversal & the signal zoo
The workhorse cross-sectional signals (each a function of the past, lookahead-safe):
- **Short-term reversal** on *residuals* (1-day to 1-month): residual losers bounce — overreaction (Day 149 behavioral edge).
- **Cross-sectional momentum** (3–12 month): residual winners keep winning (a longer-horizon, opposite-sign effect — the famous momentum anomaly, Day 150).
- **Mean-reversion baskets:** a stock vs a *cointegrated combination* of peers (Johansen, Day 172) — pairs generalized to many legs.
- **Fundamental/quality/value residuals**, and increasingly **alt-data** signals (Day 191+).

Each is weak alone; combined and neutralized across a big universe, they aggregate into the firm's alpha (Day 177 covers combination).

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · ranking, weighting, and the IC
Turn signals into a portfolio rigorously:
- **Standardize** each signal cross-sectionally (z-score or rank across names today), so they're comparable and outlier-robust.
- **Weights** ∝ the neutralized signal, scaled to **dollar-neutral** (`Σw = 0`) and a risk target (`wᵀΣw = target`, Day 146/166). Often: `w ∝ Σ⁻¹·signal` (a mini mean-variance step, Day 147) — *with shrinkage*, because raw `Σ⁻¹` is unstable.
- **Measure skill with the Information Coefficient (IC):** the cross-sectional correlation between *today's signal* and *next period's return*. A daily IC of **0.02–0.05** is a *real, valuable* signal at scale. Plug into the Fundamental Law:
```
IR ≈ IC · √Breadth
```
With IC = 0.03 and ~1,000 roughly-independent names traded daily, the implied IR is large — **a tiny per-name edge × enormous breadth = a strong Sharpe.** This single equation explains the entire economic logic of cross-sectional quant equity.

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the cross-section is the LLN as a machine
The conceptual leap from pairs to cross-section is the leap from *one* application of the Law of Large Numbers to an *industrial* one. The philosophy crystallizes:
- **be slightly right, very often, across many independent bets** (low IC, huge breadth) rather than very right, rarely (Day 149's two archetypes — quant equity is the low-IC/high-breadth pole),
- **neutralize everything you can cheaply replicate** (factors/beta) so you only spend risk on genuine alpha,
- **diversification is the alpha amplifier:** hundreds of weak, neutralized, roughly-independent bets convert a 3% IC into a high Sharpe — exactly Day 146's "idiosyncratic risk diversifies away" working *for* you.

This is why these firms hire armies of researchers to find *many small signals* rather than one big one: in the `IR = IC·√Breadth` world, **breadth and signal-count compound**, and a portfolio of decorrelated weak edges beats any single strong one. The whole probability/portfolio stack (Phases 6–7) is, in the end, a machine for turning faint statistical regularities into reliable returns.

**Hard puzzle:** Researcher X has one signal with IC = 0.10 on 50 names. Researcher Y has one with IC = 0.03 on 2,000 names. Whose strategy has the higher information ratio, and what's the catch? (By `IR = IC·√Breadth`: X → `0.10·√50 ≈ 0.71`; Y → `0.03·√2000 ≈ 1.34`. **Y wins decisively** — breadth dominates a modest IC, the core reason large-universe quant equity works. *The catch:* `√Breadth` assumes the bets are **independent**. If Y's 2,000 names are really driven by a few common factors (so the "effective breadth" is, say, 50 not 2,000), the advantage collapses — which is *exactly why factor-neutralization (Block 2) is essential*: it removes the common drivers, raising the *effective* number of independent bets. Breadth only counts if it's real, decorrelated breadth.)

**Career connection:** "how does a large quant equity fund make money if each prediction is barely better than a coin flip?" Strong answer: **cross-sectional, factor-neutral, breadth-driven** trading — rank a huge universe on neutralized residual signals, take many small dollar-neutral bets, and rely on `IR = IC·√Breadth` so a 0.02–0.05 IC across thousands of *independent* names compounds into a strong Sharpe; factor-neutralization is what keeps the breadth genuinely independent. Naming IC, breadth, neutralization, and the independence caveat is the complete answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · A factor-neutral cross-sectional reversal backtest
```python
import numpy as np, pandas as pd
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

N, T = 300, 800                                   # 300 stocks, 800 days
# returns = market factor + sector factor + idiosyncratic mean-reverting residual
mkt = rng.standard_normal(T)*0.01
sector = rng.integers(0,5,N)
sec_ret = rng.standard_normal((T,5))*0.008
resid = np.zeros((T,N))
for t in range(1,T):
    resid[t] = -0.10*resid[t-1] + rng.standard_normal(N)*0.02   # idiosyncratic reversal
ret = mkt[:,None] + sec_ret[:,sector] + resid

R = pd.DataFrame(ret)
ic_list, pnl = [], []
for t in range(20, T-1):
    today = R.iloc[t]
    # NEUTRALIZE: remove market + sector means -> idiosyncratic residual
    df = pd.DataFrame({'r':today,'sec':sector})
    neutral = today - df.groupby('sec')['r'].transform('mean') - today.mean()
    signal = -neutral                              # reversal: short winners, long losers
    signal = (signal - signal.mean())/signal.std()
    w = signal/np.abs(signal).sum()                # dollar-neutral weights
    fwd = R.iloc[t+1]
    pnl.append(float((w*fwd).sum()))
    ic_list.append(np.corrcoef(signal, fwd)[0,1])  # information coefficient

pnl=np.array(pnl)
print(f"mean IC        = {np.mean(ic_list):.3f}")
print(f"implied IR     ≈ {np.mean(ic_list)*np.sqrt(N):.2f}  (IC·√breadth)")
print(f"realized Sharpe= {pnl.mean()/pnl.std()*np.sqrt(252):.2f}")
```
You neutralize market+sector, trade the residual reversal dollar-neutral across 300 names, and watch a tiny IC turn into a solid Sharpe — `IR = IC·√Breadth` demonstrated end-to-end.

**Linux:** `python xsection.py | tee xs.log`; *remove* the neutralization step and watch the Sharpe degrade as factor risk leaks back in — neutralization's value, shown.

## Quant Thinking Track — Scale changes the strategy
Going from 2 names to 2,000 isn't "more of the same" — it changes the *nature* of the edge from "this relationship reverts" to "this *statistical regularity* holds on average across the cross-section." The skill is recognizing when scale + diversification transform a fragile individual bet into a robust portfolio property. Many weak, independent, neutralized signals > one strong fragile one — the deepest lesson of quant equity.
