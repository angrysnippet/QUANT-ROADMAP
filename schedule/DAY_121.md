## Block 1 — Law of Large Numbers (LLN)
The sample mean of `n` i.i.d. draws converges to the true mean as `n → ∞`:
```
X̄ₙ → μ
```
A casino with a 2% edge doesn't care about one spin — over millions of spins its average profit ≈ 2% of volume, almost surely.

*Why it matters:* a small per-trade **edge × many independent trades** = reliable profit. This is the entire logic of systematic trading.

---

## Block 2 — Central Limit Theorem (CLT)
The *sum/average* of many i.i.d. variables is approximately **Normal**, regardless of the original distribution:
```
X̄ₙ ≈ Normal( μ , σ²/n )      →    (X̄ₙ − μ) / (σ/√n) → N(0,1)
```
Even averaging dice rolls (uniform-ish) gives a bell curve fast. This is *why* the Normal distribution is everywhere.

**Concept check:** the std error of a mean of `n` samples? (`σ/√n` — the √n law again.)

---

## Block 3 — The √n law in practice
To **halve** your uncertainty about an average, you need **4×** the data. Returns "annualise" by `√252`; a strategy's t-stat grows like `√n` of independent trades.

**Key quant formula — information ratio over time:**
```
t-stat ≈ Sharpe × √(years)
```
A Sharpe-1 strategy needs ~**4 years** to reach a t-stat of 2 (statistically convincing). This is why "great backtest, 6 months" means almost nothing.

## Block 4 — Mathematics (worked)
**Exercise:** a strategy has per-trade edge of mean +0.1% and σ 2%, with 2,500 independent trades/year. Annual mean ≈ `2500·0.1% = 250%`?? No — that's notional; per-trade Sharpe `= 0.1/2 = 0.05`, annual Sharpe `= 0.05·√2500 = 2.5`. **Repetition manufactures Sharpe from a tiny edge.** **Challenge:** why does the CLT *fail to save you* when trades are correlated or tails are fat? (Effective `n` is much smaller; variance of the sum balloons.)

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking (Edge × repetition = the business)

- **LLN is the promise:** a real edge, repeated enough independent times, *will* show up.
- **CLT is the ruler:** it lets you put error bars on a backtest's mean return and decide if it's signal or luck.
- **The catch:** both assume **independence**. Markets cluster (volatility regimes, crashes), so effective sample size ≪ number of days. Overestimating independence is how funds fool themselves.

**Hard puzzle:** Strategy A: Sharpe 0.5, 10,000 trades/yr. Strategy B: Sharpe 2.0, 50 trades/yr. Which has the more statistically convincing 1-year track record? (A: t ≈ 0.5·√10000... careful — Sharpe already annual? If per-trade, A's annual t is huge; if both are *annual* Sharpe, B at 2.0 still needs years. The lesson: **always ask "how many *independent* observations back this number?"**)

**Career connection:** "is this backtest statistically significant?" → answer with t-stat ≈ Sharpe·√years and a hard look at independence. This is exactly how funds separate real alpha from data-mined noise.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · See the CLT happen
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
# Average of n uniform(0,1) draws becomes Normal as n grows
for n in [1, 2, 10, 50]:
    means = rng.random((100_000, n)).mean(axis=1)
    print(n, round(means.std(), 4), "≈ 1/√(12n) =", round((1/12/n)**0.5, 4))
```
Watch the std shrink like `1/√n` and the histogram turn into a bell curve. Bonus: simulate a +0.1%/2% edge over many trades and plot the t-stat growing like `√n`.

## Quant Thinking Track — Independence Is the Hidden Assumption
LLN and CLT are gifts *conditional on independence.* The single most common way quants delude themselves is treating correlated observations as independent — inflating apparent significance. Always discount your `n`.
