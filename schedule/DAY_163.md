## Block 1 — The price–yield curve bends
The price–yield relationship (Day 161) isn't a line — it's **convex** (curves upward, `d²P/dy² > 0`). Duration is the *tangent line* at the current yield; the true curve lies **above** that tangent everywhere. **Convexity** measures how much it bends. The bigger the yield move, the more the straight-line duration estimate errs, and the more convexity matters.

*Why it matters:* for small moves, duration is enough. For large moves and for *comparing* bonds with equal duration, convexity is the tie-breaker — and it's always in the long bondholder's favor.

---

## Block 2 — Why convexity is a gift
Because the curve lies above its tangent:
- when **yields fall**, the price rises *more* than duration predicted (bonus upside),
- when **yields rise**, the price falls *less* than duration predicted (cushioned downside).

**Heads you win more, tails you lose less.** For a long position in a standard (option-free) bond, convexity is *always positive* and *always helpful* — a free improvement over the linear estimate. This is why, between two bonds of equal duration and yield, the **more convex one is worth more** (and will trade at a slightly lower yield — you pay for convexity).

**Concept check:** two bonds, both duration 7, both yielding 5%. Bond X has higher convexity. Rates make a big move (either direction). Which bond's holder is happier? (X's — higher convexity means more upside when rates fall and less downside when they rise. Convexity is only valuable when moves are *large*; for tiny moves the two are nearly identical.)

---

## Block 3 — Negative convexity: when the gift reverses
Some bonds have **negative convexity** — the curve bends the *wrong* way over some yield range:
- **Callable bonds:** the issuer can redeem early. If rates fall, the bond won't rally much (it'll likely be called at par) — the upside is capped. You're **short a call option** to the issuer (Day 151).
- **Mortgage-backed securities (MBS):** homeowners *prepay* (refinance) when rates fall, handing you cash exactly when you'd rather keep the high-yielding bond — you're short a prepayment option.

Negative convexity = "tails you lose more, heads you win less" — the opposite of the gift, and you must be *paid* (higher yield) to hold it. Modeling the embedded option (often via trees / Monte Carlo, Days 157–158) is a whole quant sub-industry.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the second-order term
The full Taylor expansion (Day 162), now keeping convexity:
```
ΔP/P ≈ −ModDur·Δy  +  ½·Convexity·(Δy)²
```
- The duration term is **linear and signed** (down when rates rise).
- The convexity term is **quadratic and (for positive convexity) always positive** — it adds to return regardless of the sign of `Δy`. That's the mathematical statement of "the gift."

Convexity, like duration, **rises with maturity and falls with coupon**, and crucially it's *higher when cash flows are more dispersed in time*. Hence:
- **Bullet** (single maturity) vs **Barbell** (mix of short + long bonds with the *same* duration): the **barbell has higher convexity** (cash flows spread to the extremes), so it outperforms the bullet on large moves — but yields slightly less in calm markets. The convexity/yield trade-off is a classic relative-value trade.

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · convexity is positive gamma you don't pay theta for
The deep parallel to options: **convexity = gamma.** A long option is long gamma but pays theta (Day 154); a long option-free bond is long convexity and pays *nothing extra* — the convexity is baked in free. The catch is that the market *knows* this, so high-convexity bonds yield a touch less (you "pay" via lower carry, not theta). Conversely, *selling* convexity (holding callables/MBS, or running a barbell-vs-bullet short) earns extra yield as compensation for the negative-gamma profile — the same risk-premium-for-bearing-bad-payoffs pattern as short vol (Day 159) and the skew premium (Day 156). **Across the whole curriculum, the lesson rhymes: convexity has value, and someone is always paid to be short it and exposed in the tails.**

**Hard puzzle:** an MBS yields 1% more than a Treasury of equal duration. Is that a free 1% of alpha? (No — it's compensation for **negative convexity / short the prepayment option.** When rates fall, homeowners refinance and you get your money back early (reinvested at the new *lower* rates) — your upside is capped exactly when you'd want it most; when rates rise, prepayments slow and your duration *extends* into the selloff — downside amplified. The extra yield pays for that "lose more / win less" tail, the mirror of the variance risk premium. Same lesson as Day 150: extra yield ⇒ hidden short-tail risk, not free money.)

**Career connection:** "what's convexity and why does it matter?" Strong answer: it's the second-order (gamma-like) rate sensitivity; **positive convexity helps the holder in both directions** (more upside when rates fall, less downside when they rise), so duration alone *understates* gains and *overstates* losses for big moves — and bonds with embedded short options (callables, MBS) carry *negative* convexity that you must be paid for. Connecting convexity to gamma and to the prepayment option shows cross-domain mastery.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Convexity and bullet-vs-barbell
```python
import numpy as np

def price(face, cpn, y, n, freq=2):
    t = np.arange(1, n*freq+1)/freq; c = face*cpn/freq
    cf = np.full(len(t), c); cf[-1] += face
    return (cf/(1+y/freq)**(t*freq)).sum()

def dur_conv(face, cpn, y, n, freq=2):
    h=1e-4; P=price(face,cpn,y,n,freq)
    dP=(price(face,cpn,y+h,n,freq)-price(face,cpn,y-h,n,freq))/(2*h)
    d2P=(price(face,cpn,y+h,n,freq)-2*P+price(face,cpn,y-h,n,freq))/h**2
    return -dP/P, d2P/P                       # modified duration, convexity

# a single 10y bullet vs a barbell (2y + 30y) of similar duration
y=0.04
mod_b, cvx_b = dur_conv(100,0.04,y,10)
# barbell: blend 2y and 30y to match ~the 10y duration
m2,c2=dur_conv(100,0.04,y,2); m30,c30=dur_conv(100,0.04,y,30)
w=(mod_b-m30)/(m2-m30)                         # weight on 2y to match duration
mod_bb, cvx_bb = w*m2+(1-w)*m30, w*c2+(1-w)*c30
print(f"bullet : dur={mod_b:.2f}  convexity={cvx_b:.1f}")
print(f"barbell: dur={mod_bb:.2f}  convexity={cvx_bb:.1f}  <- higher convexity, same duration")

# convexity payoff on a big move
for dy in (0.02,-0.02):
    lin = -mod_b*price(100,0.04,y,10)*dy
    full= price(100,0.04,y+dy,10)-price(100,0.04,y,10)
    print(f"Δy={dy:+.0%}  duration est={lin:+.2f}  actual={full:+.2f}  convexity gain={full-lin:+.2f}")
```
The barbell shows higher convexity at matched duration, and the convexity gain is **positive for both** a +2% and −2% move — the gift, quantified.

**Linux:** `python convexity.py | tee cvx.log`; widen `dy` and watch the convexity term grow with the *square* of the move.

## Quant Thinking Track — Second-order matters in the tails
Convexity is the universal lesson that **first-order approximations fail exactly when it matters most — in big moves.** Duration/delta handle the calm; convexity/gamma rule the storm. Any risk system that stops at the linear term is blind to precisely the scenarios that blow up accounts. Always ask: *where's my second-order term, and who's short it?*
