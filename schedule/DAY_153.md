## Block 1 — The big idea: hedge the risk away
Black, Scholes & Merton (1973) asked: can I **replicate** a call by continuously holding `Δ` shares of stock + cash, rebalancing as `S` moves? If yes, the option's price = the cost of that self-financing hedge (no-arbitrage, Day 152). The magic: by holding exactly `Δ = ∂C/∂S` shares, the portfolio's *random* `dW` term **cancels** — the hedged position is instantaneously **risk-free**, so it must earn `r`. Setting that up gives the **Black–Scholes PDE**:
```
∂C/∂t + ½σ²S²·∂²C/∂S² + rS·∂C/∂S − rC = 0
```
*Why it matters:* the option is priced by *manufacturing* it, not by forecasting `S`. Risk is removed by hedging, so your *opinion* about the drift `μ` is irrelevant.

---

## Block 2 — Why the real-world drift disappears (risk-neutral valuation)
Here's the famous shock: **μ, the stock's expected return, is not in the formula.** Because you hedge away the risk, two investors who disagree wildly about `μ` still agree on the option price. Equivalently, you may price *as if* everyone is risk-neutral: discount the **expected payoff under a risk-neutral measure** where the stock drifts at `r` (not `μ`):
```
C = e^{−rT} · E^Q[ max(S_T − K, 0) ]      with   dS = rS dt + σS dW^Q
```
This **risk-neutral pricing** (the Q-measure) is the deepest idea in the field: hedgeable risk earns no premium, so we substitute `r` for `μ` and take a plain discounted expectation. The Monte-Carlo pricer you built on Day 139 was *exactly* this expectation.

## Block 3 — Reading the formula (no fear)
For a European call on a non-dividend stock:
```
C = S·N(d₁) − K·e^{−rT}·N(d₂)
d₁ = [ ln(S/K) + (r + ½σ²)T ] / (σ√T)
d₂ = d₁ − σ√T
```
Decode it as **"expected stock if exercised, minus expected cost of paying K"**:
- `N(d₂)` = **risk-neutral probability the option finishes ITM** (`Q(S_T > K)`).
- `K·e^{−rT}·N(d₂)` = present value of paying the strike, times the chance you do.
- `S·N(d₁)` = the expected value of receiving the stock, conditional on exercise (a probability-weighted, drift-adjusted term).
- `N(d₁) = Δ`, the hedge ratio (Day 154) — how many shares to hold.

Put: `P = K·e^{−rT}·N(−d₂) − S·N(−d₁)` (or just use parity, Day 152).

**Concept check:** as `σ→0`, what does `C` become? (Deterministic: `max(S − K·e^{−rT}, 0)` — pure discounted intrinsic; all option value is volatility value.)

## Block 4 — Assumptions (know exactly where it breaks)
BS is a *baseline*, and every assumption is a place reality intrudes:
- **Constant σ** → false: vol clusters and is stochastic (Day 143); produces the **volatility smile** (Day 156).
- **GBM / no jumps / log-normal prices** → false: fat tails, crashes, gaps (Day 119).
- **Continuous, costless hedging** → false: discrete rebalancing + transaction costs (Day 155).
- **Constant `r`, no dividends (basic form), perfect liquidity, no early exercise** → all relaxable with extensions.

The genius isn't that BS is *true* — it's that it gives a *common language* (one number, σ, the implied vol) in which the whole market quotes and reasons. **Traders quote BS implied vol the way physicists quote temperature: a convenient summary of a messier reality.**

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the model is a *change of variables*
The profound reframe: BS doesn't predict prices — it **translates** between a price and a single intuitive parameter, *implied volatility*. Nobody believes σ is constant; everyone uses BS to convert dollar prices ⇄ vols so they can compare, interpolate, and hedge. This is the recurring quant pattern: a "wrong" model that's *useful as a coordinate system*. Understanding that BS is the field's shared coordinate frame — not a truth claim — is the leap from student to practitioner.

**Hard puzzle:** your friend says "BS assumes the stock grows at `r`, which is obviously wrong — stocks earn more than the risk-free rate, so the model is garbage." Where's the error? (Conceptual: the `r` drift is under the *risk-neutral measure Q*, a pricing device, **not** a forecast of the real-world (P-measure) return. Because the option is *hedged*, the real drift `μ` is irrelevant to its price — risk-neutral valuation isn't claiming stocks earn `r`; it's exploiting that hedgeable risk carries no premium. Confusing Q with P is the single most common derivatives misconception.)

**Career connection:** "explain Black–Scholes." A weak answer recites the formula. A strong one tells the *story*: hedge the stock to cancel risk → the hedged portfolio earns `r` → therefore price = discounted **risk-neutral** expectation, with `μ` replaced by `r` → `N(d₂)` is the ITM probability and `N(d₁)` is the delta → and σ is the one free parameter the market actually trades. That narrative — replication, risk-neutrality, the meaning of each term, and the assumptions — is a complete senior answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · BS in closed form, checked by Monte Carlo
```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import norm

def bs_call(S, K, r, sigma, T):
    d1 = (np.log(S/K) + (r + 0.5*sigma**2)*T) / (sigma*np.sqrt(T))
    d2 = d1 - sigma*np.sqrt(T)
    return S*norm.cdf(d1) - K*np.exp(-r*T)*norm.cdf(d2), norm.cdf(d1), norm.cdf(d2)

S,K,r,sig,T = 100,100,0.05,0.2,1.0
price, delta, itm_prob = bs_call(S,K,r,sig,T)
print(f"BS call = {price:.4f}   delta=N(d1)={delta:.3f}   P(ITM)=N(d2)={itm_prob:.3f}")

# Monte Carlo under the RISK-NEUTRAL drift (r, not mu) — must match BS
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
ST = S*np.exp((r-0.5*sig**2)*T + sig*np.sqrt(T)*rng.standard_normal(1_000_000))
mc = np.exp(-r*T)*np.maximum(ST-K,0).mean()
print(f"MC call (risk-neutral) = {mc:.4f}   <-- matches BS, confirming the expectation")
```
The Monte-Carlo price (Day 139's machinery, now with drift = `r`) converges to the closed-form BS price — *seeing* that BS **is** the discounted risk-neutral expectation.

**Linux:** `python bs.py | tee bs.log`; sweep σ and watch the price rise monotonically — vol is the price.

## Quant Thinking Track — Decomposition
BS decomposes option value into two interpretable pieces — `S·N(d₁)` (expected benefit of holding the stock if exercised) minus `K·e^{−rT}·N(d₂)` (expected discounted cost of the strike) — and one tradeable parameter, σ. Decomposing a scary formula into *probability × value* terms is how you read any pricing model without intimidation.
