# Day 168 — 💵🔀 Rates World · Short-Rate Models — When the Rate Itself Is Random

> Black–Scholes assumed a *constant* rate. But the short rate is the most-watched random variable in macro finance. **Short-rate models** (Vasicek, CIR, Hull–White) make `r` itself a stochastic process — letting you price bonds and rate derivatives in a world where the discount rate wanders.

## Block 1 — Why rates need their own model
Interest rates don't behave like stocks:
- they **mean-revert** — pulled toward a long-run level by central-bank policy (a stock has no "correct" price to revert to),
- they're **bounded-ish** — historically can't run off to infinity (and mostly stay non-negative),
- their **whole curve** must move consistently (Day 164), not just one price.

So GBM is wrong for rates (no mean reversion, multiplicative). We need processes built around **mean reversion** — the defining feature of rate dynamics.

---

## Block 2 — The Vasicek model
The first and cleanest short-rate model (Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process):
```
dr = a(b − r) dt + σ dW
```
- `a` = **speed of mean reversion** (how fast `r` is pulled back),
- `b` = **long-run mean level** the rate reverts toward,
- `σ` = volatility of rate moves.

The `a(b−r)` drift is the engine: when `r > b` it pushes down, when `r < b` it pushes up. Vasicek is **analytically tractable** — bond prices and even bond-option prices have closed forms, and the model is **affine** (yields are linear in `r`). Its one famous flaw: rates can go **negative** (the Gaussian shock is unbounded) — once seen as fatal, now less so after Europe/Japan actually had negative rates.

**Concept check:** `a=0.3, b=4%, r=6%`. Direction and rough pull of the drift? (Down: `0.3(0.04−0.06) = −0.6%`/yr instantaneous pull toward 4% — mean reversion in action.)

---

## Block 3 — CIR and the model zoo
- **Cox–Ingersoll–Ross (CIR):** `dr = a(b−r)dt + σ√r dW`. The `√r` makes volatility shrink as `r → 0`, **keeping rates non-negative** (fixing Vasicek's flaw) while staying affine/tractable. The workhorse for non-negative rate (and hazard-rate, Day 167) modeling.
- **Hull–White (extended Vasicek):** lets `b` be time-dependent so the model **exactly fits today's observed yield curve** — essential for *consistent pricing* (you can't have your model disagree with quoted bond prices).
- **Multi-factor / HJM / LMM:** model the *whole curve* or forward rates directly for realistic curve dynamics — what production desks actually use for exotics.

The progression mirrors options (BS → local vol → stochastic vol, Day 156): start simple and tractable, then add realism to fit the market.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · mean reversion and the affine bond price
The Ornstein–Uhlenbeck (Vasicek) SDE has a known distribution: `r_t` is **Normal** with
```
E[r_t] = b + (r₀ − b)e^{−at}        (decays to b)
Var[r_t] = (σ²/2a)(1 − e^{−2at})    (saturates at σ²/2a)
```
The half-life of a rate shock is `ln2/a`. And because Vasicek/CIR are **affine**, zero-coupon bond prices take the form:
```
P(t,T) = A(t,T)·e^{−B(t,T)·r_t}
```
— closed-form functions `A, B` of the parameters. This affine structure is *why* these models dominate: you get the entire yield curve (Day 164) as an explicit function of one state variable `r`, so calibration and pricing are fast. Pricing is still **risk-neutral** (Day 153): bonds and rate options are discounted expectations under Q, with the drift adjusted by the *market price of risk* — the rates version of "μ → r."

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the model is for *pricing consistency*, not forecasting
A crucial mindset, easy to get backwards: short-rate models are **not** there to forecast where rates will go (forwards and macro do that). They exist to **price rate derivatives consistently with each other and with today's curve.** You *calibrate* the model (pick `a, b, σ`, or Hull–White's `b(t)`) so it **exactly reprices** the liquid market — the curve, caps/floors, swaptions — and then use it to price *less* liquid instruments **arbitrage-free** relative to those. Same philosophy as the vol surface (Day 156): the model is a **consistent interpolation/extrapolation engine**, a coordinate system glued to market prices — not a crystal ball. Judging a rate model by "did it predict rates?" is the wrong test; the right test is "does it reprice the market and hedge stably?"

**Hard puzzle:** the Vasicek model can produce negative interest rates, which was long called a fatal flaw. Is it? (Historically people thought rates couldn't go below zero, so a model assigning probability to `r < 0` seemed broken — and CIR's `σ√r` was invented to prevent it. But **Europe, Japan, and Switzerland ran negative policy rates in the 2010s**, so Vasicek's "flaw" turned out to describe reality the non-negative models *couldn't*. The real lesson: every model encodes assumptions about what's *possible*, and the market periodically teaches you that your "impossible" was just unimagined — the same humility theme as fat tails (Day 119) and correlation breakdown (Day 146). Choose the model whose *failure modes* you can live with.)

**Career connection:** "how would you model interest rates, and why not just use GBM?" Strong answer: rates **mean-revert** (central-bank anchored) and must keep the *whole curve* consistent, so GBM is wrong; use a mean-reverting short-rate model — **Vasicek** (`dr=a(b−r)dt+σdW`, Gaussian, can go negative) or **CIR** (`σ√r` keeps it non-negative), and **Hull–White** when you need to exactly fit today's curve. Stress that the model's job is **arbitrage-free pricing/calibration**, not forecasting. Naming the mean-reversion term and the calibration purpose is the senior framing.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Simulate Vasicek vs CIR
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

def simulate(model, r0=0.06, a=0.3, b=0.04, sigma=0.02, T=10, steps=2520, paths=5):
    dt=T/steps; r=np.full((paths,steps+1), r0)
    for t in range(steps):
        dW = np.sqrt(dt)*rng.standard_normal(paths)
        if model=="vasicek":
            r[:,t+1] = r[:,t] + a*(b-r[:,t])*dt + sigma*dW
        else: # CIR: vol scales with sqrt(r), floored at 0
            rt = np.maximum(r[:,t],0)
            r[:,t+1] = rt + a*(b-rt)*dt + sigma*np.sqrt(rt)*dW
    return r

for m in ("vasicek","cir"):
    r = simulate(m)
    print(f"{m:8s} terminal mean={r[:,-1].mean():.4f} (long-run b=0.04)  "
          f"min over all paths={r.min():.4f}")
```
Both revert toward `b=0.04` from `r₀=0.06`; Vasicek's path minimum can dip **below zero**, while CIR's `√r` term keeps it non-negative — the defining difference, observed directly.

**Linux:** `python shortrate.py | tee rate.log`; raise `sigma` and watch Vasicek breach zero more often while CIR holds the floor.

## Quant Thinking Track — Calibrate, don't forecast
Short-rate models crystallize a principle that applies to *every* pricing model you'll build: its value is **internal consistency with observed prices**, not predictive accuracy about the future. Calibrate to what's liquid, price what isn't, hedge the difference. Confusing a *pricing/calibration* model with a *forecasting* model is one of the most common — and costly — conceptual errors in quant finance.
