## Block 1 — Immunization: matching duration to a horizon
The classic problem: you owe a fixed liability `L` at time `H` (a pension payment, an insurance claim). **Immunization** builds a bond portfolio whose **duration equals the liability's horizon `H`**, so that a small rate move's two effects *cancel*:
- rates **up** → bond prices fall (capital loss) **but** reinvested coupons earn more,
- rates **down** → prices rise **but** reinvestment earns less.

At duration = horizon, the price-risk and reinvestment-risk offset to first order, and your terminal wealth is protected against small parallel moves. **Asset-liability management (ALM)** for banks, insurers, and pensions is immunization at industrial scale: match the duration (and ideally convexity) of assets to liabilities.

*Why it matters:* solvency depends on it. Mismatch duration and a rate move silently erodes net worth — the SVB lesson (Day 162).

---

## Block 2 — Why one number isn't enough
A single duration assumes the curve moves in **parallel** — every rate shifts by the same `Δy`. Real curves move in (at least) three independent ways (the PCA result of Block 4):
- **Level** (parallel shift) — ~all rates up/down together,
- **Slope** (steepening/flattening) — short and long ends move oppositely,
- **Curvature** (butterfly) — the belly moves vs the wings.

A duration-neutral book can still be massively exposed to **slope**: long the 10y, short the 2y to zero net duration, then the curve *steepens* and you lose despite "no duration." Hedging the curve requires neutralizing each mode, not just the level.

---

## Block 3 — Key-rate (bucketed) durations
The fix: **key-rate durations (KRDs)** — measure the portfolio's sensitivity to a move in *each segment* of the curve separately (2y, 5y, 10y, 30y "key rates"), holding the others fixed. The vector of KRDs tells you your exposure to *every* part of the curve, so you can:
- hedge each bucket with an instrument of matching maturity (a **DV01 ladder**),
- deliberately *run* a curve view (e.g. long-the-belly butterfly) while neutralizing the rest,
- see at a glance whether you're exposed to steepening, flattening, or a twist.

KRDs are the rates analogue of having a full *vector of Greeks* (Day 154) instead of just delta — risk management in the right dimensionality.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · PCA says the curve has ~3 factors
Run **principal component analysis** (Day 146's covariance-matrix thinking) on historical curve changes and a remarkable empirical fact emerges: **~99% of yield-curve variance is captured by just 3 factors:**
1. **Level** (~85–90%): a roughly parallel shift — the dominant risk, what duration captures.
2. **Slope** (~5–10%): steepening/flattening.
3. **Curvature** (~1–3%): butterfly/twist.

So the high-dimensional curve effectively lives on a **3-dimensional manifold.** This is why hedging three modes (level, slope, curvature) — or equivalently a handful of key rates — captures almost all the risk, and why PCA-based hedging and curve trades are standard. It's dimensionality reduction (a recurring theme) applied to the most important curve in finance: the curve is complex, but its *movements* are nearly low-rank.

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · hedge in the right number of dimensions
The meta-lesson spans the whole course: **match your hedge's dimensionality to the risk's true dimensionality — no more, no less.** One number (duration) under-hedges (misses slope/curvature); hedging all 30 curve points over-fits noise and racks up costs. PCA reveals the *right* answer is ~3. This is the bias-variance trade-off (Day 128) wearing a fixed-income costume: too few factors = biased/under-hedged, too many = noisy/over-traded. Great risk management isn't "hedge everything" — it's "identify the few factors that carry the risk and neutralize *those*." Whether it's Greeks for options, key rates for bonds, or factors for equities (Day 148), the discipline is one: **decompose risk into its principal modes and hedge the ones that matter.**

**Hard puzzle:** you're long \$50M 10-year, short \$50M 2-year, and your *net duration is zero*. Are you hedged? (No — you've neutralized **level** but you're heavily exposed to **slope**: this is a *flattener* (you profit if the curve flattens — 10y rallies vs 2y — and lose if it steepens). Zero net duration ≠ zero risk; you've simply *chosen* to express a curve-shape view instead of a level view. To be truly hedged you'd need matched **key-rate durations** at both the 2y and 10y buckets. "Duration-neutral" is a statement about one factor only — a classic trap.)

**Career connection:** "you've duration-hedged your bond book — what risk remains?" Strong answer: **curve risk** — slope and curvature. Duration only neutralizes parallel shifts; a duration-neutral book can still lose on steepening/flattening or a butterfly. Real hedging uses **key-rate durations** (or the 3 PCA factors: level, slope, curvature) to neutralize each mode. Naming the three PCA factors and ~99% variance figure marks you as someone who's seen a real rates risk report.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · PCA of curve moves + key-rate exposure
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# simulate daily curve changes driven by 3 latent factors + noise
mats = np.array([1,2,3,5,7,10,30])
level = np.ones(len(mats))
slope = (mats - mats.mean())/mats.std()
curv  = (mats - mats.mean())**2; curv = (curv-curv.mean())/curv.std()
F = np.column_stack([level,slope,curv])
days = 2000
loadings = rng.standard_normal((days,3))*np.array([6.0,2.0,0.8])   # bp/day
dY = loadings @ F.T + rng.standard_normal((days,len(mats)))*0.3    # +noise

# PCA on the curve changes
C = np.cov(dY.T)
vals, vecs = np.linalg.eigh(C)
order = np.argsort(vals)[::-1]; vals = vals[order]
print("variance explained by top 3 PCs:", (vals[:3]/vals.sum()).round(3),
      " cumulative:", round(vals[:3].sum()/vals.sum(),3))
print("PC1 (level)  loading signs:", np.sign(vecs[:,order[0]]).astype(int))
print("PC2 (slope)  loading signs:", np.sign(vecs[:,order[1]]).astype(int))
```
The top 3 components soak up ~99% of curve-change variance, with PC1 all-same-sign (level), PC2 sign-flipping across maturity (slope) — the empirical 3-factor structure, reproduced.

## Quant Thinking Track — The right basis
Key-rate durations and PCA factors are just *choices of basis* for the same risk. Picking the basis in which risk is **sparse and interpretable** (level/slope/curvature) turns an unmanageable 30-dimensional problem into a 3-knob control panel. Finding the natural low-dimensional basis of a messy problem is, across every domain, what separates insight from brute force.
