## Block 1 — Value at Risk (VaR)
**VaR** answers: "What's the most I can lose over a given horizon, at a given confidence level?" Formally, the **1-day 95% VaR is the loss that won't be exceeded 95% of the time** (exceeded only 5% of days):
```
P(Loss > VaR) = 1 − confidence     (e.g. 5% for 95% VaR)
```
"95% 1-day VaR of \$1M" = "on 95% of days, you lose less than \$1M; on the worst 5%, you lose more." VaR became the industry/regulatory standard (Basel) because it's a **single, intuitive number** summarizing risk in dollar terms. It's everywhere — risk limits, capital requirements, reporting.

*Why it matters:* VaR is the lingua franca of risk management — you *must* know it. But its flaws (Block 4) are equally important, and misunderstanding them has caused real disasters.

---

## Block 2 — Three ways to compute VaR
- **Parametric (variance-covariance):** assume returns are Normal, VaR = `z × σ × √horizon` (e.g. 1.65σ for 95%). Fast and simple — but **assumes Normality**, badly underestimating fat tails (Day 119).
- **Historical simulation:** use the *actual* historical distribution of returns — take the 5th percentile of the past loss distribution. No distributional assumption (captures real fat tails), but assumes *the past represents the future* (and is limited by sample length/regime, Day 142).
- **Monte Carlo:** simulate many scenarios (Day 139) from a model, compute the loss distribution, read off the percentile. Flexible (handles nonlinear payoffs, options) but model-dependent and compute-heavy.

Each makes different assumptions; a good risk system uses *several* and compares. The parametric-Normal version is the most common *and* the most dangerous (it ignores fat tails).

**Concept check:** your parametric (Normal) 99% VaR says you won't lose more than \$2M on 99% of days. In the 2008 crisis, you lost \$8M in a day. What went wrong? (The **Normal assumption** — real returns have **fat tails** (Day 119), so extreme losses are *far* more likely than a Normal distribution implies. The Normal 99% VaR drastically *underestimated* tail risk; the actual distribution had much heavier tails. This is *the* canonical VaR failure — parametric-Normal VaR is blind to exactly the crashes it's supposed to warn about.)

---

## Block 3 — CVaR / Expected Shortfall: a better tail measure
VaR has a fatal blind spot: it tells you the *threshold* but says **nothing about how bad losses are *beyond* it.** Two portfolios can have the *same* VaR but wildly different tail risk — one loses slightly more than VaR in the worst 5%, the other loses *catastrophically*. **Conditional VaR (CVaR)**, a.k.a. **Expected Shortfall (ES)**, fixes this:
```
CVaR = E[Loss | Loss > VaR]     = the average loss in the worst (1−confidence)% of cases
```
CVaR is the **expected loss *given* that you're in the tail** — it captures the *severity* of tail losses, not just the threshold. Properties:
- **CVaR ≥ VaR always** (it averages the losses *beyond* VaR),
- it's **sensitive to the shape of the tail** — fat tails make CVaR much larger than VaR,
- regulators have moved **from VaR to Expected Shortfall** (Basel III/FRTB) precisely because ES captures tail severity that VaR misses.

CVaR is the more honest tail-risk measure, and it's what you should optimize and limit on (Block 4 explains the deeper reason).

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · coherent risk measures (why CVaR > VaR theoretically)
There's a *rigorous* theory of what makes a risk measure "good." **Artzner et al. (1999)** defined four axioms a **coherent risk measure** must satisfy:
1. **Monotonicity** — if portfolio A always loses more than B, A is riskier. (Obvious.)
2. **Translation invariance** — adding cash reduces risk by that amount. (Obvious.)
3. **Positive homogeneity** — doubling the position doubles the risk. (Scale.)
4. **Subadditivity** — `Risk(A + B) ≤ Risk(A) + Risk(B)` — **diversification can only *reduce* risk, never increase it.**

The bombshell: **VaR is NOT coherent — it can violate subadditivity.** There exist portfolios where `VaR(A + B) > VaR(A) + VaR(B)` — i.e., VaR says a *diversified* portfolio is *riskier* than its parts, which is absurd and contradicts the foundational principle of diversification (Day 146). This isn't a corner case; it happens with skewed/fat-tailed/discrete payoffs (e.g. portfolios of bonds with default risk, or options). The consequences are serious:
- **VaR can penalize diversification** — a coherent risk system should *reward* it; VaR sometimes punishes it,
- **VaR can be "gamed"** — you can rearrange a portfolio to lower reported VaR while *increasing* true tail risk (sell deep-OTM options: tiny VaR, catastrophic tail — exactly the short-vol payoff of Day 159),
- **VaR ignores the tail beyond the threshold** — so it's blind to *how bad* the worst case is.

**CVaR / Expected Shortfall *is* coherent** — it satisfies all four axioms, including subadditivity, so it always rewards diversification and captures tail severity. This is the *theoretical* reason (beyond the practical fat-tail reason) that CVaR is superior and regulators adopted it. The lesson: **a risk measure isn't just a number — it has mathematical *properties*, and bad properties (like VaR's non-coherence) lead to bad decisions** (penalizing diversification, hiding tail risk, being gameable). Choosing a risk measure is choosing what behavior you incentivize.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the measure you choose shapes the behavior you get
VaR and coherent risk measures teach a profound lesson that extends to *all* of risk management and metric design: **the risk measure you choose is not a neutral observation — it actively shapes behavior, and a flawed measure incentivizes dangerous behavior.** This unfolds on several levels:
- **VaR's non-coherence has real consequences** — because VaR can be reduced by *adding* tail risk (selling far-OTM options gives tiny VaR but catastrophic tail exposure — Day 159's "picking up nickels in front of a steamroller"), a firm that *limits and optimizes on VaR* unwittingly *incentivizes* its traders to load up on hidden tail risk that doesn't show up in the metric. The measure *creates* the exposure it fails to capture. This is a structural, not accidental, flaw — and it contributed to real blowups (firms with "low VaR" that were secretly short enormous tail risk),
- **what you measure is what you manage** — and what you *fail* to measure grows unchecked. VaR's blindness to *beyond-threshold* severity means the catastrophic-but-rare losses it ignores are exactly the ones that accumulate where no one's looking,
- **metrics can be gamed** — any single number used as a *limit* or *target* will be optimized against (Goodhart's law: "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure"). VaR-as-a-limit gets gamed by hiding risk in the tail it can't see; this is why you need **multiple, complementary measures** (VaR *and* CVaR *and* stress tests *and* max drawdown — Day 144) that are hard to game simultaneously,
- **mathematical properties matter for practical safety** — the coherence axioms aren't academic pedantry; subadditivity *encodes* "diversification reduces risk," and a measure that violates it will make systematically wrong decisions (penalizing diversification, the opposite of Day 146). Choosing a measure with the right properties is choosing a system that *behaves* sensibly.

The unifying wisdom: **risk measurement is not passive observation — it's an active force that shapes incentives and behavior, so choose measures whose mathematical properties align with the behavior you want, use multiple complementary ones to resist gaming, and never forget that the risks *not* captured by your metric are precisely the ones that will hurt you.** This connects to the curriculum's spine of *intellectual honesty about risk* (Days 119 fat tails, 144 drawdown/σ's blind spots, 145 Sharpe's traps, 178 unmodeled crowding): every risk metric is a *partial, gameable* view, and the mature risk manager holds *several* of them, understands what each misses, prefers coherent measures (CVaR over VaR), stress-tests beyond all of them, and stays vigilant for the tail risk hiding in the gaps. **The number is not the risk; it's a lossy, behavior-shaping projection of it — respect what it captures, distrust what it omits, and never let a single metric become the whole truth.** This is why the best risk managers are defined less by the sophistication of their models than by their *humility about what their models can't see* — and CVaR vs VaR is the canonical lesson that even the *choice of measure* embeds deep assumptions with real consequences.

**Hard puzzle:** a hedge fund reports a low, stable Value at Risk and markets itself as "low risk." On investigation, you find its main strategy is selling far-out-of-the-money put options on the S&P 500. Why is the low VaR dangerously misleading, and what would coherent risk measures and stress tests reveal? (This is the **canonical VaR-gaming disaster** — the low VaR is not just misleading, it's *generated by* the very risk it hides: (1) **VaR is blind to the tail beyond its threshold** (Block 3) — selling far-OTM puts produces a payoff that's a **small, steady premium 99% of the time** (Day 159's variance risk premium) and a **catastrophic loss in the rare crash**. The 95% or 99% VaR sits in the "collect premium" region where losses are tiny, so **VaR is genuinely low** — it *correctly* reports that on 95–99% of days you make a little money. But it says *nothing* about the 1% tail, which is *catastrophic*. The fund isn't low-risk; it's **concentrated tail risk that VaR structurally cannot see.** (2) **VaR's non-coherence enables the gaming** (Block 4) — selling OTM options is a way to *lower* reported VaR while *increasing* true tail exposure; the fund has (perhaps unwittingly, perhaps deliberately) optimized for the metric rather than for actual safety (Goodhart's law). "Low VaR" markets well precisely *because* the metric is blind to the strategy's real risk. (3) **What CVaR/Expected Shortfall reveals** — CVaR = *average loss in the worst 1–5%* (Block 3), which would be **enormous** for this strategy because the tail losses are catastrophic. CVaR ≫ VaR here, immediately exposing the hidden tail risk: the gap between VaR (tiny) and CVaR (huge) is the *signature* of hidden tail risk. (4) **What stress tests reveal** — apply a 2008/1987-style scenario (S&P −20% in days, vol spike): the far-OTM puts blow up, and the stress loss dwarfs the VaR by orders of magnitude. Stress tests *bypass* the probability-threshold blindness of VaR by asking "what happens in *this specific bad scenario*?" (5) **The skew/distribution shape** — the return distribution is *highly negatively skewed* (Day 145): tiny positive returns most of the time, rare huge losses. Any measure sensitive to skew/kurtosis (CVaR, the full distribution) flags it; VaR (a single percentile) doesn't. **The lesson:** a single, low VaR is *exactly* what a hidden-tail-risk strategy produces, and quoting it as "low risk" is either ignorance or marketing. You **must** look at **CVaR (huge), stress tests (catastrophic), the return distribution's skew (very negative), and the strategy's economics (short tail/insurance)** to see the truth. This is *the* reason regulators moved from VaR to Expected Shortfall, why you never trust a single risk number, and why "low VaR" should *increase* your scrutiny of a short-options strategy rather than reassure you. The risk the metric can't see is the risk that kills you — and VaR is structurally blind to precisely the catastrophic tail this fund is loading up on.)

**Career connection:** "what's VaR, and what are its problems?" Strong answer: **Value at Risk** is the loss not exceeded at a given confidence over a horizon (e.g. "95% 1-day VaR of \$1M" = lose less than \$1M on 95% of days) — the industry/regulatory standard because it's a single intuitive number. But it has serious flaws: (1) **parametric-Normal VaR ignores fat tails** (Day 119), badly underestimating crash losses; (2) it says **nothing about losses *beyond* the threshold** — two portfolios with the same VaR can have wildly different tail severity; and (3) crucially, **VaR is not a *coherent* risk measure** — it can violate **subadditivity** (`VaR(A+B) > VaR(A)+VaR(B)`), so it can *penalize diversification* and be **gamed** by hiding risk in the tail (selling far-OTM options → low VaR, catastrophic tail exposure). The fix is **CVaR / Expected Shortfall** = the *average* loss in the worst cases — it's **coherent** (satisfies all four axioms including subadditivity), captures **tail severity**, and is what regulators moved to (Basel III/FRTB). I'd never rely on a single measure — I'd use VaR *and* CVaR *and* stress tests *and* max drawdown, because every metric is partial and gameable. Naming the coherence axioms, VaR's subadditivity failure, the OTM-option gaming, and the move to Expected Shortfall is the senior risk-management answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · VaR vs CVaR, and the fat-tail trap
```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import norm
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# real-ish returns with FAT TAILS (Student-t) vs the Normal assumption
ret = rng.standard_t(df=3, size=100_000)*0.01          # fat-tailed (t with 3 dof)
conf = 0.99

# parametric (Normal) VaR — assumes thin tails, UNDERESTIMATES
var_normal = norm.ppf(conf)*ret.std()
# historical VaR & CVaR — capture the real fat tail
var_hist  = -np.quantile(ret, 1-conf)
cvar_hist = -ret[ret <= np.quantile(ret, 1-conf)].mean()   # avg loss in the worst 1%

print(f"99% VaR (Normal assumption): {var_normal*100:.2f}%   <- underestimates the tail")
print(f"99% VaR (historical)       : {var_hist*100:.2f}%")
print(f"99% CVaR (historical)      : {cvar_hist*100:.2f}%   <- tail SEVERITY, ≥ VaR")
print(f"CVaR/VaR ratio: {cvar_hist/var_hist:.2f}  (large gap ⇒ fat-tailed, hidden tail risk)")

# the short-OTM-option trap: low VaR, catastrophic CVaR
pnl = np.where(rng.random(100_000) < 0.01, -0.50, 0.005)   # 1% chance of -50%, else +0.5%
print(f"\nshort-OTM strategy: 95% VaR = {-np.quantile(pnl,0.05)*100:+.1f}%  (looks SAFE)")
print(f"                    95% CVaR = {-pnl[pnl<=np.quantile(pnl,0.05)].mean()*100:.1f}%  (CATASTROPHIC tail)")
```
Normal VaR underestimates the fat-tailed loss; CVaR exceeds VaR and exposes tail severity. And the short-OTM strategy shows the killer gap — a *low VaR* hiding a *catastrophic CVaR*, the signature of hidden tail risk.

## Quant Thinking Track — The number is a lossy projection of the risk
Every risk metric is a *partial, gameable* projection of the true risk — it captures some aspects and is blind to others, and once used as a limit or target it *will* be optimized against (Goodhart's law). The disciplined risk manager uses *multiple* complementary measures, prefers ones with sound mathematical properties (coherence), stress-tests beyond all of them, and stays most worried about the risks the metrics *can't* see. The number is never the risk; respect what it captures, distrust what it omits.
