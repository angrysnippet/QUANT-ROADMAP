## Block 1 — Why attribution matters
A portfolio's return is a *single number*, but it has many *sources*. **Performance attribution** decomposes it to answer: *where did the return come from?* This matters enormously:
- **for the manager:** is my edge where I *think* it is? Am I being paid for skill or just bearing factor risk (Day 148)?
- **for allocators:** is this manager's return *skill* (alpha, worth paying fees for) or just *factor exposure* (beta, cheaply replicable)? (Day 149's "beta is cheap, alpha is dear")
- **for risk:** are my returns coming from where I *intended*, or from unintended bets (Day 211)?
- **for improvement:** which decisions added value, which destroyed it? (the feedback loop, Day 188)

Attribution turns "I made 12%" into "I made 12%: 7% from market beta, 3% from a value tilt, 1% from sector allocation, 1% from genuine stock selection (alpha)" — a *vastly* more useful, actionable, and honest statement.

*Why it matters:* without attribution, you can't distinguish skill from luck or alpha from beta — and you'll mis-allocate capital, mis-pay managers, and mis-judge your own edge.

---

## Block 2 — Factor-based attribution
The quant-standard approach uses the **factor model** (Day 211) to decompose returns:
```
portfolio return = Σ (factor exposures × factor returns) + specific (alpha) return
```
- the **factor component** = your returns explained by exposures to market, size, value, momentum, industry, etc. — this is *beta*, the part you could've gotten cheaply (Day 148),
- the **specific/residual component** = the return *not* explained by factors — this is your *genuine alpha* (or unexplained luck, Day 149).

This is exactly the regression decomposition of Day 148 (`r = α + β·factors + ε`), now applied to *attribute* P&L. It answers the crucial question: **how much of my return is repackaged factor exposure vs real skill?** Most "star managers," attributed honestly, turn out to be largely *factor exposure* — long value, or momentum, or small-cap — dressed up as alpha (Day 149). Attribution strips off the costume.

---

## Block 3 — Brinson attribution (allocation vs selection)
For traditional (long-only, benchmark-relative) portfolios, **Brinson attribution** decomposes the return *relative to a benchmark* into:
- **allocation effect** — did you add value by *over/underweighting sectors/asset-classes* relative to the benchmark? (top-down: "I was overweight tech, which outperformed"),
- **selection effect** — did you add value by *picking better securities within* each sector? (bottom-up: "within tech, my stocks beat the tech benchmark"),
- **interaction effect** — the cross-term.

This separates the *two distinct skills* a manager might have: **asset allocation** (getting the big sector/class bets right) vs **security selection** (stock-picking within them). A manager might be great at one and terrible at the other — Brinson reveals which, so you know where the (if any) skill truly lies.

**Concept check:** a fund beat its benchmark by 3%. Brinson attribution shows: allocation +0.2%, selection +2.9%, interaction −0.1%. What's the manager's actual skill? (**Security selection** — almost all the outperformance came from *picking better stocks within sectors* (+2.9%), not from sector allocation (+0.2%, near zero). The manager's edge is *stock-picking*, not top-down allocation. This is actionable: lean into their selection skill, don't pay them for allocation they're not adding. Without attribution, you'd just see "+3%" and not know *which* skill produced it — or whether it's repeatable.)

## Block 4 — Mathematics · risk-adjusted attribution and the skill-vs-luck problem
Raw return attribution isn't enough — you must **risk-adjust** and account for **luck**:
- **risk-adjusted attribution:** attribute not just *return* but *risk-adjusted* return (information ratio, Day 145) — a manager who made 3% by taking *huge* risk is different from one who made 3% with *low* risk. Decompose the *information ratio* and the *risk contributions* (Day 214) by source, not just the return,
- **the skill-vs-luck problem (the deepest issue):** any *single period's* attribution is dominated by **luck** (Day 149). A manager with +2.9% selection *this year* might have *zero* skill — it could be one good year out of randomness (Day 121). To distinguish skill from luck you need:
  - **statistical significance** — is the alpha/selection effect significant given its volatility and the sample length? (the t-stat of alpha, Day 148; a manager needs *many* periods of consistent positive attribution),
  - **persistence** — does the attribution *persist* across periods and out-of-sample? (consistent selection skill, not one lucky year),
  - **correction for the number of managers/trials** — with thousands of managers, *some* show great attribution by pure chance (the deflated-Sharpe logic, Day 199, applied to manager selection),
  - **an economic/process rationale** — *why* does this manager have selection skill? (a repeatable process, not luck — Day 149),
- **attribution is also a *risk-control* tool** — it reveals **unintended exposures**: if your "stock-picking" fund's returns attribute mostly to the *momentum factor*, you're unknowingly running a factor bet (Day 211), and that "alpha" will vanish (or crash) when momentum does (Day 204). Attribution catches this.

The mathematics unifies attribution with the whole skill-vs-luck apparatus: **return attribution tells you the *sources*, but only *statistical significance + persistence + trial-correction + economic rationale* tell you whether the skillful-looking sources are *real skill or luck*** — the exact same discipline as judging a strategy (Day 199) or a signal (Day 149), now applied to judging a *manager* or *your own decisions*.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · know where your returns *really* come from
Performance attribution crystallizes a principle that is both intellectually fundamental and intensely practical: **you must know where your returns *actually* come from — because the source determines whether they're repeatable, what they cost, whether they're skill or luck, and whether you're taking the risks you think you are.** This reorganizes how to evaluate *any* track record — your own or a manager's:
- **a return number is meaningless without its sources** — "+12%" could be skill, luck, factor exposure, leverage, or hidden tail risk (Day 215); attribution decomposes it into *interpretable, judgeable* pieces, turning an opaque outcome into an actionable diagnosis,
- **most "alpha" is repackaged beta** (Day 149) — honest factor attribution repeatedly reveals that star managers' returns come largely from *factor exposures* (value, momentum, size) that are *cheaply replicable*, not from genuine skill. This matters enormously for *fees* (you shouldn't pay alpha fees for beta exposure) and for *expectations* (factor returns are cyclical and can reverse — Day 204), and it's *why* attribution is central to the active-vs-passive and smart-beta revolutions,
- **single-period attribution is dominated by luck** (Days 121/149) — so attribution must be paired with *significance testing, persistence analysis, trial-correction, and economic rationale* to separate skill from luck (the *exact* discipline of Day 199's deflated Sharpe and Day 149's skill-vs-luck, now applied to evaluating managers and decisions). A great year proves nothing; *consistent, significant, economically-grounded* attribution across many periods is the evidence of skill,
- **attribution closes the feedback loop** (Day 188) — it tells you *which decisions added value and which destroyed it*, enabling systematic improvement: lean into where you have real edge (e.g. selection, not allocation), hedge out unintended factor bets, and stop doing what doesn't work. Without attribution, you can't *learn* from your track record — you just have outcomes,
- **attribution is a risk-control tool, not just a performance tool** — it surfaces *unintended exposures* (your "stock-picking" returns secretly being a momentum bet, Day 211), which is *critical* because those hidden factor bets carry risks you didn't intend and will reverse when the factor does (Day 204).

This connects to the deepest spine of the curriculum — **intellectual honesty about the sources and reality of your returns**: skill vs luck (Day 149), alpha vs beta (Day 148), deflated Sharpe / backtest overfitting (Day 199), factor decomposition (Day 211), and the feedback-loop discipline (Day 188). The unifying wisdom: **never accept a return at face value — decompose it into its sources, risk-adjust it, test whether the skillful-looking parts are statistically real and persistent (not luck or trials), check for unintended exposures, and use the decomposition to learn and improve.** The mature quant (and allocator) is relentlessly curious about *where returns come from*, because that's the only way to know whether an edge is *real, repeatable, correctly-priced, and intended* — and because the alternative (judging by the headline number) leads to overpaying for beta, mistaking luck for skill, running unintended risks, and failing to improve. **A return you can't attribute is a return you don't understand — and a return you don't understand is one you can neither trust, repeat, nor improve.** Attribution is how you turn the opaque verdict of the P&L into the actionable, honest knowledge of *why* — which is the foundation of every good decision about capital, fees, risk, and self-improvement.

**Hard puzzle:** a hedge fund has returned 15%/year for 5 years (vs the market's 9%), and markets itself as a "skilled stock-picking alpha generator" charging 2-and-20. As an allocator, how do you determine — using attribution — whether to invest, and what would make you walk away? (**Run a full attribution to decompose the 15% and test skill-vs-luck — and walk away if the "alpha" is really beta, luck, or hidden risk:** (1) **Factor attribution first** (Block 2) — regress the fund's returns on factor returns (market, size, value, momentum, quality, industry — Day 211). The critical question: **how much of the 15% (or the 6% over the market) is explained by *factor exposures* vs *genuine residual alpha*?** Very often, a "stock-picking" fund's outperformance attributes largely to **factor tilts** — e.g. it's persistently long *small-cap value* or *momentum*, which happened to outperform over these 5 years. If so, the "alpha" is **repackaged beta** (Day 149) — cheaply replicable via a smart-beta ETF at ~0.2% fees, *not* worth 2-and-20. **Walk away if the outperformance is mostly factor exposure** — you're being charged alpha fees for beta. (2) **Risk-adjust** (Block 4) — did they make 15% by taking *more risk* (higher vol, leverage, or hidden tail risk — Day 215)? Compute the **information ratio** and check the **drawdown/tail** (Day 217) — a 15% return with a 40% drawdown or a short-vol tail (Day 159) is *not* skill, it's risk-taking or a hidden short-tail bet that will eventually blow up. **Walk away if the return is just leverage/tail risk.** (3) **Skill-vs-luck statistics** (Days 149/199) — 5 years is a **small sample** (only ~5 independent annual bets — Day 191's effective-sample problem). Is the residual alpha **statistically significant** given its volatility (the t-stat)? With *thousands* of funds, *some* beat the market for 5 years by **pure chance** (Day 121), so I must **deflate for the number of managers** (Day 199) — a 5-year record is *weak* evidence. **Walk away if the alpha isn't statistically significant after trial-correction.** (4) **Persistence & process** (Day 149) — is the alpha *consistent* across the 5 years (or driven by one or two lucky years)? Is there a *repeatable economic process* explaining the edge, or just a story? **Walk away if it's one or two lucky years or there's no credible process.** (5) **Unintended exposures / crowding** (Days 211/178) — does the attribution reveal hidden concentrated factor bets that will reverse (e.g. all the "alpha" is a tech/momentum bet that crashes when momentum does, Day 204)? Is the strategy crowded? (6) **Brinson (if applicable)** — is the edge in *selection* or *allocation*, and is *that* persistent? **What makes me invest:** the attribution shows **genuine, statistically-significant, persistent *residual* alpha** (not factor exposure), achieved with *controlled* risk (reasonable drawdown, no hidden tail), explained by a *repeatable process*, surviving trial-correction — i.e., real skill worth paying for. **What makes me walk away (the common outcome):** the "15% alpha" is mostly **factor beta** (replicable cheaply), and/or **luck** (insignificant after 5 years and thousands of funds), and/or **hidden risk** (leverage/tail), and/or **unintended crowded factor bets** that will reverse. The deep lesson: **a headline return — even a *good* multi-year one — is not evidence of skill; only honest attribution (factor-decomposed, risk-adjusted, significance-tested, persistence-checked, trial-corrected) can distinguish real, fee-worthy alpha from repackaged beta, luck, and hidden risk.** This is *exactly* why sophisticated allocators run attribution before investing, why most active "alpha" doesn't survive it, and why the rise of factor investing / smart beta came from realizing that much of what was sold as alpha was cheaply-replicable factor exposure. Don't pay for beta, don't mistake luck for skill, and don't accept a return you can't attribute.)

**Career connection:** "a fund returned 15% — how do you know if it's skill?" Strong answer: **performance attribution** — decompose the return into its *sources*. First, **factor attribution** (regress on market/size/value/momentum/industry — Day 211): how much is *factor exposure* (beta, cheaply replicable — Day 149) vs genuine *residual alpha* (skill)? Most "alpha" turns out to be repackaged beta. Second, **risk-adjust** (information ratio, drawdown, tail — Days 145/215/217): was the return earned with controlled risk or just leverage/hidden tail risk? Third — the crux — **separate skill from luck** (Days 149/199): is the residual alpha **statistically significant** given the sample length, **persistent** across periods (not one lucky year), and **trial-corrected** (with thousands of funds, some look skilled by chance — deflate accordingly), with a **repeatable economic process** behind it? For long-only, **Brinson attribution** further splits the edge into *allocation* vs *selection* skill. Attribution is also **risk control** — it surfaces *unintended factor bets* that will reverse (Day 204). The bottom line: **a return number is meaningless without its sources; only honest, risk-adjusted, significance-tested attribution distinguishes real fee-worthy alpha from beta, luck, and hidden risk.** Framing attribution as *skill-vs-luck + alpha-vs-beta decomposition* — with significance, persistence, and trial-correction — is the senior allocator/risk answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Factor & allocation/selection attribution
```python
import numpy as np
import statsmodels.api as sm
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# a fund's returns: mostly factor exposure (value tilt) + a little real alpha + noise
T = 60                                            # 60 months (5 years)
mkt = rng.standard_normal(T)*0.04 + 0.006
value = rng.standard_normal(T)*0.03 + 0.002        # value factor return
true_alpha = 0.001                                 # tiny real monthly alpha
fund = 1.0*mkt + 0.8*value + true_alpha + rng.standard_normal(T)*0.01

# factor attribution: regress fund returns on factors -> alpha (intercept) vs beta
X = sm.add_constant(np.column_stack([mkt, value]))
res = sm.OLS(fund, X).fit()
alpha, b_mkt, b_val = res.params
print(f"market beta : {b_mkt:.2f}  (return from market exposure)")
print(f"value beta  : {b_val:.2f}  (return from the value factor — repackaged beta!)")
print(f"alpha       : {alpha*12:.2%}/yr,  t-stat = {res.tvalues[0]:.2f}  "
      f"({'significant' if abs(res.tvalues[0])>2 else 'NOT significant — likely luck'})")

# decompose the average return into sources
avg = fund.mean()*12
print(f"\ntotal return {avg:.1%}/yr = "
      f"market {b_mkt*mkt.mean()*12:.1%} + value {b_val*value.mean()*12:.1%} + alpha {alpha*12:.1%}")
print("→ most of the return is FACTOR exposure (beta), little is genuine alpha")
```
Attribution decomposes the fund's return into market beta, value-factor beta (repackaged beta!), and a tiny, likely-insignificant alpha — revealing that the "outperformance" is mostly cheaply-replicable factor exposure, not skill. The alpha t-stat tells you whether even that sliver is real or luck.

**Linux:** `python attribution.py | tee attr.log`; the alpha t-stat over only 60 months is usually *insignificant* — a vivid lesson that short track records can't prove skill.

## Quant Thinking Track — Decompose the outcome into its causes
A single outcome number — a return, a P&L, a result — hides the *causes* that determine whether it's repeatable, skillful, correctly-priced, and intended. The discipline of *decomposing outcomes into their sources* — and then testing whether the favorable sources are real (significant, persistent) or luck — is fundamental to honest evaluation everywhere. Never judge by the headline number; attribute it to its causes, and judge *those*. A result you can't attribute is a result you don't understand.
