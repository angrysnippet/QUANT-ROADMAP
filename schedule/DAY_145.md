## Block 1 — The definition
```
Sharpe = (E[r] − r_f) / σ
```
Excess return (over the risk-free rate `r_f`) per unit of volatility. It rotates the (σ, μ) picture from Day 144 into a *slope*: how much reward you extract per unit of risk. Higher = better risk-adjusted performance. It's unitless and lets you rank a bond strategy against a crypto strategy on one axis.

*Why it matters:* leverage is (almost) free to apply — you can dial any strategy's volatility up or down — so the thing that *can't* be changed by leverage, the **Sharpe**, is the true quality of the edge. Two strategies with the same Sharpe are equivalent up to leverage.

---

## Block 2 — Annualizing it (the √t law again)
Returns scale with `t`, volatility with `√t`, so:
```
Sharpe_annual = Sharpe_period × √(periods per year)
```
Daily Sharpe × √252, monthly × √12. A daily Sharpe of 0.1 → annual ≈ 1.6. **This assumes i.i.d. returns** — positive autocorrelation (trend) inflates it, negative deflates it; the naive √t is an approximation desks adjust for.

**Concept check:** a strategy has a *daily* Sharpe of 0.05. Annualized? (`0.05×√252 ≈ 0.79`.) Good or mediocre? (Mediocre — ~0.8; great systematic strategies live around 1–3 annualized, very-high-frequency desks higher.)

---

## Block 3 — Cousins: Information Ratio and Sortino
- **Information Ratio (IR):** Sharpe but excess return is measured *vs a benchmark* and divided by **tracking error** (std of active return). It's how active managers are actually judged. `IR = α / σ(active)`.
- **Sortino ratio:** replaces σ with **downside deviation** — only penalizes harmful volatility, not upside. Kinder to positively-skewed strategies (e.g. trend-following).
- **Calmar:** annual return / max drawdown — reward per unit of *pain*.

Each fixes a specific blind spot of vanilla Sharpe.

---

## Block 4 — How Sharpe lies to you
Sharpe is a thin summary; it hides a lot:
1. **Non-normality:** a short-vol / option-selling strategy shows a gorgeous Sharpe right up until the crash — σ doesn't see the fat left tail (Day 119). High Sharpe + negative skew = sells nickels in front of a steamroller.
2. **Autocorrelation:** illiquid/smoothed returns (private assets, some credit) *understate* σ → *overstate* Sharpe.
3. **Selection / multiple testing:** test 1,000 strategies, the best in-sample Sharpe is mostly luck (Day 124). Use the **deflated Sharpe ratio** to discount for trials.
4. **Regime dependence:** a Sharpe computed only in a bull market evaporates in the next regime.

**Hard puzzle:** strategy X has Sharpe 3.0 over 4 years selling far-OTM puts; strategy Y has Sharpe 1.0 trend-following. Which would you fund, and what one chart settles it? (Look at the *return distribution / drawdown path*: X likely has tiny gains then a catastrophic tail — negative skew, high kurtosis. Sharpe rewards X precisely because σ can't see the rare disaster. Y's positive skew is more robust. The settling chart: the histogram of returns and the equity-curve drawdowns.)

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · Sharpe is necessary, not sufficient
Use Sharpe as the *first-pass* ranking, then interrogate it:
- plot the return **distribution** (skew, kurtosis),
- plot the **drawdown** path,
- check **stability across sub-periods / regimes**,
- discount for **number of trials** (deflated Sharpe),
- confirm an **economic reason** the edge exists.

**Career connection:** "your strategy has a Sharpe of 4 — are you happy?" The *correct* answer is suspicion, not celebration: "What's the skew? Over how many independent bets? How many strategies did I try to find it? Is it just illiquidity smoothing?" Reflexive skepticism toward a too-good Sharpe is exactly what distinguishes a researcher from a backtest-overfitter.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Sharpe, Sortino, and the skew warning
```python
import numpy as np, pandas as pd
rng = np.random.default_rng(11)

def metrics(r, rf=0.0, ppy=252):
    ex = r - rf/ppy
    sharpe  = ex.mean()/r.std() * np.sqrt(ppy)
    downside= r[r < 0].std()
    sortino = ex.mean()/downside * np.sqrt(ppy)
    return round(sharpe,2), round(sortino,2), round(pd.Series(r).skew(),2)

good = rng.standard_normal(2520)*0.01 + 0.0004           # symmetric, real edge
# "short-vol": tiny steady gains + rare large losses -> high Sharpe, ugly skew
shortvol = np.where(rng.random(2520) < 0.01,
                    -rng.uniform(0.05,0.12,2520),         # rare crashes
                    0.0015)                               # steady drip
for name, r in [("normal edge", good), ("short-vol", shortvol)]:
    s, so, sk = metrics(r)
    print(f"{name:12s} Sharpe={s}  Sortino={so}  skew={sk}")
```
The short-vol line often posts a *higher Sharpe* than the honest edge — with a strongly negative skew screaming "danger." That contrast is the whole lesson.

**Linux:** `python sharpe.py | tee sharpe.log`; always print skew/kurtosis next to Sharpe so a seductive number never travels alone.

## Quant Thinking Track — Abstraction & its cost
Sharpe abstracts an entire equity curve into one scalar — powerful for ranking, dangerous because abstraction *discards* information (tails, path, trials). The mature skill is knowing *what a metric throws away* and putting that information back before you bet real money.
