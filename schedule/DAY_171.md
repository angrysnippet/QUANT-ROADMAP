## Block 1 — The premise: trade relationships, not levels
A single stock's price is ~a random walk (Day 141) — its *level* is unpredictable. But a **pair** of related stocks (Coke/Pepsi, two oil majors, a stock vs its sector ETF) often move *together* because they share risk factors. Their **spread** — a combination like `A − β·B` — can be **mean-reverting**: when it stretches too wide, it tends to snap back. Statistical arbitrage trades that snap-back:
```
Spread too HIGH  → short A, long B   (bet it narrows)
Spread too LOW   → long A, short B   (bet it widens back)
```
You're not predicting either stock — you're betting the *relationship* holds. **Relative value, not absolute value.**

*Why it matters:* this converts the unpredictable (price levels) into the predictable (a stationary spread), turning Day 142's stationarity from a diagnostic into a money-maker.

---

## Block 2 — Why the spread can be market-neutral
If `A` and `B` share the market factor (both have β≈1 to the market), then long-A/short-B **cancels the market exposure** — the position is roughly **market-neutral** (Day 148: you've hedged out beta). What remains is the *idiosyncratic* relationship between the two names. Consequences:
- you make money in up *or* down markets, as long as the spread reverts,
- your risk is the *relationship breaking*, not the market falling,
- returns are (ideally) uncorrelated with the market → a pure **alpha** stream (Day 149) and a genuine diversifier (Day 146).

This market-neutrality is the whole appeal: an edge that doesn't depend on calling market direction.

---

## Block 3 — The z-score: how stretched is the rubber band?
Standardize the spread into a **z-score** (Day 141's safe rolling stats):
```
z_t = (spread_t − rolling_mean) / rolling_std
```
Trade the extremes:
- `z > +2` → spread unusually high → **short the spread** (short A, long βB),
- `z < −2` → unusually low → **long the spread**,
- `z → 0` → revert toward mean → **exit / take profit**.

The z-score turns "the rubber band looks stretched" into a precise, backtestable rule. **Crucial:** the mean/std must be **rolling and lookahead-safe** (`.shift(1)`, Day 141) — using the full-sample mean is the classic stat-arb backtest lie.

**Concept check:** a pair's spread sits at z = +3. What's the trade, and what's the bet? (Short the spread — short A, long βB — betting it reverts toward z = 0. The risk: it's z = +3 *because the relationship is breaking* (a merger, a fundamental divergence), not because it's randomly stretched.)

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · what makes a spread tradeable
For a spread to be a *tradeable* mean-reverter, it must be **stationary** — and that's a testable property (Day 142):
- regress `A` on `B` → the hedge ratio `β` and the residual (spread) `ε_t = A_t − βB_t`,
- run an **ADF test** on `ε_t`: if it **rejects the unit root**, the spread is stationary → mean-reverting → tradeable. This is **cointegration** (Day 172, formally).
- the spread's **speed of reversion** and **half-life** (Day 173) tell you how fast it snaps back, hence the holding period.

Without stationarity you have a **spurious pair** (Day 142's Granger–Newbold trap): two correlated-looking random walks that drift apart and *never* revert — a backtest that looks great and bleeds money live. **Correlation is not cointegration**, and confusing them is the #1 rookie stat-arb error.

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · stat arb is the LLN as a business
Step back at the philosophy. *Any single pair* is a weak, noisy bet that often fails (the relationship can break). The edge isn't one trade — it's the **Law of Large Numbers** (Day 121) and the **Fundamental Law** (Day 149, `IR = IC·√Breadth`): run *hundreds* of weakly-predictable, roughly-independent mean-reversion bets, and the noise diversifies away while the small per-bet edge accumulates into a strong, steady return. This is exactly how the big quant funds (Renaissance, D.E. Shaw, the original Morgan Stanley pairs desk) were built: **not one brilliant prediction, but thousands of tiny statistical edges, repeated.** Stat arb *is* breadth-as-Sharpe made into an industry — the entire probability/portfolio stack of Phases 6–7 turned into a trading operation.

**Hard puzzle:** two stocks have a 0.95 *correlation* of daily returns. Is that a good pair to trade? (Not necessarily — **correlation ≠ cointegration.** High return-correlation means they *move together day-to-day*, but says nothing about whether their *price spread is stationary*. Two stocks can be highly correlated yet have a spread that random-walks apart forever (no reversion → no trade, and big losses when it drifts). You must test the **spread for stationarity** (ADF / cointegration, Day 172), not the returns for correlation. This single distinction separates real pairs trading from the naive version that blows up.)

**Career connection:** "how does pairs trading work, and why is it market-neutral?" Strong answer: find two cointegrated names, trade the *spread* `A − βB` when its z-score is stretched, betting on mean reversion; it's market-neutral because long-A/short-B cancels the shared market beta, leaving a pure idiosyncratic, alpha bet — and the key technical point is that you test the **spread for stationarity (cointegration), not the prices for correlation.** Raising cointegration-vs-correlation unprompted marks you as someone who's actually done it.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · A first pairs spread and z-score signal
```python
import numpy as np, pandas as pd
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# simulate two COINTEGRATED prices: shared random-walk factor + stationary spread
T = 1500
common = np.cumsum(rng.standard_normal(T)*0.01)          # shared trend (non-stationary)
spread = np.zeros(T)
for t in range(1,T):                                     # OU-like stationary spread
    spread[t] = 0.95*spread[t-1] + rng.standard_normal()*0.5
A = 100*np.exp(common + 0.01*spread)
B = 100*np.exp(common)
A, B = pd.Series(A), pd.Series(B)

# hedge ratio via rolling regression, then the spread and a SAFE z-score
beta = (A.rolling(60).cov(B) / B.rolling(60).var())
sprd = np.log(A) - beta*np.log(B)
z = (sprd - sprd.rolling(60).mean().shift(1)) / sprd.rolling(60).std().shift(1)

signal = np.where(z > 2, -1, np.where(z < -2, 1, 0))     # short/long the spread
print("z-score tail values:", z.dropna().iloc[-5:].round(2).tolist())
print("fraction of days with an active signal:",
      round(np.mean(np.abs(signal[~np.isnan(z)])), 3))
```
You construct the spread, standardize it lookahead-safely, and generate entry signals at |z|>2 — the skeleton of every pairs strategy, ready for the cointegration test (Day 172) and a proper backtest (Day 174).

**Linux:** `python pairs.py | tee pairs.log`; plot the spread and z-score to *see* the mean reversion you're betting on.

## Quant Thinking Track — Predictability hides in combinations
The master insight of stat arb: **even when every individual series is unpredictable, a clever *combination* can be predictable.** This is a search for *stationary structure in a non-stationary world* — and it generalizes far beyond pairs (baskets, factors, residuals, Day 176). Training yourself to ask "what *combination* of these unpredictable things is stable?" is a quant superpower that applies to signals, hedges, and risk alike.
