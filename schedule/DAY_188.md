## Block 1 — Implementation shortfall: the master metric
**Implementation shortfall (IS)** = the difference between the **decision price** (the price when you *decided* to trade) and the **actual average execution price**, including everything:
```
IS = (avg execution price − decision price) × shares  +  cost of unexecuted shares
```
It captures the *full* cost of turning an investment decision into a realized position — spread paid, market impact, timing drift while working the order, fees, *and* the opportunity cost of any shares you failed to execute. IS is the **honest, all-in number**: it's exactly the Almgren–Chriss objective (Day 185) measured after the fact, and it's how serious funds judge their trading. Everything else (VWAP slippage, etc.) is a component or proxy of it.

*Why it matters:* a great signal (Phase 10) with sloppy execution can have *negative* net alpha. IS is where alpha meets reality — it's the bridge between the backtest and the bank account.

---

## Block 2 — Benchmarks: cost relative to what?
"Cost" is meaningless without a reference price. The common TCA benchmarks, each answering a different question:
- **Arrival price (decision price)** → measures **implementation shortfall** (the truest cost; "how much did I slip from when I decided?").
- **VWAP** (volume-weighted average price over the period) → "did I beat the market's average?" — the most common institutional benchmark; beating VWAP means you traded better than the day's typical participant.
- **TWAP** → "did I beat a steady, time-spread execution?"
- **Close price** → for strategies benchmarked to the close.
- **Pre-trade estimate** → compare *realized* cost to the *model-predicted* cost (calibrate your impact model, Day 179).

Choosing the benchmark frames the judgment — and can be gamed (beating VWAP is easy if you *are* most of the volume). IS (arrival) is the hardest to game and the most economically meaningful.

---

## Block 3 — Decomposing the cost (attribution)
Good TCA doesn't just report a number — it **attributes** it to sources so you can improve:
- **Spread cost** — half-spread paid for crossing (Day 181).
- **Market impact** — how much *your own trading* moved the price (temporary + permanent, Day 184).
- **Timing/delay cost** — adverse price drift between decision and execution (the Almgren–Chriss timing risk, Day 185, realized).
- **Opportunity cost** — value lost on shares you *couldn't* execute (the order ran out of time / liquidity).
- **Fees/rebates** — exchange and broker costs (Day 187).

This decomposition tells you *what to fix*: high impact → trade slower/stealthier; high timing cost → trade faster/front-load; high opportunity cost → you were too passive. **Attribution turns measurement into a feedback loop** — exactly the diagnostic discipline of the whole course.

**Concept check:** your TCA shows low spread/impact cost but high *timing* cost. What went wrong, and what's the fix? (You traded **too passively/slowly** — minimal footprint, but the price **drifted away** while you dawdled, so you paid in adverse drift (timing risk) what you saved in impact. Fix: trade **faster / front-load** (higher Almgren–Chriss `λ_risk`, Day 185) to reduce exposure to drift. The decomposition pinpoints that the error was *speed*, not *technique*.)

## Block 4 — Mathematics · measuring liquidity itself
TCA also requires *quantifying liquidity* — and there are several standard measures, each capturing a facet:
- **Quoted spread** = ask − bid (the headline cost of immediacy).
- **Effective spread** = `2 × |trade price − mid|` — the *actual* cost paid, which can be *less* than quoted (price improvement) or *more* (walking the book). The honest realized spread.
- **Realized spread** = the maker's *actual earned* spread after the price moves post-trade (effective spread minus adverse selection) — separates the spread into **liquidity-provision revenue** vs **adverse-selection loss** (Day 183), directly testing Glosten–Milgrom.
- **Amihud illiquidity** = average `|return| / volume` — how much price moves per dollar traded (an empirical cousin of Kyle's λ, Day 183).
- **Depth / market impact curves** — how cost scales with size (the √-law, Day 179).

These let you compare liquidity across stocks, venues, and time, and **predict** execution cost *before* trading (pre-trade TCA) so you can choose the right strategy and size (capacity, Day 179). Note how **effective spread minus realized spread = the adverse-selection component** — TCA empirically *decomposes the spread into Day 182's inventory/processing part and Day 183's information part.* The whole module's theory becomes measurable.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · close the loop — measure, attribute, improve
TCA embodies the scientific feedback loop that runs through this entire roadmap: **you cannot improve what you don't measure, and measurement without attribution is just a scoreboard.** The mature execution process:
1. **Pre-trade:** estimate expected cost (impact model, liquidity measures) → choose strategy, size, urgency.
2. **Trade:** execute via the chosen algo (Day 185).
3. **Post-trade:** measure IS, decompose into spread/impact/timing/opportunity, **compare to the pre-trade estimate.**
4. **Improve:** recalibrate the impact model, adjust algo parameters, fix the dominant cost source — then repeat.

This is the **Learn → Implement → Review → Repeat** cycle from the README, applied to trading itself. It's also the same loop as Kalman filtering (Day 175: predict, observe, update) and honest backtesting (Day 174: hypothesize, test, falsify). The unifying meta-skill of the quant: **build a measurement-and-feedback system around every consequential activity**, so performance becomes *systematically improvable* rather than a matter of luck or feel. Execution that isn't measured drifts; execution that's TCA'd compounds small improvements into a durable cost edge — which, at scale, rivals alpha itself.

**Hard puzzle:** Fund A reports it "consistently beats VWAP" and concludes its execution is excellent. Why might this be misleading, and what would you measure instead? (Beating VWAP can be **illusory or gamed**: (1) if the fund is a *large* share of the day's volume, *it* substantially *determines* VWAP, so "beating" its own average is near-meaningless; (2) VWAP rewards trading *spread out over the day*, which can **incur large timing/opportunity cost** — you beat VWAP while the price drifted far from your *decision* price, so you "won" the benchmark but *lost* economically; (3) VWAP ignores the cost of **unexecuted** shares. The economically correct metric is **implementation shortfall vs the arrival/decision price** — it captures impact *and* timing *and* opportunity cost and is far harder to game. Decompose IS into spread/impact/timing/opportunity to see *where* cost arose. "Beats VWAP" is a scoreboard that can flatter a desk that's actually slipping badly from its decision prices. Measure IS, not VWAP-beating.)

**Career connection:** "how do you know if you executed a trade well?" Strong answer: measure **implementation shortfall** — the all-in slippage from the **decision/arrival price**, including impact, timing drift, fees, and the opportunity cost of unexecuted shares — and **decompose** it into spread, market impact, timing, and opportunity components so you know *what to fix*. I'd be wary of VWAP-beating (gameable, ignores timing/opportunity cost) and would close the loop by comparing realized cost to my **pre-trade estimate** to recalibrate. Leading with IS and cost attribution (not VWAP) signals real TCA literacy.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Implementation shortfall & cost attribution
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

decision_price = 100.00                              # price when we decided to buy 10,000 shares
shares_target  = 10_000
# simulate working the order: fills drift up (impact + adverse timing), some shares unfilled
fills_px  = decision_price + np.cumsum(rng.random(8)*0.01)   # 8 child fills, rising
fills_qty = np.array([1200]*7 + [800])                       # 9,200 filled, 800 missed
filled    = fills_qty.sum()
avg_px    = np.average(fills_px, weights=fills_qty)
final_px  = fills_px[-1]                                     # price drifted to here

# implementation shortfall = execution slippage + opportunity cost of unfilled
exec_cost = (avg_px - decision_price)*filled
opp_cost  = (final_px - decision_price)*(shares_target - filled)   # missed shares cost
IS_total  = exec_cost + opp_cost
print(f"avg execution price : {avg_px:.4f}  vs decision {decision_price:.2f}")
print(f"execution slippage  : ${exec_cost:,.0f}")
print(f"opportunity cost     : ${opp_cost:,.0f}  (800 shares missed as price rose)")
print(f"IMPLEMENTATION SHORTFALL: ${IS_total:,.0f}  ({IS_total/(shares_target*decision_price)*1e4:.1f} bps)")
```
The all-in cost in basis points — including the often-forgotten **opportunity cost** of unexecuted shares — is the number that actually hits the fund's return, and the decomposition shows whether to blame slippage or under-execution.

**Linux:** `python tca.py | tee tca.log`; compare IS against a VWAP benchmark on the same fills to see how the choice of benchmark changes the verdict.
