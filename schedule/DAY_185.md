## Block 1 — The trader's dilemma
Liquidating (or building) a large position forces a trade-off with no free answer:
- **Trade fast** → high **market impact** (you walk the book, pay temporary impact, Day 184), but low **timing risk** (you're done before the price can wander).
- **Trade slow** → low impact (small footprint, book heals between trades), but high **timing risk** (the price can drift adversely while you're still working the order — and your unexecuted shares are exposed to volatility).

```
Total execution cost = expected impact cost + risk of price drift (timing risk)
```
There is no universally "right" speed — it depends on how impactful trading is, how volatile the stock is, and **how risk-averse you are.** This is the same risk–reward structure as portfolio choice (Day 144), now in the time dimension.

*Why it matters:* getting this wrong is expensive at size. Implementation shortfall — the gap between the decision price and the achieved price — is *the* metric execution is judged on (Day 188), and Almgren–Chriss is how you minimize it.

---

## Block 2 — Almgren–Chriss: the dilemma as optimization
Almgren & Chriss (2000) formalized it. Choose a **trading trajectory** — how many shares to execute in each time slice — to minimize:
```
minimize:  E[cost]  +  λ_risk · Var[cost]
```
where:
- **E[cost]** = accumulated **market impact** (more from trading faster),
- **Var[cost]** = **timing risk** = volatility of the unexecuted position over the schedule (more from trading slower),
- **λ_risk** = your **risk aversion** (the one knob that picks your spot on the trade-off).

The solution is a **deterministic optimal trajectory.** With linear impact and constant volatility, it's an **exponential** schedule: a risk-averse trader **front-loads** (trades faster early to cut timing risk), while a risk-neutral trader (`λ_risk = 0`) just trades at a **constant rate (TWAP)** to minimize impact. Risk aversion bends the trajectory from flat (TWAP) toward front-loaded.

---

## Block 3 — The efficient frontier of trading
Sweep `λ_risk` and you trace an **efficient frontier** — exactly like Markowitz (Day 147), but the axes are *execution* quantities:
- x-axis = **timing risk** (variance of cost),
- y-axis = **expected impact cost**.

Each point is an optimal trajectory for some risk aversion; you can't reduce expected impact without taking more timing risk, and vice versa. **Faster execution = up-left (more impact, less risk); slower = down-right (less impact, more risk).** Your choice of `λ_risk` picks the point. This is *literally Markowitz's efficient frontier* transplanted to execution — the same mean–variance optimization (Day 147), the same "you can't get something for nothing," the same risk-aversion-selects-the-point logic. The whole portfolio-theory machinery reappears, now optimizing *how to trade* rather than *what to hold*.

**Concept check:** a portfolio manager must liquidate a volatile stock and is very risk-averse about price drift. Front-load or back-load the schedule? (**Front-load** — trade more aggressively early to reduce the *unexecuted* position quickly, cutting exposure to the stock's volatility. They accept higher impact cost to buy down timing risk — a high-`λ_risk` point on the frontier. A risk-neutral trader would spread evenly, TWAP, accepting drift risk to minimize impact.)

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the benchmark algorithms
Almgren–Chriss is the *theory*; in practice execution algos are its special cases or benchmarks:
- **TWAP (Time-Weighted Average Price):** trade equal size each time slice → the **risk-neutral** AC solution; minimizes impact, ignores timing risk.
- **VWAP (Volume-Weighted Average Price):** trade in proportion to *expected volume* each slice → blend into the day's natural liquidity, minimizing footprint relative to market activity; the most common benchmark.
- **Implementation Shortfall (IS) / Arrival Price:** minimize the gap to the price *when the decision was made* → this is **exactly the Almgren–Chriss objective**, front-loading per risk aversion; what you use when you fear the price running away.
- **POV (Percentage of Volume):** trade a fixed % of market volume — adaptive to realized liquidity.

The choice of benchmark *is* a choice of objective: VWAP says "match the market," IS says "minimize slippage from my decision price (AC)," TWAP says "be steady and low-impact." Modern algos add real-time adaptation (speed up when liquidity is cheap/the book is favorable, slow down when impact is high — using order-flow signals, Day 184), but they're all descendants of the AC impact-vs-risk frontier.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · execution is portfolio theory in the time dimension
The deep beauty of Almgren–Chriss is that **optimal execution is mean–variance optimization (Day 147) applied across time instead of across assets.** Line them up:
| Markowitz (Day 147) | Almgren–Chriss (Day 185) |
|---|---|
| choose weights across **assets** | choose trade sizes across **time** |
| minimize variance for given return | minimize impact for given timing risk |
| risk aversion picks frontier point | risk aversion picks trajectory |
| `wᵀΣw` (portfolio risk) | timing risk of unexecuted position |

It's the **same optimization, same efficient frontier, same risk-aversion knob** — transplanted from "what to hold" to "how to trade what you hold." This is the recurring payoff of a principles-first education (Day 169's transfer lesson): you learned mean–variance optimization once, and it prices portfolios, combines signals (Day 177), *and* schedules trades. The unifying meta-idea — **trade off expected cost against its variance, and let risk aversion choose the point** — is one of the most reused templates in all of quant finance. See a new "how aggressive should I be?" problem and you already have the frontier.

**Hard puzzle:** two traders must each liquidate the same large position in the same stock by end of day. Trader X is a market maker who's risk-neutral about this flow; Trader Y is a fund manager terrified the stock will gap down on news before they're out. Describe each one's optimal Almgren–Chriss schedule and *why they differ despite identical impact parameters.* (**X (risk-neutral, λ_risk≈0):** minimize *expected impact* only → trade at a **constant rate (TWAP)**, spreading evenly to keep the footprint smallest, indifferent to price drift. **Y (highly risk-averse, large λ_risk):** heavily **front-load** → trade fast early to slash the *unexecuted* position and its exposure to a possible gap, *accepting higher impact cost* as the price of reduced timing risk. Same impact and volatility parameters, but **different points on the same efficient frontier** because **risk aversion differs** — exactly as two investors with identical assets but different risk tolerance hold different portfolios on Markowitz's frontier (Day 147). The execution schedule encodes *risk preference*, not just market mechanics — which is why "what's the optimal execution?" has no single answer without knowing `λ_risk`.)

**Career connection:** "you have to buy a large position — how do you decide how fast to trade?" Strong answer: it's the **Almgren–Chriss** trade-off — trading fast incurs **market impact**, trading slow incurs **timing risk** (price drift on the unexecuted shares), so I minimize `E[impact] + λ·Var[cost]`, where risk aversion `λ` picks a point on an **efficient frontier** identical in structure to Markowitz's. Risk-neutral → TWAP/VWAP (minimize impact); risk-averse → front-load (IS/arrival-price), and a real algo adapts speed to live liquidity. Framing execution as *mean–variance optimization across time* is the senior answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · The Almgren–Chriss optimal trajectory
```python
import numpy as np

def almgren_chriss(X, T_steps, sigma, eta, lam):
    """X=shares to liquidate, eta=temporary impact, lam=risk aversion."""
    # closed-form: kappa sets the curvature; risk-averse => front-loaded exponential
    kappa = np.sqrt(lam*sigma**2/eta)
    t = np.arange(T_steps+1)
    # remaining holdings trajectory (Almgren–Chriss)
    if kappa < 1e-8:
        holdings = X*(1 - t/T_steps)                  # risk-neutral => linear (TWAP)
    else:
        holdings = X*np.sinh(kappa*(T_steps - t))/np.sinh(kappa*T_steps)
    trades = -np.diff(holdings)                        # shares executed each step
    return holdings, trades

X, T, sigma, eta = 1_000_000, 20, 0.02, 1e-6
for lam, label in [(0.0,"risk-neutral (TWAP)"), (1e-6,"moderate"), (1e-4,"very risk-averse")]:
    h, tr = almgren_chriss(X, T, sigma, eta, lam)
    front = tr[:5].sum()/X
    print(f"{label:22s}: first-25%-of-time executes {front:.0%} of order  (front-loading)")
```
As risk aversion rises, the optimal schedule **front-loads** — executing more of the order early to cut timing risk — while the risk-neutral case is flat (TWAP). The single `λ` knob slides you along the frontier.

**Linux:** `python almgren.py | tee ac.log`; sweep `lam` and `sigma` — higher volatility *or* risk aversion both push toward faster front-loaded trading.

## Quant Thinking Track — The same template, a new axis
Almgren–Chriss is the clearest proof that **a good framework transfers across domains by changing what the axes mean.** Mean–variance optimization went from assets (Markowitz) to time (execution) with its entire structure intact — frontier, risk-aversion knob, no-free-lunch geometry. When you deeply understand a *template* rather than a *special case*, you get whole new applications for free. Ask of any optimization you learn: *what else has this exact shape?*
