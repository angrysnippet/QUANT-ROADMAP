## Block 1 — The mechanic: stay delta-neutral
To replicate (or hedge) a call, hold `−Δ` shares against it so the *combined* position has zero delta — immune to small moves in `S`. But Δ changes as `S` moves (that's gamma), so you must **rebalance continuously**: as `S` rises, Δ rises, buy more stock; as `S` falls, Δ falls, sell. This **self-financing** buy-high/sell-low-of-your-hedge process *is* the replication portfolio from Day 153. Do it perfectly in continuous time with the right σ, and the hedge exactly funds the option — proving the BS price.

*Why it matters:* this is what options market-makers literally do all day. They warehouse options and neutralize delta, leaving themselves exposed only to the Greeks they have a view on (gamma/vega).

---

## Block 2 — Gamma scalping: where the P&L comes from
A delta-hedged **long-gamma** position makes money from *movement*. Each rebalance, you mechanically **buy low and sell high** as the stock oscillates around your hedge — "scalping" the gamma. Over a period:
```
Hedging P&L ≈ ½ · Γ · (realized move)² − theta paid
            ≈ ½ Σ Γ·(ΔS)²  −  ½σ²_impl·S²·Γ·Δt
```
- If the stock moves **more** than the implied vol you paid → realized gamma gains **beat** theta → **profit**.
- If it moves **less** → theta bleed **wins** → **loss**.

**The punchline:** a delta-hedged option's P&L ≈ `½ Σ Γ·S²·(σ²_realized − σ²_implied)·Δt`. **You are long realized vol and short implied vol.** That's the entire economic content of options trading, stated as a hedge.

---

## Block 3 — Why real hedging is imperfect
BS assumes continuous, costless trading. Reality:
- **Discrete rebalancing:** you hedge hourly/daily, not continuously → **hedging error** with variance that *grows as you rebalance less often* (and shrinks like the rebalance interval). You can't capture every wiggle.
- **Transaction costs:** more rebalancing = less error but higher costs — a genuine trade-off (Leland's adjustment widens the effective spread).
- **Gaps/jumps:** overnight gaps blow through your hedge — gamma helps but jump risk is real and *unhedgeable* with stock alone (Day 158).
- **Wrong σ:** you hedge with *some* vol; P&L depends on the path and the gap between hedge-vol and realized.

So perfect replication is an idealization; real desks run *residual* gamma/vega risk and price it in.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the hedging-error / vol-arbitrage P&L
For a position hedged at implied vol `σ_i` while the stock realizes vol `σ_r`, the (path-dependent) total P&L of the delta-hedged long option is:
```
P&L ≈ ½ ∫₀ᵀ Γ_t · S_t² · (σ_r² − σ_i²) dt
```
This beautiful result (Carr, "FAQs in option pricing") says: **you get paid the variance spread, weighted by dollar-gamma `Γ·S²` along the path.** Consequences:
- the P&L is *path-dependent* — *when* the vol shows up (at high-gamma vs low-gamma times) matters,
- to get a **path-independent** pure-variance bet you need a portfolio with constant dollar-gamma → that's a **variance swap** (Day 159),
- discrete hedging adds a mean-zero but positive-variance error term.

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · options are a *volatility* instrument
The deepest reframe of this whole module: **an option is not a directional bet — once delta-hedged, it's a bet on volatility.** Buy options → long vol (want big moves). Sell options → short vol (want calm), earning the variance risk premium (Day 143) but bearing crash risk. Market-makers are indifferent to direction; they live in (gamma, theta, vega) space, harvesting the spread between what they paid (implied) and what unfolds (realized). This is why "vol" is treated as a tradeable asset class with its own term structure and skew (Days 156, 159).

**Hard puzzle:** you buy a 1-month ATM straddle, delta-hedge daily, and the stock ends *exactly* where it started — but it whipsawed violently every day in between. Did you make money? (Likely **yes**, despite zero net move. Gamma scalping captured the daily oscillations: each day you bought low / sold high rebalancing the hedge, accumulating `½Γ(ΔS)²` gains. If the *realized* daily vol exceeded the implied vol you paid, the gamma gains beat theta. Net direction is irrelevant — *realized variance* is what you're long. This is the single most counterintuitive truth in options.)

**Career connection:** "you think a stock will be volatile but you don't know the direction — what do you do, and how do you make money?" Strong answer: buy a straddle/strangle and **delta-hedge** it; you're now long gamma/vega, and you profit if **realized vol > implied vol** via gamma scalping, paying theta as rent. Explaining the hedge *and* the realized-vs-implied P&L mechanism is exactly what vol-desk interviews probe.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Simulate delta-hedging P&L
```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import norm
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

def bs_delta(S,K,r,sig,T):
    d1 = (np.log(S/K)+(r+0.5*sig**2)*T)/(sig*np.sqrt(T)); return norm.cdf(d1)

S0,K,r,T = 100,100,0.0,1/12
sig_impl = 0.20                       # vol we PAY (hedge at)
steps = 21; dt = T/steps

def hedge_pnl(sig_real):
    pnl = []
    for _ in range(20000):
        S = S0; cash = 0.0; pos = -bs_delta(S,K,r,sig_impl,T)   # short hedge for long call
        cash -= pos*S
        for i in range(1, steps+1):
            S *= np.exp(-0.5*sig_real**2*dt + sig_real*np.sqrt(dt)*rng.standard_normal())
            tau = max(T - i*dt, 1e-6)
            newpos = -bs_delta(S,K,r,sig_impl,tau)
            cash -= (newpos-pos)*S; pos = newpos
        cash += pos*S + max(S-K,0)            # unwind hedge + option payoff
        pnl.append(cash)
    return np.mean(pnl)

print("hedged long-call P&L when realized < implied (0.10):", round(hedge_pnl(0.10),3))
print("hedged long-call P&L when realized = implied (0.20):", round(hedge_pnl(0.20),3))
print("hedged long-call P&L when realized > implied (0.35):", round(hedge_pnl(0.35),3))
```
P&L is ≈0 when realized = implied, **negative** when the stock is calmer than you paid for, **positive** when it's wilder — the `σ_r² − σ_i²` law, demonstrated.

**Linux:** `python hedge.py | tee hedge.log`; vary `steps` to watch discrete-hedging error shrink as you rebalance more often.
