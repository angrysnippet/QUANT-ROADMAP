## Block 1 — Three theories of the curve's shape
- **Pure Expectations Hypothesis (EH):** long rates = the average of *expected* future short rates. Forwards are unbiased forecasts; the curve slopes up only if the market expects rate hikes. Clean, intuitive — and empirically *incomplete*.
- **Liquidity/Term-Premium Theory:** investors demand *extra* yield to hold longer (more duration risk, Day 162), so long rates = expected short rates **+ a term premium**. This explains why the curve is *usually* upward-sloping even when no hikes are expected.
- **Market Segmentation / Preferred Habitat:** different investors (banks short, pensions/insurers long) have maturity preferences, so supply/demand in each bucket also shapes the curve.

Reality = a blend: **forward ≈ expected future rate + term premium.** Disentangling the two is one of the central problems in fixed-income research (and macro).

---

## Block 2 — Forwards are *biased* forecasts
Critical subtlety: the forward rate is the market's *risk-neutral* expectation, **not** its real-world (P-measure) forecast — the same Q-vs-P distinction as options (Day 153). Because of the positive term premium, **forwards tend to sit *above* the rates that actually materialize**, on average. Empirically, the EH is rejected: forwards over-predict future rate rises. That rejection isn't noise — it *is* the term premium, and harvesting it is the carry trade (Block 3). **A forward is what you can *lock in* today, not what the market truly *expects* — and the gap is your expected profit for bearing duration risk.**

**Concept check:** the 1y-into-1y forward is 5%, but the market's genuine *expectation* of the 1-year rate in a year is 4.3%. Where's the 0.7%? (The **term premium** — compensation for the risk of holding the longer position. It's exactly the EH's failure, and the source of carry returns.)

---

## Block 3 — Carry and roll-down (making money when nothing happens)
The classic fixed-income return decomposition for holding a bond over a period, *if the curve doesn't move*:
- **Carry** = yield income earned minus financing cost (coupon/yield minus the short-rate you fund at). On an upward-sloping curve, a long bond yields more than your funding → **positive carry**.
- **Roll-down** = as time passes, the bond "rolls down" the curve to a *shorter* maturity with a *lower* yield (on an upward curve), so its price *rises* — a capital gain purely from aging.

```
Total return (static curve) ≈ Carry + Roll-down
```
A **steep** curve offers rich carry + roll-down; the trade *loses* only if rates rise enough to overcome it (i.e. by *more than the forwards already implied*). This is "**riding the yield curve**" — one of the oldest rates strategies, and a direct harvest of the term premium.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the breakeven and the term-premium edge
The carry/roll trade has a clean breakeven: you profit unless rates rise by **more than the forward curve already priced in.** Formally, your holding-period return beats cash unless the realized future short rate exceeds the *forward* rate:
```
Profit ⟺ realized future rate < forward rate
```
Since forwards are biased *upward* by the term premium (Block 2), this inequality holds *more often than not* — which is why carry strategies have a positive long-run expected return. But the **risk profile is asymmetric and ugly**: steady small gains (the carry drip) punctuated by occasional large losses when rates spike — *negative skew* again (Days 145, 159). Carry trades across asset classes (rates, FX, credit) share this "**up the stairs, down the elevator**" signature; the Sharpe looks great until it doesn't.

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · carry is a risk premium, sized like insurance
The unifying thread of the entire curriculum surfaces once more: **carry, the term premium, the variance risk premium (Day 159), the equity risk premium, and the skew premium (Day 156) are all the same creature** — compensation for bearing a risk others want to offload, paying steadily and then biting in the tail. So the discipline is identical everywhere:
- it's **real** (the EH is rejected; term premia are positive on average),
- it's **not free** (you're short duration risk / a fat left tail),
- it must be **sized for the tail, not the Sharpe** (leverage maximization kills carry traders),
- and it's **diversifiable across sources** (rates carry + FX carry + vol carry are imperfectly correlated — though they all tend to blow up *together* in a crisis, the correlation-breakdown warning of Day 146).

**Hard puzzle:** a steep yield curve offers 3% of annual carry+roll on a leveraged Treasury position. A colleague says "free 3%, just lever it 5×." What's wrong? (You're harvesting the **term premium** — real, but it's payment for *duration/rate-spike risk*, not free. Levering 5× turns a modest selloff into a wipeout: a parallel +60 bp move on a duration-7 bond is ≈ −4.2%, ×5 = −21% — your "free" 3% gone many times over, and curve steepening or a funding-rate spike makes it worse. The 1994 bond massacre and countless levered-carry blowups are this exact trade. Size it by the tail loss, not the carry.)

**Career connection:** "the yield curve is upward-sloping — how would you make money, and what's the risk?" Strong answer: **carry and roll-down** — earn the yield spread over funding and the price gain from rolling down the curve as the bond ages, profiting unless rates rise by *more than the forwards already imply*; the risk is a rate spike / curve steepening, with the classic negative-skew "steady gains then sharp loss" profile, so you size it like selling insurance. Framing carry as a *term-premium harvest with tail risk* is the senior view.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Decompose carry + roll-down
```python
import numpy as np

# upward-sloping spot curve (annual)
mats = np.array([0.5,1,2,3,5,7,10])
spot = np.array([0.030,0.032,0.035,0.037,0.040,0.041,0.042])

def df(t):                                   # log-linear interp on spot
    z = np.interp(t, mats, spot); return (1+z)**(-t)

def price_zero(T):  return 100*df(T)

# hold a 5y zero for 1 year on a STATIC curve: carry+roll return
P0 = price_zero(5)
P1 = price_zero(4)                           # one year later it's a 4y bond (same curve)
funding = spot[1]                            # fund at the 1y rate
total_ret = P1/P0 - 1
carry_roll = total_ret                       # for a zero, all return is price appreciation
print(f"5y zero held 1yr (static curve): {total_ret*100:.2f}%  vs funding {funding*100:.2f}%")
print(f"net carry+roll over funding    : {(total_ret-funding)*100:.2f}%")

# breakeven: how much can the 4y rate rise before the trade breaks even?
z4 = np.interp(4, mats, spot)
be = (P0*(1+funding))**(-1/4)*100**(1/4)     # rough breakeven yield
print(f"4y rate today {z4*100:.2f}% ; trade profits unless it rises past ~the forward")
```
On a static upward curve the long bond out-earns its funding — the carry+roll edge — and you can see how a rate rise eats into it.

## Quant Thinking Track — Expectations vs premia, everywhere
The EH-vs-term-premium debate is one instance of a universal question: *how much of a price is genuine expectation, and how much is risk compensation?* It recurs in the equity premium, credit spreads (Day 167), implied vol, and FX forwards. Training yourself to split any "forecast" embedded in a market price into *expectation + risk premium* is one of the most transferable analytical habits in quant finance.
