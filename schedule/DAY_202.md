## Block 1 — ARCH: variance depends on past shocks
Engle's insight (Nobel 2003): model the *conditional variance* as a function of past squared shocks. **ARCH(q):**
```
σ²_t = ω + α₁ε²_{t−1} + … + α_q ε²_{t−q}
```
A big shock last period (`ε²` large) → high variance now → volatility clusters (Day 143). This was revolutionary: it let the variance be **time-varying and predictable**, unlike the constant-σ assumption of Black–Scholes (Day 153) and basic statistics. But pure ARCH needs many lags to capture persistent volatility — hence GARCH.

*Why it matters:* volatility is the *one thing* in markets that's genuinely forecastable (Day 143), and it drives risk, option prices, position sizing, and VaR. Modelling it well is foundational.

---

## Block 2 — GARCH(1,1): the workhorse
**GARCH** (Bollerslev) adds a lagged *variance* term, so variance is persistent without needing many lags:
```
σ²_t = ω + α·ε²_{t−1} + β·σ²_{t−1}
```
- **ω** — baseline (long-run) variance level,
- **α** — reaction to the latest shock (how *jumpy*),
- **β** — persistence of variance (how *long storms last*),
- **α + β** — total persistence (`< 1` for stationarity); typically **0.95–0.99** in real markets (very persistent, slowly mean-reverting).
- **long-run variance** = `ω/(1 − α − β)`.

GARCH(1,1) with just 3 parameters captures volatility clustering remarkably well — it's the *workhorse* of financial econometrics. (EWMA from Day 143 is the special case `ω=0, α+β=1`.)

**Concept check:** a fitted GARCH has α=0.07, β=0.92. Persistence and interpretation? (α+β = 0.99 → *extremely* persistent volatility: shocks to variance decay very slowly (half-life ≈ `ln2/(−ln 0.99) ≈ 69` days). Storms linger for months. The variance is mean-reverting but only barely — typical of equity indices.)

---

## Block 3 — The asymmetric extensions: EGARCH & GJR
Plain GARCH treats up and down shocks symmetrically (`ε²` ignores sign). But markets have the **leverage effect** (Day 143): *down* moves raise volatility *more* than equal up moves (fear > greed). Fixes:
- **GJR-GARCH:** adds a term that activates only for negative shocks — `+ γ·ε²_{t−1}·𝟙[ε_{t−1}<0]` — so bad news boosts variance more.
- **EGARCH:** models *log*-variance (guaranteeing positivity without parameter constraints) and includes an asymmetric term capturing the leverage effect directly.

These asymmetric models fit equity-index volatility far better than symmetric GARCH, because the down-vol-up relationship is real and strong. Choosing among GARCH/EGARCH/GJR is a standard model-selection exercise (AIC/BIC + residual checks, Day 201).

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · forecasting variance and what it feeds
GARCH's payoff is a **forecastable variance term structure**:
- **one-step forecast:** `σ²_{t+1} = ω + αε²_t + βσ²_t` (known at time `t`),
- **multi-step:** variance forecasts **mean-revert toward the long-run level** `ω/(1−α−β)` at rate `(α+β)^h` — so GARCH gives you the *whole expected path of volatility*, decaying from the current level toward the long-run mean (the same OU-style reversion as Days 168/173, now for variance).

This forecastable variance path is *directly usable* across the curriculum:
- **risk management:** time-varying VaR/CVaR (Day 139) — risk limits that tighten in storms,
- **position sizing / vol targeting** (Day 143): scale exposure inversely to forecast vol → stabilize portfolio risk and reduce drawdowns,
- **option pricing:** GARCH option-pricing models and the link to implied-vol term structure (Day 156) — the variance term structure GARCH forecasts should relate to the implied-vol surface,
- **the variance risk premium** (Day 159): compare GARCH-forecast *realized* variance to *implied* variance to trade the premium.

Crucially, GARCH models the **conditional variance given the past** — so you typically fit it on the **residuals** of a mean model (ARIMA, Day 201): mean model for the (tiny) predictable mean, GARCH for the (substantial) predictable variance. The two halves of a return series, each handled by the right tool.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the mean is noise, the variance is signal
GARCH crowns the deepest empirical truth of financial time series: **you can't forecast the direction of returns, but you *can* forecast their magnitude.** This asymmetry organizes the entire field:
- **conditional mean** of returns ≈ unpredictable (efficient markets, Day 150; ARIMA finds nothing, Day 201) → don't bet the house on directional single-series forecasts,
- **conditional variance** of returns ≈ *strongly* predictable (clustering, persistence, leverage) → GARCH captures it, and it's the basis of *risk-based* edges that are far more robust than return-prediction edges.

This is *why* so much of professional quant work is **volatility-centric**: risk management, vol targeting, options/vol trading (Day 159), and risk-parity allocation (coming, Day 211+) all exploit *predictable variance* rather than *unpredictable returns*. The strategies that survive tend to be the ones built on the forecastable thing (risk) rather than the unforecastable thing (direction). It also reframes humility productively: accepting that you *can't* predict direction frees you to excel at what you *can* predict — risk — which turns out to be enormously valuable. The recurring lesson of the whole curriculum — **build on what's actually forecastable, respect what isn't** — finds its sharpest expression here: returns are noise, but their *variance* is signal, and the quant who internalizes that channels effort where it pays. GARCH is the mathematical embodiment of "manage risk, don't predict returns."

**Hard puzzle:** a risk manager uses a *constant* historical volatility (the full-sample standard deviation) to set position limits, arguing it's "more stable and less noisy" than a GARCH estimate that "jumps around." Why is this dangerous, and what does GARCH provide that a constant can't? (The constant-vol approach is dangerous precisely *because* it's stable — it's **stable at the wrong time.** Volatility **clusters** (Day 143): a constant full-sample vol *underestimates* risk during storms (when α, β have pushed variance high) and *overestimates* it during calm. So position limits set on constant vol let the book take **too much risk exactly when markets are most dangerous** (the constant doesn't rise as the storm builds) and **too little when it's calm** (missing opportunity). GARCH provides the **conditional, time-varying variance** — it *rises before and during* turbulent periods because it reacts to recent shocks (α) and persistence (β), so risk limits computed from it **tighten automatically in storms and relax in calm** — exactly when you need them to. The "stability" the risk manager prizes is a *bug*: real risk is time-varying, and a model that's blind to that gives a false sense of security that fails in the crisis it should have caught (the SVB/duration-mismatch and 2008 lessons — risk that doesn't update is no risk control at all). The whole *point* of GARCH is that volatility is forecastable and dynamic; replacing it with a constant throws away the single most useful forecastable quantity in finance. "Less noisy" is not the goal — *correctly time-varying* is.)

**Career connection:** "what's actually predictable in financial markets, and how do you model it?" Strong answer: **not the direction of returns** (near-efficient markets make the conditional mean ~unpredictable — ARIMA finds nothing), **but the variance** — volatility **clusters, persists, and mean-reverts**, and that's modeled with the **GARCH family**: ARCH (variance from past shocks) → GARCH(1,1) (`σ²_t = ω + αε²_{t−1} + βσ²_{t−1}`, persistence α+β ≈ 0.95–0.99) → **EGARCH/GJR** for the leverage effect (down moves raise vol more). The forecastable variance path drives **time-varying VaR, vol targeting, options/vol trading, and risk parity**. Framing the mean-vs-variance asymmetry — *returns are noise, variance is signal* — and naming the GARCH family with the leverage effect is the senior volatility answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Fit GARCH, forecast the variance term structure
```python
import numpy as np
from arch import arch_model                       # pip install arch
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# simulate clustered returns (GARCH DGP) then recover the parameters
T, omega, alpha, beta = 3000, 1e-6, 0.08, 0.90
r = np.zeros(T); s2 = np.full(T, omega/(1-alpha-beta))
for t in range(1,T):
    s2[t] = omega + alpha*r[t-1]**2 + beta*s2[t-1]
    r[t]  = np.sqrt(s2[t])*rng.standard_normal()

res = arch_model(r*100, vol="Garch", p=1, q=1, mean="Zero").fit(disp="off")
a, b = res.params["alpha[1]"], res.params["beta[1]"]
print(f"recovered α={a:.3f}  β={b:.3f}  persistence α+β={a+b:.3f}")

# forecast the variance term structure (annualized vol, next 10 days)
fc = res.forecast(horizon=10)
vol_path = np.sqrt(fc.variance.values[-1]) * np.sqrt(252) / 100
print("forecast annualized vol path:", vol_path.round(3))
print("→ mean-reverts toward the long-run level at rate (α+β)^h")

# asymmetric: fit GJR-GARCH to capture the leverage effect
gjr = arch_model(r*100, vol="Garch", p=1, o=1, q=1, mean="Zero").fit(disp="off")
print(f"GJR leverage term γ = {gjr.params.get('gamma[1]', 0):.3f}  (positive ⇒ down moves raise vol more)")
```
GARCH recovers the clustering parameters (α≈0.08, β≈0.90), forecasts a *mean-reverting volatility path*, and GJR adds the leverage asymmetry — the full variance-modelling toolkit feeding risk, sizing, and vol trading.

**Linux:** `python garch.py | tee garch.log`; fit on real index returns and watch the forecast vol spike after turbulent days and decay through calm ones.

## Quant Thinking Track — Build on the forecastable
The master strategic lesson: **channel your modelling effort toward what's actually predictable.** In markets, that's risk (variance), not direction (mean). Strategies and risk systems built on the forecastable quantity are robust; those built on the unforecastable one are fragile dressed as clever. Identifying *which* aspect of a system is genuinely predictable — and building there — is a discipline that pays across every domain, not just finance.
