## Block 1 — One yes/no question = one bit
You met this in the "20 questions / weighings" puzzles. To pin down one of `N` equally likely possibilities you need about `log₂(N)` yes/no questions — that's **information measured in bits.** 1000 possibilities → ~10 bits.

*Why it matters:* "how much does this observation reduce my uncertainty?" is the literal question behind signals, feature selection, and decision trees.

---

## Block 2 — Entropy (the formula)
For outcomes with probabilities `p₁…pₙ`, **Shannon entropy** is
```
H = − Σ pᵢ log₂ pᵢ        (bits)
```
- A fair coin: `H = −(½log½ + ½log½) = 1` bit (max uncertainty for 2 outcomes).
- A biased coin `p=0.99`: `H ≈ 0.08` bits — almost no uncertainty, so almost no information when it lands.

**Concept check:** when is entropy maximised? (Uniform distribution — all outcomes equally likely.)

---

## Block 3 — Information gain & decision trees
A split that makes outcomes more *predictable* reduces entropy; the reduction is **information gain** — the criterion decision trees use to choose features. A "useless" signal adds ~0 bits; a strong predictor adds many.

**Thinking question:** if a feature perfectly predicts the target, what's the entropy *after* conditioning on it? (Zero.)

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics (Logs everywhere)
Entropy uses `log₂`; the same logarithm shows up in binary search (`log₂ N` steps), tree height, and "questions to identify N items." **Exercise:** entropy of a fair 6-sided die? (`log₂ 6 ≈ 2.585` bits.) **Challenge:** why is `log` the natural unit for "combining independent uncertainties"? (Independent → probabilities multiply → logs add.)

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking (Information = edge)

A trading signal is valuable exactly insofar as it **reduces uncertainty** about future returns. Quant research is, in one sentence, *the search for information that the market price doesn't already reflect.*

- A signal with near-zero information gain about forward returns is noise — don't trade it.
- **Mutual information** generalises correlation: it captures *any* dependence (not just linear) between a feature and the target.

**Hard puzzle:** two analysts each give you a forecast. If they're highly correlated, does the second add much information? (No — redundant signals add little; *independent* signals add the most. This is why diversification of *signals* matters, not just assets.)

**Career connection:** "is this feature actually informative, or am I fitting noise?" is the daily question of quant research — and information theory is its language.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Measure entropy
```python
import numpy as np
def entropy(p):
    p = np.asarray(p); p = p[p > 0]
    return -np.sum(p * np.log2(p))
print(entropy([0.5, 0.5]))    # 1.0  (fair coin)
print(entropy([0.99, 0.01]))  # ~0.08
```
Bonus: simulate a biased coin, estimate its entropy from samples, and watch the estimate converge.

**Linux:** `gzip -l file` — observe how compressible (≈ low-entropy) data shrinks. **Question:** why does random data barely compress? (High entropy ≈ no redundancy.)
