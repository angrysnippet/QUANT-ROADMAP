# Day 158 — 🎲🛤️ Derivatives World · Exotics & Path-Dependence — When the Journey Matters

> Vanilla options care only about `S_T`, the price at expiry. **Exotics** care about the *whole path* — the maximum reached, the average, whether a barrier was touched. Path-dependence breaks the binomial tree's recombination and sends you to **Monte Carlo** (Day 139), closing the loop on your pricing toolkit.

## Block 1 — The exotic zoo
- **Barrier options** — activate (*knock-in*) or extinguish (*knock-out*) if `S` touches a level `H`. Cheaper than vanilla (you give up some scenarios), so popular for cost-efficient hedging. Payoff depends on whether the path *ever* hit `H`.
- **Asian options** — payoff on the **average** price over the life, `max(avg(S) − K, 0)`. Lower vol than spot ⇒ cheaper; used where a single fixing is manipulable (commodities, FX, corporate hedging).
- **Lookback options** — payoff uses the path's **max or min** (e.g. buy at the lowest price seen). Expensive — you're handed hindsight.
- **Digital/binary** — pay a fixed amount if `S_T > K`, else nothing. Payoff ≈ the `N(d₂)` probability itself; tiny gamma except a *spike* at the strike near expiry (a hedging nightmare).

*Why it matters:* exotics are how real hedging needs (averages, knockouts, capped costs) get met — and pricing them is where a quant dev earns their keep.

---

## Block 2 — Why path-dependence breaks trees
A recombining tree works because `S` at a node summarizes everything you need — the past is irrelevant for a vanilla payoff. But a **barrier** needs to know *did the path touch H?*, an **Asian** needs *the running average*, a **lookback** needs *the running max*. These are extra **state variables**: now `up-then-down ≠ down-then-up`, the lattice stops recombining, and the state space explodes. You can augment the tree (track the extra state), but for several path-features the dimensionality kills it. **The natural tool becomes forward simulation — Monte Carlo.**

**Concept check:** why is `up-then-down` different from `down-then-up` for a knock-out at `H` between the two prices? (One path *touches* the barrier and dies; the other never does and survives. The terminal price is identical but the payoff differs — pure path-dependence, fatal to recombination.)

---

## Block 3 — Monte Carlo: simulate the path, average the payoff
Risk-neutral MC (Day 139, 153) generalizes effortlessly to path-dependence:
1. Simulate **many full GBM paths** under the risk-neutral drift `r` (not just terminal points — the *whole trajectory*).
2. For each path, compute the **path-dependent payoff** (check the barrier, take the average/max).
3. Price = `e^{−rT} ×` mean payoff. Error shrinks like `1/√N` (Day 139).

This is the great virtue of MC: **any payoff you can write as code on a path, you can price** — no analytics required. The cost is `1/√N` convergence (slow) and difficulty with early exercise (needs Longstaff–Schwartz regression).

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · variance reduction (make MC usable)
Raw MC for exotics can be noisy; quants accelerate it:
- **Antithetic variates:** for each path with shocks `Z`, also use `−Z`; the average has lower variance (negatively correlated pairs).
- **Control variates:** price a *related* instrument with a known closed form (e.g. a geometric-average Asian, which *has* an analytic price) alongside your target, and correct by the known error — often a 10–100× variance cut.
- **Importance sampling:** for rare events (deep-OTM digitals, far barriers), shift the sampling distribution toward where the payoff is nonzero, then reweight — essential when `P(payoff>0)` is tiny.
- **Quasi-Monte Carlo:** low-discrepancy (Sobol) sequences fill space more evenly → error closer to `1/N` than `1/√N`.

The unifying idea: **same expected value, smaller variance.** Knowing these is what separates "MC that runs overnight" from "MC that prices the book in seconds."

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · match the engine to the payoff
You now hold the full pricing toolkit — choosing among the three engines is core quant-dev judgment:

| Need | Best engine |
|------|-------------|
| Vanilla European, fast/analytic | **Black–Scholes** (closed form) |
| American / early exercise, low-dim | **Binomial tree / finite-difference PDE** |
| Path-dependent / high-dimensional / many assets | **Monte Carlo** (+ variance reduction) |
| American *and* path-dependent | **Longstaff–Schwartz** (MC + regression) |

There is no universal best — each trades off speed, dimensionality, and the ability to handle early exercise vs path-dependence. **Selecting the right numerical method *is* the engineering skill** the README's "Systems Thinking / computational trade-offs" is pointing at.

**Hard puzzle:** price a 1-year option paying the **average** weekly price minus `K` (an Asian). Tree or Monte Carlo — and is it cheaper or pricier than the vanilla call? (Monte Carlo: the running average is a path feature that wrecks tree recombination. It's **cheaper** than the vanilla — averaging *dampens volatility* (the average of correlated weekly prices has lower variance than the terminal price), and option value rises with vol (Day 151), so less effective vol ⇒ lower premium. Bonus: use the *geometric*-average Asian's closed form as a **control variate** to slash MC variance.)

**Career connection:** "how would you price a knock-out barrier option?" Strong answer: Monte-Carlo the risk-neutral paths and check the barrier along each (or a PDE with an absorbing boundary for low dimensions), because the knock-out is **path-dependent** and breaks tree recombination — then mention variance reduction (antithetics/control variates) and the BS analytic barrier formula as a sanity check. Demonstrating you *choose the engine to fit the payoff* is exactly the judgment desks hire for.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Monte-Carlo an Asian and a knock-out barrier
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
S0,K,r,sig,T = 100,100,0.05,0.2,1.0
steps, paths = 252, 200_000
dt = T/steps

# simulate full risk-neutral GBM paths
Z = rng.standard_normal((paths, steps))
logpaths = np.cumsum((r-0.5*sig**2)*dt + sig*np.sqrt(dt)*Z, axis=1)
S = S0*np.exp(logpaths)                         # shape (paths, steps)

# Asian call: payoff on the average price
asian = np.exp(-r*T)*np.maximum(S.mean(axis=1)-K, 0).mean()

# Up-and-out barrier call: dies if path ever touches H=130
H = 130
alive = S.max(axis=1) < H
barrier = np.exp(-r*T)*np.where(alive, np.maximum(S[:,-1]-K,0), 0.0).mean()

# vanilla for comparison
vanilla = np.exp(-r*T)*np.maximum(S[:,-1]-K, 0).mean()
print(f"vanilla call ≈ {vanilla:.3f}")
print(f"Asian   call ≈ {asian:.3f}   (cheaper: averaging cuts vol)")
print(f"up&out  call ≈ {barrier:.3f} (cheaper: some paths knocked out)")
```
Both exotics price *below* the vanilla, for the structural reasons above — and the code is just "simulate paths, write the payoff, average," the universal MC recipe.

**Linux:** `python exotics.py | tee exotics.log`; add antithetic variates (`np.vstack([Z,-Z])`) and watch the standard error of the price roughly halve for the same path count.

## Quant Thinking Track — State is the whole game
Path-dependence is really a lesson about **state**: a vanilla option's state is just `S`; an exotic's state includes the running max/average/touch-flag. Identifying the *minimal sufficient state* decides whether a tree/PDE suffices or you need simulation — the exact state-space thinking from Days 109–110, now pricing derivatives. **Get the state right and the method chooses itself.**
