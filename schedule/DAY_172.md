## Block 1 — Cointegration, defined
Two (or more) series that are each **non-stationary** (`I(1)`, random walks — Day 142) are **cointegrated** if some **linear combination** of them *is* **stationary** (`I(0)`). Formally: `A` and `B` are I(1), but there exists `β` such that
```
ε_t = A_t − β·B_t   is stationary (mean-reverting).
```
`β` is the **cointegrating coefficient** (the hedge ratio); `ε_t` is the **spread**. Intuitively: the two prices wander, but they're **tethered** — they can't drift arbitrarily far apart because an economic force (shared fundamentals, arbitrage) pulls the spread back. The tether *is* your trade.

*Why it matters:* cointegration is the precise, falsifiable version of "these two move together in a way I can trade." No cointegration → no reliable reversion → no edge.

---

## Block 2 — The Engle–Granger two-step test
The classic, simple procedure (Engle & Granger won the Nobel for this):
1. **Regress** `A` on `B` (OLS): `A_t = α + β·B_t + ε_t`. The slope `β̂` is your hedge ratio; the residuals `ε̂_t` are the candidate spread.
2. **ADF-test the residuals** `ε̂_t` (Day 142): if the test **rejects the unit root** (small p-value), the spread is stationary → `A` and `B` are **cointegrated** → tradeable.

(One technical note: testing residuals needs special "Engle–Granger" critical values, slightly stricter than the standard ADF table, because `β` was *estimated*.) If the residual is *non*-stationary, the "pair" is spurious — exactly the Granger–Newbold trap (Day 142) — and you walk away.

**Concept check:** you regress two stocks, get a beautiful R² = 0.97, but the ADF on the residual *fails* to reject the unit root. Trade it? (No. High R² just means they trend together (possibly spuriously); the *failed* ADF says the spread random-walks and won't revert. **The residual test, not the R², decides.**)

## Block 3 — Engle–Granger vs Johansen
- **Engle–Granger** (above): simple, intuitive, great for **two** assets. Weakness: it's *asymmetric* — regressing A-on-B vs B-on-A gives slightly different β; and it finds at most one relationship.
- **Johansen test:** a multivariate, symmetric method that finds **all** cointegrating relationships among **several** assets at once (eigenvalue-based). Essential for **baskets** — e.g. trading one stock against a *combination* of several others, or a whole sector (Day 176). It also gives a cleaner, estimation-consistent hedge vector.

Start with Engle–Granger to build intuition; reach for Johansen when you trade more than two legs.

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the Error-Correction Model (ECM)
Cointegration has a profound dual (the **Granger Representation Theorem**): if two series are cointegrated, their dynamics *must* be describable by an **error-correction model**:
```
ΔA_t = γ·(ε_{t−1}) + (short-run terms) + noise,   with γ < 0
```
In words: **the change in `A` is pulled back in proportion to how far the spread `ε` was from zero last period.** The `γ` (the *speed of adjustment*) is literally the mean-reversion force — a negative `γ` drags the system back toward equilibrium whenever the spread deviates. This is the rigorous statement of "the rubber band pulls back," and `γ` connects directly to the **half-life** of reversion (Day 173). The ECM is *why* cointegration implies tradeable reversion — not just a correlation, but a restoring force with a measurable strength.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the test is your defense against self-deception
Cointegration testing is, at heart, **discipline against overfitting** (Day 124) — the recurring theme of the whole curriculum. With thousands of possible pairs, *some will look cointegrated by pure chance* (multiple testing, Day 124). So the professional process is:
- demand cointegration **in-sample**, then **confirm it holds out-of-sample** (the relationship persists),
- require an **economic reason** for the tether (same sector, same supply chain, a hard arbitrage link like an ETF and its basket) — a *story*, not just a p-value,
- correct for the **number of pairs tested** (deflated significance),
- monitor for **breakdown** — cointegration is not permanent; mergers, regime shifts, and business changes sever tethers (Day 178).

A p-value alone is not a pair. **Cointegration + economic rationale + out-of-sample persistence** is. This is Day 149's "skill vs luck" applied to relationships.

**Hard puzzle:** you scan 2,000 stock pairs and find 60 that pass the cointegration test at the 5% level. How many should you *expect* by pure chance, and what do you do? (At a 5% false-positive rate under the null, ≈ `0.05 × 2000 = 100` pairs would pass *even if none were truly cointegrated* — so finding only 60 is *worse than random noise*! Multiple testing (Day 124) means raw p-values are meaningless at this scale. Fixes: tighten the threshold (Bonferroni/FDR), require an *economic* link, demand out-of-sample persistence, and treat the in-sample test as a *filter*, not proof. This is the quant-quake-style lesson that data-mined pairs are mostly mirages.)

**Career connection:** "how do you find and validate a pairs trade?" Strong answer: test for **cointegration** (Engle–Granger: regress for the hedge ratio, ADF the residual; or Johansen for baskets), but guard hard against **multiple testing** — with thousands of candidate pairs, some pass by luck, so require an economic rationale and out-of-sample persistence, and monitor for breakdown. Mentioning the ECM/speed-of-adjustment and the multiple-testing correction signals genuine depth.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Engle–Granger test + estimated hedge ratio
```python
import numpy as np, pandas as pd
import statsmodels.api as sm
from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller, coint
rng = np.random.default_rng(1)

T = 1500
common = np.cumsum(rng.standard_normal(T))               # shared I(1) trend
A = 20 + 1.5*common + np.cumsum(rng.standard_normal(T)*0.1) + rng.standard_normal(T)*0.3
B = 10 + 1.0*common + np.cumsum(rng.standard_normal(T)*0.1)               # cointegrated w/ A
C = 30 + np.cumsum(rng.standard_normal(T))               # independent random walk (spurious)

# Engle–Granger step 1: hedge ratio by OLS
beta = sm.OLS(A, sm.add_constant(B)).fit().params[1]
resid = A - beta*B
print(f"hedge ratio β = {beta:.3f}")
print(f"ADF on A~B residual p-value: {adfuller(resid)[1]:.4f}  (small ⇒ cointegrated)")

# built-in cointegration test for both candidates
print(f"coint(A,B) p-value = {coint(A,B)[1]:.4f}  <- the real pair")
print(f"coint(A,C) p-value = {coint(A,C)[1]:.4f}  <- spurious, should NOT reject")
```
The true pair (A,B) rejects the unit root in the residual (cointegrated); the spurious (A,C) does not — the test cleanly separating a real tether from a mirage.

**Linux:** `python coint.py | tee coint.log`; wrap it in a loop over many candidate pairs and *count* how many pass — then confront the multiple-testing problem from Block 5 yourself.

## Quant Thinking Track — Falsifiability as edge-protection
Cointegration testing embodies the scientific method inside trading: state a hypothesis (these revert), define a falsification test (ADF on the residual), and *believe it only if it survives* — in-sample, out-of-sample, and with a mechanism. The quants who last are the ones whose default attitude toward their own signals is **skeptical until proven**, because the market ruthlessly charges for self-deception.
