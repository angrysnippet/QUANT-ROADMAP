## Block 1 — Population vs sample
You rarely see the **population** (all possible outcomes); you see a **sample**. A statistic (e.g. sample mean `x̄`) is an **estimator** of a population parameter (`μ`). Estimators are themselves random — they have a distribution (the **sampling distribution**).

*Why it matters:* every backtest number is a *sample statistic.* Treating it as the truth is the original sin of quant research.

---

## Block 2 — Good estimators: bias & variance
- **Unbiased:** `E[estimator] = true parameter` (e.g. `x̄` is unbiased for `μ`).
- **Sample variance** uses `n−1` (Bessel's correction) to stay unbiased:
```
s² = (1/(n−1)) Σ (xᵢ − x̄)²
```
- **Standard error** of the mean: `SE = s/√n` (precision of your estimate).

**Concept check:** why divide by `n−1`, not `n`? (Using `x̄` instead of the true `μ` "uses up" one degree of freedom, biasing the `n` version downward.)

---

## Block 3 — Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)
Pick the parameter that makes the observed data **most probable**:
```
θ̂ = argmax_θ  ∏ P(xᵢ | θ)     (usually maximise the log-likelihood)
```
For a Normal, MLE gives `μ̂ = x̄` and `σ̂² = (1/n)Σ(xᵢ−x̄)²`. MLE underpins regression, GARCH, and most model fitting you'll do.

---

## Block 4 — Confidence intervals
A 95% CI for a mean (large `n`): `x̄ ± 1.96·SE`. It means: *if we repeated the sampling many times, ~95% of such intervals would contain the true μ.* (It is **not** "95% probability μ is in this interval" — that's the Bayesian credible interval.)

**Exercise:** 100 daily returns, mean 0.05%, sd 1%. SE = `1%/√100 = 0.1%`. 95% CI for the mean ≈ `0.05% ± 0.196%` → **includes 0.** Conclusion: one quarter of data can't distinguish this edge from zero. **Challenge:** how many days to make the CI exclude 0? (Need `SE` small enough → large `n` → the √n grind.)

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking (Every number has error bars)

- A backtest mean return is an **estimate**; report it with a standard error, not as a fact.
- **Estimating covariance matrices** (needed for portfolios) is notoriously noisy: with 500 assets you have ~125k parameters but limited history → estimates are unstable → **shrinkage** (Day 122, Ledoit–Wolf) is standard practice.
- **Survivorship & selection bias:** if your sample excludes dead funds/delisted stocks, your estimator is biased upward — a classic way backtests lie.

**Hard puzzle:** you compute a strategy's Sharpe = 1.5 over 1 year (252 days). The standard error of a Sharpe estimate is ≈ `√((1 + 0.5·Sharpe²)/n)` per year of daily data ≈ `√((1+1.125)/252) ≈ 0.09`... (annualisation aside) — is 1.5 ± noise convincing yet? Roughly, you need several years before a Sharpe estimate is tight. (Reinforces Day 121.)

**Career connection:** "what's the uncertainty on that estimate?" is the question that separates a researcher from someone who reports point numbers. Always attach error bars.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Sampling distribution & CI
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
true_mu, true_sd, n = 0.05, 1.0, 100
# Sampling distribution of the mean via repeated samples
xbar = rng.normal(true_mu, true_sd, size=(20000, n)).mean(axis=1)
print(round(xbar.mean(),3), round(xbar.std(),3))   # ≈ true_mu, ≈ sd/√n = 0.1
# One sample's 95% CI
s = rng.normal(true_mu, true_sd, n); se = s.std(ddof=1)/np.sqrt(n)
print(s.mean()-1.96*se, s.mean()+1.96*se)
```

**Linux:** `sort -n | awk` to get quick quantiles of a returns file — then prefer numpy.

## Quant Thinking Track — Data → Truth Is Always Uncertain
Statistics is the discipline of *honest uncertainty.* The professional reflex is never "the answer is X" but "the estimate is X ± SE, given these assumptions, and here's what could bias it."
