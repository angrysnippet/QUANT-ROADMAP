## Block 1 — The two atoms: call and put
- **Call option:** the right (not obligation) to **buy** the underlying at strike `K` on/before expiry `T`.
- **Put option:** the right to **sell** at `K`.
- **European** = exercise only at `T`; **American** = any time up to `T`.

Payoff at expiry (this is the whole foundation):
```
Call payoff = max(S_T − K, 0)
Put  payoff = max(K − S_T, 0)
```
You pay a **premium** up front for this right. The buyer's loss is capped at the premium; the upside is large (call) or large-down-to-zero (put). That capped-loss / open-upside shape is **optionality**.

*Why it matters:* every structured product, every hedge, every convertible, and the entire \$600T+ derivatives market is built from `max(·,0)` payoffs. Master this kink and you can decompose almost anything.

---

## Block 2 — Payoff diagrams (draw these in your sleep)
Plot payoff (y) vs `S_T` (x):
- **Long call:** flat at `−premium` until `K`, then slopes up 45°. Break-even `= K + premium`.
- **Long put:** slopes down-to-up; high on the left, flat at `−premium` right of `K`. Break-even `= K − premium`.
- **Short call:** mirror of long call — flat profit (premium) then *unlimited loss* up.
- **Short put:** collect premium, lose as `S_T` falls toward 0 ("selling insurance").

**Concept check:** you sell a call at `K=100` for \$3. `S_T=110`. Your P&L? (`+3 − (110−100) = −7`. The buyer's gain is your loss — options are zero-sum between the two parties.)

## Block 3 — Intrinsic value vs time value
At any time before expiry, an option premium splits into:
```
Premium = Intrinsic value + Time value
```
- **Intrinsic** = `max(S−K,0)` for a call — what you'd get exercising *now*. **ITM** (in-the-money) if positive, **OTM** if zero, **ATM** if `S≈K`.
- **Time value** = the rest — the value of *uncertainty and time remaining*. Always ≥ 0 before expiry; **decays to zero at `T`** (theta, Day 154). More time and more volatility → more time value, because more can happen.

**Key intuition:** an option is a bet *on volatility and time*, not just direction. Two stocks at the same price can have very differently-priced options if their vols differ.

---

## Block 4 — Why options exist (three real users)
1. **Hedgers** — an airline buys oil calls to cap fuel cost; a fund buys puts as portfolio insurance. They pay premium to *transfer tail risk*.
2. **Speculators** — leverage and defined risk: control 100 shares of exposure for a small premium, lose at most that premium.
3. **Market makers / vol traders** — provide liquidity, hedge dynamically (Day 155), and trade the *spread between implied and realized vol* (Day 143's variance risk premium).

Options let you express views impossible with the stock alone: "I think it'll move *a lot, direction unknown*" (long straddle), or "I think it'll *stay put*" (short straddle). **Stocks trade direction; options trade direction, magnitude, *and* time.**

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · convexity is the hidden engine
The `max(·,0)` kink makes an option's value a **convex** function of `S`. Convexity means: you gain more from a big up-move than you lose from an equal down-move (for a long option). This is **Jensen's inequality** (Day 119) made tradeable — `E[max(S−K,0)] > max(E[S]−K,0)` — which is *exactly why* volatility has value and why the price isn't just the intrinsic value of the expected price. Hold this thought: **convexity = the mathematical reason options cost more than their intrinsic value, and the reason vol is an asset.**

**Hard puzzle:** two stocks both trade at \$100 with the same expected price in a year. Stock A is calm (σ=10%), stock B is wild (σ=60%). Which has the more valuable \$100 call, and why — even though the *expected stock price* is identical? (B's, by far. The call payoff is convex: it caps the downside at 0 but keeps all the upside, so higher σ fattens the *useful* right tail without adding left-tail cost. Expected price is irrelevant; *dispersion* is what a call sells. This is the seed of Black–Scholes — option value rises with σ.)

**Career connection:** "what's the difference between buying a stock and buying a call?" Strong answer: the stock is linear exposure; the call is *convex, time-decaying* exposure — limited downside, leveraged upside, and a bet on volatility/time, not just price. Framing options as *convexity + theta* (not "leverage") signals you actually understand derivatives.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Payoffs and the convexity of value
```python
import numpy as np
S = np.linspace(50, 150, 201)
K, prem_c, prem_p = 100, 6.0, 5.0

long_call  = np.maximum(S-K, 0) - prem_c
long_put   = np.maximum(K-S, 0) - prem_p
short_call = -(np.maximum(S-K, 0)) + prem_c

for name, pay in [("long call", long_call), ("long put", long_put), ("short call", short_call)]:
    be = S[np.argmin(np.abs(pay))]
    print(f"{name:11s} max loss={pay.min():6.1f}  P&L@S=120: {pay[S==120][0]:6.1f}  ~break-even≈{be:.0f}")

# convexity: expected call payoff under low vs high vol (same mean price)
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
for sigma in (0.10, 0.60):
    ST = 100*np.exp(-0.5*sigma**2 + sigma*rng.standard_normal(200_000))  # E[ST]=100
    print(f"sigma={sigma:.2f}  E[ST]={ST.mean():.1f}  E[max(ST-100,0)]={np.maximum(ST-100,0).mean():.2f}")
```
The high-vol expected call payoff dwarfs the low-vol one despite identical `E[ST]` — convexity/Jensen, the engine of option value, shown by simulation.

## Quant Thinking Track — Abstraction
An option abstracts a messy contract into one object: a **convex, time-decaying claim on a distribution**. Once you see payoffs as functions of `S_T` and value as an *expectation over the terminal distribution*, pricing becomes "integrate the payoff against the (risk-neutral) density" — the entire Black–Scholes program in one sentence (Day 153).
