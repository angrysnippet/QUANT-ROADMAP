## Block 1 — The lemons problem comes to markets
Market makers face a version of Akerlof's "market for lemons": **they can't tell who they're trading with.**
- **Informed traders** trade *because they know* the price should move (an upcoming earnings beat, a large order to come). They **buy from you right before it rises, sell to you right before it falls** — you're *always on the wrong side* of them.
- **Noise/liquidity traders** trade for reasons unrelated to future price (rebalancing, cash needs, hedging). Trading with them is *fair* — no informational disadvantage.

The MM can't distinguish the two, so **every trade carries the risk it's with an informed counterparty.** This is **adverse selection**, and it's why a spread is needed *even with zero inventory and processing cost*: the maker must charge everyone to recoup the losses to the informed few.

*Why it matters:* adverse selection is the *informational* foundation of the spread — and it links microstructure to information theory (Day 117) and efficient markets (Day 150).

---

## Block 2 — Glosten–Milgrom: the spread as protection
The Glosten–Milgrom model (1985) gives the cleanest result: even a **risk-neutral, competitive, zero-cost** market maker must quote a spread purely to break even against informed flow. The logic:
- a **buy** order is *more likely* to come from an informed trader who knows the price is going up → so after a buy, the MM **revises their fair value upward**,
- therefore the MM sets the **ask above the expected value** (to not lose to informed buyers) and the **bid below** (to not lose to informed sellers),
- the spread = `2 × (probability informed) × (size of the information)`.

So the spread is **the maker's insurance premium against being picked off.** Wider when more traders are informed, or when the potential information is bigger (e.g. around earnings). Each trade also *updates the price* (Bayesian, Day 122) — the book **learns** from order flow.

---

## Block 3 — Order flow carries information → price discovery
The profound consequence: **trades move prices because trades reveal information.** When the MM sees a buy, they rationally infer "maybe someone knows something good" and mark up. Aggregated, this is **price discovery** — the market learning the true value through the *direction and intensity of order flow*. Key ideas:
- **order flow imbalance** (more buys than sells) predicts price moves — the basis of many microstructure signals (Day 187),
- prices become **a Bayesian estimate** that updates with every trade,
- this is the *mechanism* behind the efficient-market abstraction (Day 150): information gets into prices precisely *through informed trading against market makers.* Grossman–Stiglitz (Day 150) made flesh — the informed are paid (they profit off the MM), and that payment is what pulls prices toward fair value.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · Kyle's lambda — the price of information
Kyle's model (1985) is the other pillar and gives microstructure's most famous number, **lambda (λ)** — the **price impact per unit of order flow**:
```
ΔPrice = λ × (net order flow)
```
λ is *linear* market impact, and Kyle derives:
```
λ = σ_v / σ_u  ·  (a constant)     ≈  √(information variance / noise-trading variance)
```
- **more informed (higher value uncertainty `σ_v`)** → higher λ → trades move price more (the market is *jumpy* and defensive),
- **more noise trading (`σ_u`)** → lower λ → the informed can *hide* in the noise, so each trade reveals less → smaller impact (this is why the informed *want* liquid, noisy markets — camouflage),
- **λ is illiquidity** — literally the slope of price vs order flow; `1/λ` is **market depth.**

Kyle's λ is the theoretical sibling of the empirical **square-root impact law** (Day 179): both say *trading moves price*, λ giving the linear, information-driven version. It connects information theory (Day 117), Bayesian updating (Day 122), and execution cost (Day 179) into one coefficient — *the price the market charges for revealing information through your trades.*

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · the spread is an information phenomenon
The grand synthesis of microstructure: **the bid–ask spread and price impact are not frictions — they are the market's response to *information asymmetry*.** This reframes everything:
- **Why the spread exists:** to protect liquidity providers from informed traders (Glosten–Milgrom) — *not just* inventory/processing cost (Day 182). Even perfect, costless, risk-neutral MMs quote a spread.
- **Why trades move prices:** because order flow *is information* (Kyle's λ) — the market rationally updates on what your trade reveals.
- **Why you have impact:** because the market *can't tell* if you're informed, so it must assume you might be and move against you — your impact is the market pricing the *possibility* that you know something.
- **Why the informed want liquidity/noise:** camouflage — they hide their information-driven trades among noise traders to minimize λ and maximize profit.

This is why **execution is adversarial**: when you trade, the market treats you as *potentially informed* and charges you λ for it. Minimizing impact (Day 185) is largely about **disguising your order flow** so the market underestimates how informed/large you are. The entire cost of trading, at its root, is the **price of your own potential information leaking into the market.** Information isn't just *in* prices (Day 150) — the *flow* of information *through trades* is what spreads, impact, and price discovery all measure.

**Hard puzzle:** two stocks have identical volatility and identical average daily volume. Stock A's price barely moves when large orders hit; Stock B's jumps sharply. What does Kyle's model say is different, and which stock would an informed trader prefer to trade? (By Kyle, impact `λ ≈ σ_v/σ_u`. Equal volatility/volume but different λ means the **mix of informed vs noise trading differs**: B has *high λ* → either more information-driven trading (high `σ_v`) or *less* noise to hide in (low `σ_u`), so the market is defensive and every trade reveals a lot. A has *low λ* → lots of noise trading to camouflage information, so trades barely move price. An **informed trader strongly prefers A** — the noise provides *camouflage*, letting them accumulate a position before the market figures it out, minimizing the impact cost of their own information. Counterintuitively, the informed seek out the *liquid, noisy* market precisely *because* it hides them — the deepest insight of Kyle's model. "Liquidity" is, in part, *cover for the informed.*)

**Career connection:** "why does the bid–ask spread exist — isn't it just a transaction fee?" Strong answer: no — beyond processing and inventory costs, the spread exists because of **adverse selection**: market makers can't tell informed from noise traders, so they must quote a spread to recoup losses to the informed (Glosten–Milgrom), and trades move prices because **order flow conveys information** (Kyle's λ = impact per unit flow = illiquidity). The spread is the *price of information asymmetry*, and that's why even a costless, risk-neutral MM still needs one. Connecting the spread to information (not just friction) signals real microstructure depth.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Glosten–Milgrom price updating & Kyle's λ
```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# Glosten–Milgrom: MM Bayesian-updates value from order direction
# true value is High or Low; informed know it, noise traders are random
def simulate_gm(alpha=0.3, V_high=101, V_low=99, n=2000, true="high"):
    p_high=0.5; mid=[]
    for _ in range(n):
        # is this trader informed?
        if rng.random()<alpha:                         # informed: trades toward truth
            buy = (true=="high")
        else:                                          # noise: random
            buy = rng.random()<0.5
        # Bayesian update of P(High) given the observed buy/sell
        if buy: p_high = p_high*( (alpha*(true=="high")+ (1-alpha)*0.5) ) / \
                          ( p_high*(alpha+ (1-alpha)*0.5) + (1-p_high)*((1-alpha)*0.5) )
        p_high=min(max(p_high,1e-6),1-1e-6)
        mid.append(p_high*V_high+(1-p_high)*V_low)
    return mid
mid = simulate_gm(true="high")
print(f"price discovery: start {mid[0]:.3f} -> end {mid[-1]:.3f}  (learns toward 101)")

# Kyle's lambda: price impact is linear in net order flow
sigma_v, sigma_u = 1.0, 2.0                            # info vs noise std
lam = 0.5*sigma_v/sigma_u                              # Kyle's λ (schematic)
flow = np.array([-200, -50, 100, 500])
print("net flow:", flow, " price impact = λ·flow:", (lam*flow).round(3))
print(f"λ (impact per unit flow) = {lam:.3f};  depth 1/λ = {1/lam:.1f}")
```
The Glosten–Milgrom mid **drifts toward the true value** as the MM learns from order direction (price discovery via Bayesian updating); Kyle's λ converts net flow into impact and shows depth `= 1/λ`.
