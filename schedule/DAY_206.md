## Block 1 — From pairs to systems
Engle–Granger (Day 172) finds *one* cointegrating relationship between *two* series. Real relative value is richer:
- a **basket**: one stock vs a weighted combination of several peers,
- the **yield curve**: many maturities tethered by no-arbitrage (Day 164),
- **currency triangles**, **commodity spreads** (crack/crush), **ETF vs constituents**,
- **multiple cointegrating relationships** among the same set of assets.

With `n` non-stationary series, there can be up to `n−1` independent **cointegrating relationships** — each a stationary, mean-reverting combination you could trade. You need a method that finds *all* of them simultaneously and symmetrically. That's **Johansen**.

*Why it matters:* multi-asset relative value (basket arb, curve trades, index arb) is a major class of strategies, and it lives on multivariate cointegration.

---

## Block 2 — The Johansen procedure
The **Johansen test** is the multivariate, symmetric cointegration method (vs Engle–Granger's two-step asymmetry, Day 172). It:
- works within a **VAR/VECM** framework (Day 205) and uses **eigenvalue decomposition** to find the cointegrating relationships,
- determines the **cointegrating rank `r`** = the *number* of independent stationary combinations among the `n` series (via the **trace** and **maximum-eigenvalue** test statistics),
- returns the **cointegrating vectors** themselves — the weights `β` defining each stationary combination (the hedge ratios for a basket),
- is **symmetric** (no arbitrary choice of which variable is "dependent") and estimation-consistent, unlike Engle–Granger.

So Johansen answers both "*how many* tradeable equilibrium relationships exist among these assets?" and "*what are they* (the weights)?" — in one procedure.

**Concept check:** you run Johansen on 4 related stocks and find rank `r = 2`. What does that mean for trading? (There are **2 independent stationary (mean-reverting) combinations** among the 4 stocks — so 2 distinct relative-value spreads to trade, each with its own cointegrating-vector weights. The 4 prices are non-stationary, but they're tethered by 2 equilibrium relationships, leaving 2 independent "common trends" driving them.)

---

## Block 3 — The Vector Error Correction Model (VECM)
A **VECM** is the natural model for cointegrated systems — it's a VAR (Day 205) on the *differenced* (stationary) series, *plus* an **error-correction term** that pulls the system back toward equilibrium:
```
Δx_t = α·(β'·x_{t−1})  +  (lagged Δ terms)  +  ε_t
```
- `β'·x_{t−1}` = the **cointegrating combination** = how far the system is from equilibrium last period (the "spread", the disequilibrium),
- `α` = the **speed-of-adjustment** vector = how strongly *each* series corrects back toward equilibrium (the multivariate version of the ECM `γ` from Day 172).

So a VECM says: *each asset's change is driven partly by short-run dynamics and partly by a pull back toward the equilibrium whenever the spread deviates.* This is the multivariate **error-correction** mechanism (Granger Representation Theorem, Day 172, generalized) — and the `α` vector tells you *which* assets do the correcting (some adjust, some are "weakly exogenous" and don't). It directly generates the trading signal: when `β'·x` deviates, the system will correct → trade the basket back toward equilibrium.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the rank, common trends, and basket construction
The mathematics of cointegration rank is beautiful and practical:
- with `n` series and cointegrating rank `r`, there are `r` **stationary (mean-reverting) relationships** and `n − r` **common stochastic trends** (the non-stationary "factors" the assets share). E.g., a yield curve with many maturities has high cointegration rank — the maturities are tethered, driven by a few common trends (level/slope/curvature, Day 166 — the PCA connection!),
- the **cointegrating vectors `β`** are the basket weights that produce stationary spreads — *these are your tradeable portfolios*,
- the **speed-of-adjustment `α`** governs the mean-reversion dynamics → feeds the half-life and OU modelling (Day 173) of each spread.

Practical cautions (the recurring discipline):
- **rank selection is delicate** — the trace/max-eigenvalue tests are sensitive to sample size, lag length, and deterministic-term assumptions; over-stating rank invents spurious "spreads" that aren't really stationary (overfitting, Day 191),
- **cointegrating relationships are not stable forever** — they break (mergers, regime shifts, Day 204) → re-test on rolling windows (the stat-arb breakdown risk, Day 174),
- **in-sample vs out-of-sample** — a relationship that's cointegrated in-sample must *persist* out-of-sample to be tradeable (Days 172, 199),
- **economic rationale required** — a hard arbitrage link (ETF-vs-basket, curve no-arbitrage) is far more trustworthy than a data-mined statistical one (Day 172).

The deep connection: **cointegration rank and common trends are the non-stationary analogue of PCA/factor structure** (Days 166, 209). A cointegrated system = "a few common stochastic trends drive many series, leaving stationary residual spreads to trade" — which is *exactly* the factor-model worldview (Day 148) applied to *levels* rather than returns. The `n − r` common trends are the non-stationary "factors"; the `r` cointegrating residuals are the tradeable mean-reverting alphas.

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · structure in levels, not just returns
Cointegration systems crystallize a powerful and underappreciated idea: **there is exploitable structure in the *levels* of non-stationary series, not just in their returns.** Most of finance models *returns* (stationary, Day 142) and treats *price levels* as unpredictable random walks. Cointegration says: while *individual* levels random-walk, *combinations* of levels can be stationary and mean-reverting — so there's predictable structure hiding in the *level relationships* that a returns-only view completely misses. This reframes relative value:
- the **common trends** (`n − r` of them) are the non-stationary, unpredictable part — the shared random walks you *hedge out* (analogous to factor exposure, Day 176),
- the **cointegrating residuals** (`r` of them) are the stationary, mean-reverting part — the *tradeable alpha* (analogous to the idiosyncratic residual you trade, Day 176),
- so **cointegration is factor-neutralization for non-stationary levels**: strip out the common trends (the shared random walks), trade the stationary residual.

This unifies a huge swath of the curriculum into one picture: **pairs trading (Day 171), basket/index arbitrage, yield-curve trades (Day 166), and cross-sectional stat arb (Day 176) are all the same idea** — *find a combination that removes the unpredictable common component and leaves a stationary, mean-reverting residual to harvest.* Whether the common component is "the market factor" (returns, Day 148), "the common stochastic trend" (levels, today), or "level/slope/curvature" (curves, Day 166), the move is identical: **decompose into (unpredictable shared part) + (tradeable residual), hedge the first, trade the second.** Seeing cointegration as the *level-space version* of factor decomposition is the kind of cross-domain unification that marks deep understanding — and it tells you *where to look* for relative-value alpha: not in returns alone, but in the equilibrium relationships among non-stationary levels. The mature quant exploits structure wherever it lives — and a great deal of it lives in the cointegrating relationships among prices that everyone else dismisses as "just random walks."

**Hard puzzle:** you have 10 government bonds across maturities (a yield curve). A Johansen test reports a cointegration rank of around 7–8. Interpret this richly, and explain what it implies for trading and risk. (A rank of ~7–8 among 10 series means there are **7–8 independent stationary (mean-reverting) relationships** and therefore only **2–3 common stochastic trends** (`n − r ≈ 2–3`) driving the entire curve. This is the **cointegration-rank expression of the PCA result from Day 166**: the yield curve is driven by ~3 factors (**level, slope, curvature**), so the 10 maturities are tethered by ~7 equilibrium relationships and share only ~3 common trends — *the two methods agree because they're describing the same structure from different angles* (cointegration in levels ↔ PCA on changes). **Trading implications:** (1) there are **many tradeable mean-reverting spreads** — any maturity (or combination) that deviates from its cointegrating relationship with the others should revert → **relative-value curve trades** (butterflies, condors, maturity spreads), each with Johansen-supplied weights `β` and OU-style half-lives (Day 173). (2) But you must **hedge out the 2–3 common trends** — a naive long-one-bond position is exposed to the unpredictable common factors (level shifts, Day 162 duration risk); the *alpha* is in the **cointegrating residual**, so you trade *combinations* that are factor-neutral (the level-space version of Day 176 neutralization). **Risk implications:** (1) the curve's risk is genuinely **low-dimensional** (~3 factors) — so hedge with key-rate durations / the 3 PCA factors (Day 166), consistent with the rank result; (2) **the cointegrating relationships can break** in stress (a maturity becomes special, a regime shifts — Day 204), so the "stationary" spreads can blow out → size for that, re-test rank on rolling windows, and never assume the equilibrium is permanent (Days 172/174); (3) the high rank means *most* of the curve's movement is *predictable mean-reversion around equilibrium*, with only ~3 dimensions of *unpredictable* common-trend risk — a quantitative statement of *why* curve relative-value is a rich, well-defined strategy space. The deep takeaway: **cointegration rank, PCA factor count, and the economic no-arbitrage structure of the curve all tell the same story** — the curve is a few common trends plus many tradeable stationary spreads — and recognizing that unification is what lets you trade and hedge it coherently.)

**Career connection:** "how would you find relative-value trades among many related assets, like a yield curve or a basket?" Strong answer: **multivariate cointegration via the Johansen procedure** — it finds the **cointegrating rank** (*how many* independent stationary, mean-reverting combinations exist) and the **cointegrating vectors** (the *basket weights* for each spread), symmetrically across all assets — then model the dynamics with a **VECM** whose error-correction term `α·(β'x)` gives the mean-reversion signal and speed of adjustment. Crucially, this is **factor-neutralization in level-space**: the `n − r` common stochastic trends are the unpredictable shared random walks to hedge out, and the `r` cointegrating residuals are the tradeable mean-reverting alpha — the same decompose-and-trade-the-residual logic as cross-sectional stat arb, and (for a curve) consistent with the ~3-factor PCA structure. I'd validate rank carefully (it overfits), require an economic tether, and re-test for breakdown out-of-sample. Naming Johansen/VECM, the rank-vs-common-trends interpretation, and the unification with factor models/PCA is a standout relative-value answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Johansen cointegration & a VECM
```python
import numpy as np, pandas as pd
from statsmodels.tsa.vector_ar.vecm import coint_johansen, VECM
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# simulate 3 cointegrated series: 2 common trends + 1 cointegrating relationship
T=1500
trend1 = np.cumsum(rng.standard_normal(T)*0.1)
trend2 = np.cumsum(rng.standard_normal(T)*0.1)
A = trend1 + rng.standard_normal(T)*0.3
B = trend2 + rng.standard_normal(T)*0.3
C = 0.6*trend1 + 0.5*trend2 + rng.standard_normal(T)*0.3      # tethered to A,B
df = pd.DataFrame({"A":A,"B":B,"C":C})

# Johansen test: how many cointegrating relationships (rank)?
joh = coint_johansen(df, det_order=0, k_ar_diff=1)
print("trace statistics:", joh.lr1.round(2))
print("95% crit values :", joh.cvt[:,1].round(2))
rank = int(np.sum(joh.lr1 > joh.cvt[:,1]))
print(f"=> cointegration rank r = {rank}  (≈1 stationary relationship, 2 common trends)")

# fit a VECM and read the error-correction (speed of adjustment) terms
vecm = VECM(df, k_ar_diff=1, coint_rank=max(rank,1)).fit()
print(f"cointegrating vector β (the basket weights): {vecm.beta.flatten().round(2)}")
print(f"speed-of-adjustment α (who corrects): {vecm.alpha.flatten().round(3)}")
```
Johansen recovers the cointegration rank (the number of tradeable spreads), and the VECM gives the basket weights `β` and the speed-of-adjustment `α` — the complete multivariate relative-value toolkit.

**Linux:** `python johansen.py | tee joh.log`; run it on a real yield curve and watch the rank reflect the ~3-factor (level/slope/curvature) structure of Day 166.

Recognizing one principle across many representations is the unifying habit that turns a pile of techniques into a single, generative understanding.
