## Block 1 — A neuron
A neuron computes a weighted sum plus bias, then a **nonlinear activation**:
```
output = activation( Σ wᵢxᵢ + b )
```
Stack neurons into **layers**; stack layers into a network. Without the nonlinearity, layers collapse into a single linear map — the activation (ReLU, sigmoid, tanh) is what enables complexity.

*Why it matters:* nets are **universal function approximators** — given enough capacity they can fit almost any relationship. In finance that power is double-edged (it fits noise too).

---

## Block 2 — How it learns (backprop + GD)
A net's loss is minimised by **gradient descent**; gradients are computed efficiently by **backpropagation** (the chain rule applied layer by layer, right to left). That's it — yesterday's GD plus the chain rule.

**Concept check:** what does the activation function provide that a stack of linear layers can't? (Nonlinearity → ability to represent curved/interacting relationships.)

---

## Block 3 — Universal approximation ≠ usefulness
A net *can* approximate any function — but in markets the "function" is mostly **noise** with a sliver of drifting signal. Universal approximation then means *universal overfitting* unless heavily constrained. Capacity must be matched to the (tiny) signal and (large) data, with strong regularization.

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics (capacity & regularization)
More layers/neurons → more parameters → lower bias, **higher variance** (Day 128). Defences: dropout, weight decay (L2), early stopping, small architectures. **Exercise:** a net with 1M parameters trained on 2,000 daily returns — predict the outcome. (Memorises noise; great train, useless live.)

---

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking (where NNs actually earn their keep)

**Honest map** (this is what separates hype from practice):
- ✅ **High signal, high data:** limit-order-book microstructure, market-making, **alternative data** (NLP on news/filings, satellite/image data), option-surface modelling.
- ✅ **Nonlinear feature extraction** where relationships are genuinely nonlinear and data is abundant.
- ❌ **Low-frequency return prediction** from a few features and short history — simple linear/regularized models usually win (Day 128).

**Hard puzzle:** a candidate proposes a 10-layer net to predict daily stock returns from 5 features and 3 years of data. Your critique? (Wildly over-parameterised for the SNR and sample → certain overfit; a regularized linear/factor model is the right tool. Reserve deep nets for high-SNR, high-data regimes.)

**Career connection:** firms want to see you know **when *not* to use deep learning.** "Use the simplest model that captures the signal; reserve NNs for high-data, genuinely-nonlinear problems like microstructure/alt-data" is the mature answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Tiny net, big overfit warning
```python
import numpy as np
from sklearn.neural_network import MLPRegressor
from sklearn.model_selection import TimeSeriesSplit
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
X = rng.normal(size=(750, 5)); y = X[:,0]*0.2 + rng.normal(0,1,750)  # 1 weak signal + noise
for hidden in [(2,), (200,200)]:
    scores=[]
    for tr,te in TimeSeriesSplit(5).split(X):
        m=MLPRegressor(hidden, max_iter=800, alpha=1e-3).fit(X[tr],y[tr])
        scores.append(m.score(X[te],y[te]))
    print(hidden, "OOS R²:", round(np.mean(scores),3))   # big net usually worse OOS
```

**Linux:** `nvidia-smi` (if GPU) / `htop` — neural training is resource-heavy; know your hardware.
