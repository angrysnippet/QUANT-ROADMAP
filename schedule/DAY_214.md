# Day 214 — 🧮⚖️ Portfolio World · Risk Parity — Allocating by Risk, Not Capital

> A "balanced" 60/40 stock/bond portfolio *sounds* diversified — but ~90% of its risk comes from stocks. **Risk parity** flips the question: instead of allocating *capital* equally, allocate *risk* equally. It's the philosophy behind some of the world's largest funds (Bridgewater's All Weather) and a profound reframing of what diversification means.

**Focus:** Risk Contribution · Risk Parity · Risk vs Capital Allocation · Leverage & Risk Parity · The Diversification Reframe

## Block 1 — The hidden concentration in "balanced" portfolios
The classic 60/40 (60% stocks, 40% bonds) *looks* diversified by **capital**. But stocks are ~3× as volatile as bonds, so by **risk**, the portfolio is hugely concentrated:
- ~**90% of the 60/40's risk comes from the 60% stock allocation** — it's almost a pure equity bet,
- when stocks crash, the portfolio crashes, because the bonds (low risk) can't offset the stocks (high risk).

**Capital allocation ≠ risk allocation.** Equal *dollars* does not mean equal *risk* — and risk is what actually matters for your P&L and drawdowns. This realization is the seed of risk parity: *measure and allocate the thing that matters (risk), not the thing that's easy to measure (capital).*

*Why it matters:* most "diversified" portfolios are secretly concentrated in their riskiest asset — a fragility hidden by looking at capital weights instead of risk contributions.

---

## Block 2 — Risk contribution: who's actually driving the risk
The key concept is each asset's **marginal contribution to portfolio risk** — how much of the *total portfolio volatility* comes from each position. For asset `i` with weight `w_i`:
```
Risk Contribution_i = w_i × (∂σ_p / ∂w_i)     and    Σ_i RC_i = σ_p (they sum to total risk)
```
The marginal term `∂σ_p/∂w_i = (Σw)_i / σ_p` depends on the asset's volatility *and its correlations* with the rest of the portfolio. So risk contribution captures not just "how volatile is this asset" but "how much does it add to the *portfolio's* risk given everything else" — a *marginal, conditional* measure (the same marginal-contribution logic as Day 177's signal value and Day 146's diversification). In the 60/40, the stock's risk contribution is ~90% even though its capital weight is 60% — the model makes the hidden concentration *visible and quantified*.

**Concept check:** two assets, A (vol 30%) and B (vol 10%), uncorrelated, equal capital weights. Which contributes more risk? (**A, by far** — risk scales with volatility, so the 30%-vol asset dominates the portfolio's risk despite equal dollars. Roughly, A contributes ~90% of the variance (`0.3² vs 0.1²` = 9:1). Equal capital → wildly unequal risk. To *equalize risk*, you'd hold *much less* of A and *much more* of B.)

---

## Block 3 — The risk parity allocation
**Risk parity** chooses weights so that **every asset contributes *equally* to total portfolio risk**:
```
RC_1 = RC_2 = … = RC_N = σ_p / N
```
Practically, this means:
- **underweight (in capital) the volatile assets** (stocks) and **overweight the calm assets** (bonds) — the opposite of 60/40's risk concentration,
- the result is genuinely *risk-balanced* — no single asset dominates the portfolio's fate.

But there's a catch: a risk-balanced portfolio of low-vol and high-vol assets has **low overall expected return** (you're heavy in calm, low-returning assets like bonds). So risk-parity funds **apply leverage** to scale the *whole risk-balanced portfolio* up to a target return/volatility — keeping the *balance* while achieving competitive returns. This leverage is the controversial part (Block 4): it makes risk parity sensitive to funding costs and to scenarios where *all* assets fall together (correlations →1, Day 146).

---

## Block 4 — Mathematics · the case for (and against) risk parity
**The case FOR** (the theory):
- **diversification is maximized by equalizing risk contributions** — intuitively, no single bet dominates, so the portfolio is robust to *any one* asset/factor performing badly,
- it **doesn't require expected-return forecasts** — risk parity uses only the covariance matrix (Day 211), *not* `μ`, sidestepping the impossible-to-estimate expected returns (Day 149) that wreck mean-variance optimization (Day 147). This is a huge robustness advantage — you're betting only on *risk*, which is far more estimable than *return* (Day 202),
- with leverage to a target vol, it can deliver **better risk-adjusted returns** than 60/40 historically (more balanced, less equity-dominated).

**The case AGAINST / the critiques:**
- **leverage risk** (Day 178/213) — to get competitive returns, risk parity *levers up* the (low-return) balanced portfolio, introducing leverage sensitivity, funding-cost risk, and forced-deleveraging vulnerability,
- **correlation breakdown** (Day 146) — risk parity assumes the diversification *holds*; in a crisis when correlations →1 (everything falls together), the leveraged portfolio can suffer badly (and *all* the levered assets drop at once),
- **bond-heavy / rate-sensitive** — equalizing risk means large bond positions; in a rising-rate regime (2022), levered bonds hurt, and risk parity had a rough year,
- **it ignores expected returns entirely** — which is robust (no bad forecasts) but also means it can't tilt toward genuinely higher-return assets; it's agnostic by design.

The mathematical elegance: risk parity is the portfolio where the **marginal risk contributions are equalized**, solvable by optimization (no closed form in general, but a convex problem). It sits in a spectrum of **risk-based portfolios** that *avoid `μ`*: minimum-variance (Day 147), maximum-diversification, equal-risk-contribution (risk parity), and 1/N (equal capital) — all robust precisely *because* they don't depend on expected-return forecasts (Day 147's lesson that 1/N often beats MVO out-of-sample).

## Block 5 — Quant Thinking · allocate the thing that matters, and forecast only what you can
Risk parity embodies two of the deepest principles of robust portfolio construction. **First: allocate the thing that actually matters (risk), not the thing that's easy to measure (capital).** The 60/40's hidden equity concentration is a special case of a universal error — judging diversification by *capital weights* (easy, visible) instead of *risk contributions* (what determines your fate). The discipline of measuring and allocating the *consequential* quantity, even when it's harder to compute, recurs throughout the curriculum: risk-adjusted (not raw) returns (Day 145), marginal (not standalone) signal value (Day 177), effective (not nominal) breadth (Day 176), risk decomposition by source (Day 211). **Look past the convenient surface metric to the one that drives outcomes.**

**Second, and deeper: forecast only what you can, and build robustness from that.** Risk parity's most profound feature is that it **uses only the covariance matrix, not expected returns** — it deliberately *refuses* to forecast `μ` because expected returns are nearly impossible to estimate (Day 149) and forecasting them badly destroys portfolios (the MVO error-maximizer, Day 147). Instead, it builds the entire allocation on **risk**, which — as the whole time-series and portfolio arc has shown (Day 202: "variance is signal, mean is noise"; Day 146: covariance structure; Day 211: factor risk models) — is *far more estimable and stable* than return. This is a *strategic* choice about *where to place your epistemic bets*: don't build your portfolio on the quicksand of return forecasts; build it on the firmer ground of risk estimates. It connects to the recurring meta-lesson — **respect what you can't forecast (returns), exploit what you can (risk)** — and to the robustness philosophy of Black–Litterman (Day 212: anchor on a prior, deviate only with confidence) and 1/N (Day 147: when estimation is hard, default to a robust rule). The mature insight: **a portfolio's robustness comes from minimizing its dependence on hard-to-estimate inputs.** Mean-variance depends critically on `μ` (fragile); risk parity depends only on `Σ` (robust). Neither is universally "right" — risk parity's leverage and correlation-breakdown vulnerabilities are real (Block 4) — but the *principle* is invaluable: **understand which inputs your strategy depends on, how reliable those inputs are, and prefer approaches that lean on the reliable ones.** Risk parity is the canonical example of trading away the (impossible) return forecast for robustness, and of allocating by the consequential metric (risk) rather than the convenient one (capital). The quant who internalizes both — *measure what matters, forecast only what you can* — builds portfolios that survive contact with reality, which is the entire game.

**Hard puzzle:** an investor proudly shows you a "diversified" portfolio: 50% in an S&P 500 fund, 30% in a tech-heavy growth fund, 20% in corporate bonds, "spread across stocks and bonds." Critique it using risk parity thinking, and explain what genuine risk-balancing would look like. (**The critique — it's a *capital*-diversified but *risk*-concentrated portfolio, and worse, the "diversification" is largely illusory:** (1) **By risk, not capital, it's almost entirely an equity bet.** The 80% in equity funds (50% S&P + 30% tech growth) is far more volatile than the 20% corporate bonds, so by **risk contribution** (Block 2), the equities likely drive **~95%+** of the portfolio's risk — the bonds are nearly irrelevant to the portfolio's fate. The "20% bonds" provides a *false sense* of balance; in an equity crash, the portfolio crashes. (2) **The two equity funds are highly correlated** — the S&P 500 and a tech-growth fund overlap heavily (tech is a huge chunk of the S&P) and are both equity-beta, so they're *not independent bets* (Day 146); the "diversification" between them is mostly illusory — it's really ~80% concentrated equity, even more concentrated in tech. (3) **Corporate bonds aren't even a clean diversifier** — corporate (vs government) bonds carry *credit risk* that's positively correlated with equities (they sell off *with* stocks in a crisis, Day 167), so the 20% "safe" allocation is partly *another* equity-correlated bet — it won't protect in the crash when you need it. **What genuine risk-balancing (risk parity) would look like:** (1) **Measure risk contributions**, not capital weights — reveal that the portfolio is ~95% equity risk. (2) **Allocate by equal risk contribution** across *genuinely diversifying* risk sources — not two correlated equity funds, but assets with *different drivers*: equities, *government* bonds (true safe-haven, negatively correlated in crises), commodities/inflation hedges, and possibly international/other factors. (3) **Underweight (in capital) the high-vol equities and overweight the low-vol diversifiers** so each contributes *equal risk* — which would mean *far less* than 80% in equities by capital, and *much more* in bonds/diversifiers. (4) **Apply modest leverage** to the risk-balanced portfolio to reach the target return/volatility (since it's now heavier in low-return safe assets) — *while respecting* leverage and correlation-breakdown risks (Block 4). (5) **Ensure the risk sources are genuinely uncorrelated** — the whole point is that no single shock (an equity crash, a rate spike, an inflation surprise) dominates; that requires *different economic drivers*, not different funds with the same driver. **The deep lesson:** "diversification" judged by *capital across labels* ("stocks and bonds") is often a mirage; *true* diversification is *equal risk contribution across genuinely independent risk sources*, which usually looks *nothing* like a capital-balanced portfolio — and revealing that gap is exactly what risk parity / risk-contribution analysis is for. The investor's portfolio is a concentrated, tech-tilted equity bet wearing a diversified costume; risk parity strips off the costume and rebuilds it around *risk*, not *labels*.)

**Career connection:** "what's wrong with a 60/40 portfolio, and what's risk parity?" Strong answer: a 60/40 is **capital-balanced but risk-concentrated** — stocks are ~3× as volatile as bonds, so **~90% of the portfolio's risk comes from the 60% equity sleeve**; it's almost a pure equity bet despite *looking* diversified. **Risk parity** fixes this by allocating so that **every asset contributes equal risk** (equalizing *marginal risk contributions*, not capital) — underweighting volatile assets, overweighting calm ones — then applying **leverage** to reach a target return. Its key robustness advantage is that it **uses only the covariance matrix, not expected-return forecasts** (which are nearly impossible to estimate and wreck mean-variance optimization), betting only on *risk*, which is far more estimable. The trade-offs: **leverage sensitivity, funding-cost risk, and vulnerability to correlation breakdown** (everything falling together in a crisis) and rising rates (bond-heavy). Framing it as *allocate by risk not capital* + *forecast only what you can (risk, not return)* — with the leverage/correlation caveats — is the institutional risk-allocation answer.

## Block 6 — Python (quant) · Risk contributions & a risk-parity portfolio
```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import minimize

# 3 assets: stocks (high vol), bonds (low vol), commodities (mid), modest correlations
vols = np.array([0.18, 0.05, 0.15])
corr = np.array([[1.0, -0.2, 0.3],[-0.2,1.0,0.0],[0.3,0.0,1.0]])
Sigma = np.outer(vols,vols)*corr

def risk_contributions(w, Sigma):
    sig_p = np.sqrt(w @ Sigma @ w)
    mrc = Sigma @ w / sig_p                          # marginal risk contribution
    return w * mrc                                    # total RC per asset (sums to σ_p)

# a "60/40-style" capital-weighted portfolio
w_6040 = np.array([0.6, 0.35, 0.05])
rc = risk_contributions(w_6040, Sigma)
print("capital weights :", w_6040)
print("risk contrib %  :", (rc/rc.sum()*100).round(0), " <- stocks dominate the RISK")

# solve for risk parity: minimize dispersion of risk contributions
def objective(w):
    rc = risk_contributions(w, Sigma)
    return np.sum((rc - rc.mean())**2)               # equalize risk contributions
cons = ({'type':'eq','fun':lambda w: w.sum()-1},)
res = minimize(objective, np.ones(3)/3, bounds=[(0,1)]*3, constraints=cons)
w_rp = res.x
rc_rp = risk_contributions(w_rp, Sigma)
print("\nrisk-parity weights:", w_rp.round(2), " <- much less in stocks, more in bonds")
print("risk contrib %     :", (rc_rp/rc_rp.sum()*100).round(0), " <- now EQUAL")
```
The capital-weighted portfolio is risk-dominated by stocks (~80%+ of risk); the risk-parity solution holds *far less* stock and *more* bonds to **equalize risk contributions** — diversification by risk, not capital, made concrete.
